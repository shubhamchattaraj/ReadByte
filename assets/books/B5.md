The air in the reception alpha of Chrono-Securities, LLC smelled faintly
of ozone and expensive, unburned tobacco. It was a sterile,
high-ceilinged room designed to make a man feel small long before he
ever looked at a contract.

Julian Vance sat in a chair molded from a single piece of dark, matte
polymer that offered no give. He kept his hands wedged between his
thighs to stop them from shaking, watching the digital clock on the
wall. The numbers didn\'t tick; they shifted seamlessly, a fluid bleed
of seconds that felt less like time passing and more like water draining
from a tub.

Across from him sat a woman with a child---a boy no older than six,
asleep with his head heavy against her coat. The woman's skin had that
specific, translucent gray quality that belonged to people who had
already visited the basement levels. Her collarbones stood out like
small, sharp shelves under her collar. Julian looked away, his chest
tightening. He knew the math she was doing in her head. A year of
childhood was worth double on the open market, but a mother's prime
working years fetched the highest premium if she was willing to
surrender the fine motor skills that went with them.

\"Julian Vance,\" a voice chimed. It wasn't a human voice, nor was it
the flat drone of an old-world AI. It was synthesized from the recorded
vowels of a long-dead stage actress---warm, wealthy, and entirely devoid
of pity. \"Station four.\"

Julian stood up, his knees clicking in the quiet room. He adjusted the
cuffs of his jacket---his only good jacket, an old tweed piece from his
university days when time was something he wasted on poetry and cold
coffee. Now, at thirty-four, he was a man whose debts had become a
physical weight, a ledger of numbers that woke him at three in the
morning with a cold sweat on the back of his neck.

Station four was a small cubicle partition separated from the lobby by a
pane of smart-glass that went opaque the moment he stepped inside.
Sitting behind a desk of polished synthetic marble was a man whose name
tag read simply: *C. Miller, Senior Valuation Analyst.*

Miller didn't look up immediately. He was studying a three-dimensional
biometric rendering of Julian's left lung, which floated above a small
projector disk on the desk. The lung expanded and contracted in a
rhythmic, ghostly cycle.

\"Sit, Mr. Vance,\" Miller said, flicking his fingers through the
projection. The lung vanished, replaced by a dense cascade of financial
data, flashing red at the bottom in a sum that Julian knew by heart:
**\$412,000.**

\"The interest on the cartel loan matures at midnight,\" Julian said,
his voice scraping against his throat. He hadn\'t drank water since
yesterday morning; he'd been afraid it would alter his hydration metrics
during the preliminary scans.

\"I am aware,\" Miller said softly. He was a clean-shaven man of
indeterminate age---the kind of smooth, unlined face that money bought
when you lived in the upper districts. He could have been forty or
seventy. \"The syndicate you borrowed from is remarkably efficient with
their filings. They've already placed a lean on your physical equity. If
you don\'t clear the principal by twelve, they will simply take the
meat. And frankly, their harvesting methods are\... crude. They don\'t
use local anesthetics for the major vessels.\"

Julian swallowed. \"I'm here to clear it. All of it.\"

\"Twenty years,\" Miller said, finally looking up. His eyes were a very
pale, striking blue, completely steady. \"That is a significant
transaction for an unaugmented biological unit. Your baseline life
expectancy, based on your current telomere degradation and familial
history, is seventy-eight. Removing twenty years leaves you with
fifty-eight. You are thirty-four now. That gives you twenty-four years
of remaining liquidity.\"

\"I can live on twenty-four years,\" Julian said. It sounded like a lie
even to him. It sounded like a man agreeing to live in a room with three
walls.

\"You can,\" Miller agreed, pulling up a long sheet of digital
parchment---the standard-form *Chrono-Exchange Indenture.* \"But you
must understand the nature of the extraction. Many clients believe we
simply sleep them through a long winter or accelerate their cellular
aging overnight. We do not. The flesh remains thirty-four. The bones
remain thirty-four. What we extract is the *duration*---the cognitive
and neurological capacity to experience those twenty years. We take the
experiential substrate.\"

\"The memories,\" Julian whispered.

\"The potential for them, yes. And the space they would occupy,\" Miller
said, rotating a small stylus between his fingers. \"The process
requires a deep-tissue neural sweep. We harvest the redundant memory
pathways---the things you aren\'t using---to build the anchor for the
extraction. It's like clearing a site for a building. We remove the old
topsoil to lay the foundation for the buyer\'s life-extension suite.\"

\"Will I forget who I am?\"

\"No,\" Miller said, his tone dripping with the practiced reassurance of
a doctor who had delivered this speech a thousand times. \"You will
retain your core identity. Your name, your language, your basic
professional skills. But the specific, non-essential color of your
past---the tertiary memories, the emotional fringes---will be scrubbed
to create the necessary neural clearance. Think of it as a hard drive
partition. You won\'t miss what you don\'t remember having.\"

The analyst slid the stylus across the marble desk. The tip of it glowed
with a faint, violet light---the digital signature of a life-binding
contract.

Julian looked at the pen. If he walked out, the men from the syndicate
would be waiting by his apartment building before the sun went down.
They wouldn\'t take his years cleanly; they'd take an eye, a kidney, a
hand, leaving him a broken thing that still owed them money. Here, at
least, he would walk out clean. He would be poorer in time, but the
numbers in his bank account would read zero instead of a negative red.

He reached out, his fingers closing around the cool metal of the stylus.

\"Where do the memories go?\" Julian asked, his hand hovering over the
signature line. \"The ones you scrub to make room?\"

Miller smiled, a small, professional parting of the lips that didn\'t
reach his pale eyes. \"They are processed as raw data, Mr. Vance.
High-fidelity human experience is a very scarce commodity. The elite use
it to enrich their own synthetic extensions. It's entirely anonymous, I
assure you. Your childhood summers or your first heartbreak might end up
as the background texture for a director's board meeting in the clouds.
It's quite poetic, if you think about it.\"

\"Poetic,\" Julian repeated. He didn\'t think it was poetic. He thought
it was a butcher shop where the meat was made of light and time.

He brought the stylus down onto the glass. The violet light flared,
biting into his thumb with a sharp, cold sting as it verified his DNA
against the registry. He signed his name---*Julian David Vance*---in a
long, jagged script that looked like a man falling off a cliff.

The moment the final stroke cleared, the lights in the cubicle didn\'t
dim, but a strange, hollow sensation bloomed behind Julian's eyes. It
wasn\'t pain. It was the feeling of an apartment being emptied in the
apartment above yours---the distant, muffled thud of furniture being
dragged across floorboards, leaving behind empty spaces where things
used to be.

\"Excellent,\" Miller said, the digital parchment rolling back into the
desk with a satisfying click. \"Please proceed through the rear door to
Extraction Theater Seven. The technicians are already calibrated for
your sequence.\"

The extraction theater was cold---far colder than the reception room.
Julian lay on a padded table that tilted backward until his feet were
slightly higher than his head. A web of fine, silver filaments was
lowered over his skull, each one ending in a tiny, cold needle that
pressed against his temples, his hairline, and the base of his neck.

\"Count backward from one hundred,\" a technician said from behind a
glass screen. Her voice was muffled, coming through an intercom that
rattled with static.

\"One hundred,\" Julian said.

He thought of his mother\'s house in the country, the way the wild
blackberries smelled after rain when he was nine years old.

\"Ninety-nine.\"

The silver filaments began to hum. It was a sound he felt in his teeth
rather than heard with his ears---a deep, resonant vibration that made
his eyes twitch under his lids.

\"Ninety-eight.\"

The smell of the blackberries began to sour, turning into something
chemical, like turpentine. The image of the kitchen table---the specific
pattern of the yellow oilcloth where he used to do his homework---began
to blur at the edges, the yellow running like wet paint.

\"Ninety-seven.\"

He tried to hold onto the face of a girl he had loved in
college---Claire. She had a small, dark mole just beneath the outer
corner of her left eye. He reached for it in his mind, but his fingers
found only smooth, blank skin. The mole was gone. Then her name was
gone, replaced by a generic label: *Female. Companion. Age twenty-two.*

\"Ninety-six.\"

A violent spike of vertigo hit him, as if the table had been dropped
down an elevator shaft. A bright, blinding flash of violet light filled
his vision, though his eyes were clamped shut. Inside that light, he saw
strings of alphanumeric code passing through his own memories, tagging
them like inventory items in a warehouse.

*File_0988: First bicycle (Red). Status: Extracted.* *File_1142: Fear of
drowning (Aged seven). Status: Extracted.* *File_4401: Smoldering embers
of first professional failure. Status: Extracted.*

He wanted to scream, to tell them to stop, that he wanted the fear of
drowning back, that the red bicycle was important, but his tongue was a
heavy piece of dry leather in his mouth. The machine hummed louder, a
roaring torrent of wind that seemed to blow right through the center of
his brain, carrying away the dust of twenty years.

And then, total, dead silence.

Julian woke up on a bench outside the Chrono-Securities building. The
sun was setting, painting the jagged tops of the city\'s towers in a
harsh, industrial orange.

He sat up quickly, his heart hammering against his ribs. He felt\...
light. That was the first thing he noticed. The crushing weight that had
lived in his stomach for three years---the constant, grinding panic of
the debt---was gone. He pulled out his personal handheld device and
checked his account.

**Balance: \$0.00.**

The syndicate had been paid automatically upon the extraction\'s
completion. He was free. He didn\'t owe a single dollar to any living
soul.

But when he looked down at his hands, they looked older than he
remembered. The skin over his knuckles was slightly drier, the veins a
bit more prominent. He knew, intellectually, that he was still
thirty-four. The machine hadn\'t aged his body; it had merely taken the
*time* he had left. He had thirty-four years of physical wear, but the
internal clock---the one that dictated how many breaths he had left
before the system failed---had been wound down by two decades.

He stood up, his balance slightly off, like a man who had lost his inner
ear dynamic. He began the long walk back to his small apartment in the
lower district.

As he walked, he tried to remember his twentieth birthday. He knew he
had one. He knew there must have been cake, or a drink, or friends. But
when he searched that corner of his mind, he found only a clean, white
room with nothing in it. He knew the word \"birthday,\" and he knew he
had been twenty once, but the actual experience of it was an empty
shelf.

He reached his apartment---a single room with a fold-out bed and a
window that faced a brick wall. He didn\'t feel sad. That was the
strangest part. The extraction had taken the emotional apparatus
required to grieve for what he had lost. He felt only a dull, flat
neutrality.

He lay down on the bed without taking off his shoes. He closed his eyes
and slept a deep, dreamless sleep---the sleep of an engine that had been
turned off and cleared of fuel.

The next morning, the chime of his terminal woke him at exactly six
o\'clock.

Julian rubbed his face, his skin feeling tight. He walked over to the
desk and tapped the screen. A new notification was blinking in the upper
right corner---an encrypted priority message from the domain
*chrono-securities.internal.*

He opened it, expecting the standard automated receipt for his
transaction. Instead, the document that loaded onto his screen was a
complex corporate memo, marked with a red banner across the top:
**CONFIDENTIAL // INTERNAL ONLY // DISPOSAL & ALLOCATION DIVISION.**

Julian frowned, his eyes tracing down past his own name and account
number. Below his details, in a section marked *CC:* there was a long
string of corporate aliases, and then, at the very bottom, his own
public encryption key, clearly included by a clerical error in the
automated routing system.

The message read:

**From:** Distribution_Node_4@chrono-sec.internal **To:** Senior VP of
Acquisition, Sector 9 **Subject:** Allocation of Asset: Vance, Julian
(Ref: #889-Alpha)

The deep-tissue neural sweep for Asset #889-Alpha (Vance) was completed
at 16:30 hours with a higher-than-average yield of high-fidelity
mnemonic residue. Due to the asset\'s background in classical literature
and low-level historical research prior to his insolvency, the extracted
cognitive material is remarkably dense and well-structured.

Pursuant to the *Premium Extension Program* contract with **Carlyle
Group / Subscriber #001 (Vane, Sterling)**, the harvested twenty-year
block has been cleared for immediate transfer.

**Special Handling Instructions:** The buyer has requested specific
target integration. Do not scrub the artistic associative pathways in
the Vance file. Subscriber Vane intends to use the Vance memories of
creative expression and early romantic attachments to combat his own
advanced cognitive decay (Stage 4 Senescence).

The Vance substrate will be used to \'pad\' Subscriber Vane's failing
memory banks during the upcoming renewal procedure on June 8th. The
asset\'s memories of his mother\'s death and his subsequent isolation
are to be repurposed as a synthetic grief-buffer to allow Vane to
process the loss of his own holdings without emotional collapse.

Total credit value credited to Sector 9: \$1,200,000.

*Note: Ensure the asset receives the standard automated \"Thank You\"
template. No further contact required. The asset\'s remaining life
expectancy suggests total system failure within 24 years due to
accelerated neural thinning---monitor for potential secondary harvest if
he falls into debt again.*

Julian stood frozen in front of the screen, the cool light of the
terminal bathing his face in blue.

His heart, which had felt so quiet since the extraction, began to strike
against his ribs like a trapped bird. He read the name again. *Vane,
Sterling.* One of the city\'s founding magnates---a man who lived in a
glass needle three hundred stories above the smog, a man who had lived
for nearly two hundred years by consuming the pieces of men like Julian.

They hadn\'t just taken his years. They were going to give his private,
unremembered self to an old monster to use as medicine.

The terminal screen didn\'t fade, but Julian's ability to read it did.
The letters began to swim, blurring into long, black ribbons against the
white background. He sat down on the edge of his bed, the synthetic
mattress groaning under his weight.

*Subscriber #001 (Vane, Sterling).*

Julian knew the name. Everyone in the lower districts knew it, though
they spoke it the way ancient sailors spoke of deep-sea monsters---with
a mixture of superstitious distance and absolute certainty of its teeth.
Sterling Vane was the majority shareholder of Chrono-Securities. He was
the architect of the *Chrono-Exchange Indenture* itself, the man who had
looked at a city drowning in debt and realized that the poor had one
last asset that could be strip-mined for profit: their future.

Julian looked down at his trembling hands. The contract was signed. The
transaction was legal. By the laws of the Accord, those twenty years no
longer belonged to Julian Vance; they were the property of the
corporation, a line item on a ledger to be bought, sold, or injected
into the failing gray matter of a billionaire.

But the text in the memo was specific: *The Vance substrate will be used
to \'pad\' Subscriber Vane's failing memory banks during the upcoming
renewal procedure on June 8th.*

June 8th. Julian looked at the corner of his terminal screen.

**June 6th, 2026.**

The procedure hadn\'t happened yet. His twenty years---the memories of
his twenties, his first poems, his mother's long, slow decline in that
small house with the leaky roof---were currently sitting in a cold,
digital reservoir somewhere in the bowels of Sector 9, waiting to be
uploaded into an old man's brain in less than forty-eight hours.

A strange, hot sensation began to stir in Julian\'s chest. It was a
phantom limb of an emotion. The extraction had taken his ability to
remember *why* those memories mattered, but it hadn\'t taken his
baseline human revulsion at being stolen from. They had bought his time
for four hundred thousand dollars, but they were selling it to Vane for
over a million. And worse, they were going to use his private
grief---the terrible, quiet weeks after his mother died---as an
emotional shock absorber for a man who had never felt a day of hunger in
his long, synthetic life.

He stood up, driven by a sudden, jagged energy. He needed to see what
was left of himself.

Julian left his apartment and descended into the streets of the lower
district. The city was a vertical labyrinth, where the light of the sun
was a commodity reserved for those who lived above the hundredth floor.
Down here, at the forty-second level, the world was illuminated by the
flickering glare of neon advertisements for synthetic proteins and
high-interest credit lines.

He walked without a destination, his mind spinning. He tried to force
himself to think about his college days. He knew he had gone to the
University of the Delta. He knew he had studied literature. But when he
tried to recall the face of his favorite professor, he saw only a
silhouette. When he tried to remember the title of his thesis---a
project he had spent eighteen hours a day writing---he found only an
empty folder in his mind labeled *Academic_01*.

He was a ghost in his own skin. He had the vocabulary of an educated
man, but no history to justify it. He was a library whose books had been
hollowed out to hide contraband.

\"Julian?\"

The voice came from behind him, cutting through the heavy rumble of the
overhead transit rail.

Julian turned. Standing under the sputtering green light of a noodle
kiosk was a woman. She was wearing a grease-stained canvas coat, her
dark hair pulled back with a piece of copper wire. Her eyes were wide,
fixed on his face with a mixture of disbelief and sudden, sharp pain.

Julian blinked. His mind whirred, searching its newly partitioned
directories. *Female. Approximate age: thirty-two. Familiarity index:
high.* But there was no name attached to the file. The database had been
scrubbed.

\"It\'s you,\" she said, stepping forward. Her hand reached out toward
his face, then dropped back to her side as she noticed the small, faint
violet discoloration on his temple---the mark left by the extraction
needles.

The woman stopped dead. Her face drained of what little color it had.
\"You did it,\" she whispered. \"You went to Chrono.\"

\"I\... had to,\" Julian said. His voice sounded flat, even to him. He
didn\'t know who she was, but the sight of her made a strange, dull ache
bloom behind his breastbone. \"The syndicate. They were going to---\"

\"I told you I could get the money, Julian,\" she said, her voice
cracking. \"I was working the double shifts at the filtration plant. I
just needed three more weeks. We could have paid the principal.\"

\"Three weeks was too late,\" Julian said. He searched her face,
desperate for a clue, a spark, anything to tell him who she was to him.
\"Who are you?\"

The woman stared at him, her lips parting slightly. A tear tracked
through the grime on her cheek, catching the green neon light. \"Oh,
God. They took me. They took us.\"

\"I have a core identity retention,\" Julian said, reciting the
corporate brochure Miller had given him. \"I know my name. I know my
baseline data.\"

\"But you don\'t know who I am,\" she said. It wasn\'t a question. It
was a statement of absolute ruin. \"I\'m Leah, Julian. We lived together
for four years. We\... we were going to go south next winter.\"

*Leah.* The name echoed in his head, but it didn\'t attach to anything.
It was like typing a search query into a broken terminal. *Error: File
not found.* Yet, looking at her, he felt a profound sense of theft. This
woman loved a version of him that no longer existed. The man who had
promised to take her south had been sold yesterday morning for four
hundred thousand dollars.

\"I\'m sorry,\" Julian said, and for the first time since the procedure,
the neutrality of his tone slipped, revealing a raw, jagged edge of
panic. \"I don\'t\... I don\'t have the files.\"

Leah looked at his temple again, at the violet bruise that would take a
week to fade. She stepped back, shaking her head. \"They didn\'t just
pay your debt, Julian. They killed you. They just left the machine
running.\"

She turned and vanished into the crowd before he could stop her. Julian
reached out a hand, but his fingers closed on the greasy, damp air of
the lower district. He didn\'t follow her. He couldn\'t. To follow her
would be to pretend he could be the man she wanted, and that man was
currently sitting in a storage drive in Sector 9.

He stood in the middle of the crowded walkway, people brushing past him
without a glance. The realization hit him with the force of a physical
blow: he wasn\'t free. He hadn\'t escaped the debt. He had simply
transferred the ownership of his soul from the syndicate to Sterling
Vane.

And then, the anger came.

It wasn\'t the hot, explosive anger of his youth---a memory of which he
could no longer access---but a cold, calculated fury that felt like ice
water in his veins. They had left him with twenty-four years of life
expectancy. They had left him with an empty apartment and a missing
history. But they had made a mistake. They had accidentally sent him the
routing code.

Julian turned around and began to walk back to his apartment, his stride
turning long and purposeful.

He wasn\'t a hacker. He wasn\'t a corporate saboteur. But before he was
a debtor, he had been a researcher. He knew how databases were
structured. He knew that every corporate transfer required an audit
trail, and he knew that if a file was marked for transfer on June 8th,
it had to be staged in a local cache before the final upload.

He closed the door of his room behind him and sat down at the terminal.
He didn\'t look at his bank balance. He opened the internal memo again,
his eyes focusing on the distribution node address:
*Distribution_Node_4@chrono-sec.internal.*

Attached to that node was an encryption key---his own public key, which
had been used to sign the receipt. Because of the clerical error, his
terminal was still authenticated as a trusted endpoint for that specific
data stream. The system thought he was an internal auditor verifying the
asset before disposal.

Julian tapped his fingers against the desk, a habit he didn\'t remember
forming.

\"You want my memories, Mr. Vane?\" Julian whispered to the empty room.
\"Let\'s see what else is in the vault.\"

He began to type. He didn\'t use the standard user interface; he
bypassed it, dropping down into the raw command-line terminal he had
used during his university years. The commands came back to him
naturally---fine motor skills and technical training were part of the
\"core identity\" the extraction left intact.

He entered the routing code from the memo. The screen blinked, a
progress bar appearing in the center of the display:

**CONNECTING TO CHRONO-SEC.INTERNAL \[NODE 4\]\...** **AUTHENTICATING
VIA ACCESS KEY \[VANCE_J_09918\]\...** **ACCESS GRANTED. STATUS: LEVEL 2
AUDIT PENDING.**

Julian's breath hitched. The door was open. He was looking directly into
the staging server for Sector 9's upcoming extractions.

There were hundreds of files listed, each one representing a human being
who had signed away a piece of their life in the last forty-eight hours.
They were listed by asset numbers, their futures quantified in gigabytes
and credit values.

He searched for his own identifier: *Asset #889-Alpha.*

He found it. The file size was massive---nearly twelve terabytes of
uncompressed neural data. This was him. His twenties. His loves, his
failures, the smell of his mother's kitchen, the sound of Leah's voice
when she laughed---things he could no longer feel were all locked inside
that digital vault, waiting to be sent up the elevator to the
three-hundredth floor.

Julian reached for his external storage drive---an old, heavy
military-grade solid-state unit he had kept from his father's things. He
plugged it into the terminal's physical port.

He didn\'t just want to delete the file. If he deleted it, they would
simply arrest him, drag him back to the theater, and perform a
secondary, forced harvest to replace the lost data. He knew how
corporate liability worked. If the asset was destroyed by the seller\'s
malice, the seller's remaining life was forfeit to cover the loss.

He didn\'t want to destroy it. He wanted it back.

He initiated a direct local download of *Asset #889-Alpha*.

The progress bar began to move, agonizingly slow. **0.01%\...
0.02%\...**

The terminal's cooling fans began to whine, high and shrill, as the
massive data stream began to pull through the lower-district network
lines. Julian sat in the dark, watching the numbers tick upward, knowing
that every gigabyte he pulled down increased the digital footprint he
was leaving behind.

At this rate, it would take six hours to download his twenty years. And
he had no guarantee that the Chrono-Securities internal security team
wouldn\'t notice the anomalous traffic before the sun came up.

Suddenly, a red warning box flared on his screen, cutting off the
progress bar.

**WARNING: INTRUSION DETECTION SIGNAL DETECTED FROM PRIMARY DOMAIN.**
**REMOTE TERMINATION SEQUENCE INITIATED BY ADMINISTRATOR.**

Julian's heart stopped. They had found him.

But the text that followed wasn\'t a standard security lockdown. A new
window popped up on his screen, overriding his command line. It wasn\'t
from the security team. It was a live text feed from an unknown internal
source.

**Unknown:** *Stop the download, you idiot. You're lighting up the
entire sector network like a flare. They'll be at your door in ten
minutes.*

Julian stared at the screen, his fingers hovering over the abort key. He
typed back:

**Julian:** *Who is this?*

**Unknown:** *Someone who doesn\'t want Sterling Vane to live another
fifty years. If you want your life back, Julian, you need to stop trying
to steal it from the bottom. You have to go up.*

Julian's fingers hovered over the keyboard, the plastic keys slick with
cold sweat. The warning box continued to pulse a venomous red, casting
rhythmic shadows across the bare walls of his room.

**Julian:** *Go up? That\'s suicide. I can\'t even get past the security
gates at Level 100 without a corporate visa.*

**Unknown:** *You already have a visa, Julian. You just don\'t know it.
Look at the routing header on the memo they misdirected to you. It
contains a high-level cryptographic token meant for Vane's medical
detail. As long as that token is active on your personal terminal, the
city infrastructure thinks you are a piece of premium medical cargo.*

Julian's eyes darted back to the top of the original email. Among the
strings of alphanumeric code was a line highlighted in pale blue:
AUTH_TOKEN: BENEFACTOR_001_PRE_UP.

**Unknown:** *They're staging the upload from the Apex Lounge in the
spires tomorrow night. Vane likes to watch the city while he takes his
infusions. If you stay in your hole, the security sweeps will find your
download footprint by dawn, and they will liquefy your remaining
twenty-four years as a breach penalty. Disconnect the drive. Get to the
central transit elevator at Level 60. I\'ll open the gate.*

The text window vanished, deleting its own history from Julian\'s
terminal before he could reply. The red warning box cleared, and the
screen returned to its flat, neutral gray. The download had stopped at
**1.4%**.

Julian looked at the external storage drive plugged into his console.
Inside that tiny sliver of metal was less than two percent of his
missing self---perhaps a single childhood memory, or a fraction of an
afternoon spent with Leah that he would never truly understand. He
pulled the drive from the slot and slipped it into the inner pocket of
his tweed jacket.

He didn\'t have ten minutes. He probably had five.

He didn\'t pack. He had nothing to pack. The extraction had stripped him
of sentimental attachment to the books on his shelves or the clothes in
his closet. He was a man traveling light because he was traveling empty.

The central transit elevator at Level 60 was an industrial monolith. It
was a massive steel shaft that ran through the core of the city like a
spine, connecting the toxic slums of the foundations to the filtered-air
palaces of the clouds.

To the citizens of the lower districts, the elevator was an object of
distant myth. It was guarded by automated turrets and armored corporate
enforcers wearing helmets with blank, mirrored visors. Julian stood in
the shadow of a concrete pillar, watching the security checkpoint. A
line of commercial freight haulers was being scanned by a violet laser
grid---the same light that had bitten into his thumb at the valuation
office.

His heart thudded in his ears. He pulled out his handheld terminal, the
authentication token pulsing on the small screen.

\"Step forward,\" a synthesized voice boomed from the checkpoint arch as
Julian approached.

An enforcer stepped into his path, a heavy kinetic rifle slung across
his chest. The mirrored visor reflected Julian's pale, gaunt face.
\"Level 60 is a restricted freight zone. State your business or face
transit detention.\"

Julian didn\'t speak. He couldn\'t trust his voice not to crack.
Instead, he lifted his handheld terminal and pressed it against the
scanner plate.

The scanner hummed. For three agonizing seconds, the light stayed a
dull, warning yellow. Julian felt the enforcer's hand shift toward the
grip of his rifle. Then, with a soft, melodic chime that Julian had
never heard in the lower districts, the light turned a brilliant,
welcoming green.

The automated voice changed entirely, shifting from the harsh authority
drone to a deferential, soft tone. *\"Welcome, Health Logistics Envoy.
Sector 9 Express Transit authorized. Priority clear to Level 300.\"*

The enforcer immediately stepped back, his rifle lowering. He offered a
curt, robotic nod. \"Apologies, sir. Proceed to Bay 3.\"
