The dashboard clock clicked over to 2:14 AM, casting a pale, neon-blue glow across Leo’s tired face. The air inside the sedan smelled faintly of stale gas-station coffee, damp upholstery, and the artificial vanilla air freshener hanging from the rearview mirror—a combination that had become the permanent sensory backdrop to his sleepless nights. He was ready to hit "Sign Off" on the app and head back to his cramped, quiet apartment, desiring nothing more than a few hours of dreamless sleep.

He pulled into a deserted gas station parking lot under the harsh, buzzing hum of flickering fluorescent lights, letting the engine idle. He unbuckled his seatbelt, stretched his arms over his head, and stared at the digital map on his mounted phone. The screen displayed a sprawling grid of grayed-out streets, signifying the dead hours of the city when the nightlife had burned out and the morning commuters were still hours away from waking. He raised his hand to tap the red button to log off when his phone buzzed violently in its plastic mount, the vibration rattling against the plastic dashboard.

*Ping.*

A pickup request flared onto the screen.

Normally, Leo would have swiped it away without a second thought. It was too late, he was too tired, and the pickup location made his stomach drop slightly. The map showed a single pin dropped on the desolate, winding stretch of Route 9, right alongside the boundary wall of the old municipal cemetery. It was an industrial wasteland of abandoned warehouses, overgrown gravel turnouts, and centuries-old wrought-iron fences. No one hung out there at two in the morning unless they were up to something they shouldn't be.

But then his eyes drifted to the fare calculation. The app was registering a massive surge—triple the standard rate, with a flat-rate bonus that would cover more than half of his remaining monthly rent. He looked at the countdown bar ticking away, a shrinking blue line warning him that he only had ten seconds to accept. He thought about his bank account, the past-due notice sitting on his kitchen counter, and the landlord who didn’t believe in extensions.

With a heavy sigh that felt like it emptied the last bit of air from his lungs, Leo tapped the screen. *Ride Accepted.*

The rider’s profile was unnervingly sparse. There was no uploaded profile picture, just a gray silhouette. The rating was blank, indicating a brand-new account, and the name field contained only a single, capital letter: *M*.

Leo shifted the car into drive, the transmission clunking softly, and steered the sedan back onto the empty, glistening blacktop. It had rained heavily an hour ago, and the asphalt looked like oil slick under the yellow tint of the passing streetlamps. As he left the commercial district behind, the city’s usual background hum faded into an oppressive, heavy silence. The towering concrete overpasses gave way to skeletal trees and dense patches of woods that lined the edge of the highway.

It didn't roll in gradually; it seemed to rise directly from the damp earth, thick and soupy, swallowing the beams of his headlights and reducing his visibility to just a few feet in front of his bumper. Leo slowed down, his grip tightening on the steering wheel until his knuckles turned white. The GPS voice, usually a comfortingly robotic presence, sounded strange and distorted as it echoed through the car's speakers: *“In five hundred feet, your destination will be on the right.”*

Leo leaned forward, squinting through the sweeping rhythm of his windshield wipers. There was nothing out here. To his left was a steep drop-off leading down to the black, invisible rush of the river; to his right was the high stone wall of the cemetery, its granite blocks covered in dark ivy and weeping moss.

He brought the car to a slow crawl, the tires crunching over gravel and broken glass on the shoulder of the road. The app chimed, a cheerful sound that felt entirely out of place in the gloom. *“You have arrived at your rider’s destination.”*

Leo put the car in park but kept his foot firmly pressed on the brake. He looked out the passenger-side window. The headlights cut through the swirling fog, illuminating a rusted, padlocked iron gate that had long since fallen off its hinges, leaning drunkenly against a crumbling stone pillar. Beyond the gate, the silhouettes of forgotten headstones and weeping willow trees stretched into the darkness. There was no porch light, no sidewalk, no bus stop. Just the woods, the graves, and the fog.

"Great," Leo muttered to himself, checking his mirrors. "A prank. Or a setup."

He reached for his phone, fully intending to cancel the ride as a 'no-show' and get the hell out of there. His thumb hovered over the screen. But before he could press it, a sudden, unnatural chill swept through the interior of the car. The temperature dropped so rapidly that his breath plumed into a white cloud in front of his face. The heater, which had been blowing warm air just a moment prior, suddenly began to rattle, blowing out a draft that felt like the inside of a meat locker.

Then came the sound. *Click.*

The rear passenger door handle moved. Leo froze, his heart slamming against his ribs like a trapped bird. He hadn't seen anyone approach. He had been staring directly at the side mirror, and the road behind him had been completely empty. Yet, the door swung open with a slow, heavy creak.

The interior dome light snapped on, bathing the backseat in a stark, clinical white glare. A figure slid into the car, moving with a fluid, silent grace that didn't disturb the air.

Her head was bowed deeply, a thick curtain of long, damp, raven-black hair falling forward, completely obscuring her face from view. She sat perfectly still, her hands tucked deep into her pockets.

As the door clicked shut behind her, the dome light timed out, plunging the back of the car into deep shadow. Instantly, the heavy, suffocating scent of river water, crushed pine needles, and old, rain-soaked earth filled the vehicle. It was an organic, heavy smell—the scent of a forest floor after a torrential downpour, mixed with something faintly metallic that made Leo’s throat tighten.

Leo swallowed hard, his throat dry. He forced his eyes away from the rearview mirror, trying to maintain his professional demeanor despite the primal instinct screaming in the back of his mind to run.

"Uh... hi," Leo said, his voice cracking slightly before he cleared his throat. "M?"

The passenger didn't speak. She didn't move. She simply tilted her head forward in a slow, mechanical nod.

"Right. Got it," Leo said, looking down at his phone to verify the destination. The address had updated. "Heading to 442 Elm Street? It’s about a twenty-minute drive from here."

Again, there was no verbal response. The figure in the backseat gave another slow, deliberate nod.

Leo felt a bead of cold sweat trace a line down his temple. He had picked up weird passengers before—drunk college students, eccentric late-night workers, people who were crying or arguing on their phones—but this silence was different. It felt heavy, deliberate, and entirely unnatural.

"Alright, then," Leo said, trying to inject a casual, everyday warmth into the freezing atmosphere of the car. "Just, uh, do me a favor and click that seatbelt on for safety, please. Cops love to ticket people on the highway this time of night."

For a long moment, nothing happened. Leo watched her through the mirror, wondering if he should just ask her to get out. Then, slowly, her pale hands emerged from the oversized sleeves of the coat. In the dim light filtering through the windows from the distant highway lamps, her skin looked translucent, almost blue, the dark veins beneath her wrists tracing intricate, branch-like patterns.

She reached up, her movements stiff, as if her joints were frozen. Her fingers wrapped around the black strap of the seatbelt. With agonizing slowness, she pulled the belt across her chest. Leo found himself holding his breath, watching the deliberate movement. She guided the metal tongue toward the buckle, and then, with a sharp, echoing force, she pushed it home.

*Snap.*

The sound of the seatbelt clicking into place was incredibly loud in the confined space of the sedan. It sounded like a bone snapping, sharp and definitive.

"Great. Thank you," Leo said quickly, eager to look away.

He shifted the car into drive, took his foot off the brake, and pressed down on the accelerator. The tires spun for a fraction of a second on the wet gravel before catching the asphalt, and the car moved away from the cemetery gates, leaving the rusted iron and the silent graves behind in the fog.

As they drove, Leo tried to shake off the unease. He turned on the radio, keeping the volume low, flipping through the stations until he found a jazz track—something smooth and ambient to fill the void. But the music sounded strange; the brass instruments felt distant, and the bass hummed at a frequency that vibrated uncomfortably in his teeth.

He kept his eyes focused on the road ahead, but his gaze was constantly drawn back to the rearview mirror. The girl hadn't moved an inch since she had buckled herself in. Her head remained bowed, her hands folded neatly in her lap. She didn't check a phone, she didn't look out the window at the passing dark trees, and she didn't adjust her posture. She sat as rigidly as a mannequin, perfectly centered in the middle of the back seat.

The fog outside seemed to grow thicker, pressing against the glass of the windows like a physical weight. The world outside his car had completely disappeared, reduced to nothing more than a ten-foot pocket of light cast by his high beams. Leo felt an overwhelming sense of isolation, a creeping realization that it was just him, this empty road, and the silent entity sitting directly behind him.

He decided to try making small talk again, desperate to break the spell of her silence. "Cold night out there, huh? Summer weather has been crazy lately. One day it's a heatwave, the next it feels like October."

The silence that followed his question was absolute. Not even a sigh or a shift in weight responded to his words. Leo’s eyes drifted back to the mirror.

In the pale reflection, he noticed something that made his heart skip a beat. The windows of the car were beginning to fog up from the inside due to the intense drop in temperature. His own breath was creating a light mist on the windshield, which the defroster was struggling to clear. But in the backseat, the glass right next to the girl’s head was perfectly clear.

He looked closer at her reflection. Her chest wasn't rising or falling. There was no rhythmic movement of breathing, no soft exhalations fogging the air around her face. She was completely, utterly still.

Leo’s hands began to shake on the steering wheel. He wanted to pull over. He wanted to tell her the ride was over, that his car was breaking down, anything to get her out of the vehicle. But they were on a narrow, two-lane bridge now, crossing over the black expanse of the river. There was no shoulder, no place to stop, only concrete barriers on either side.

He forced his eyes back to the road, his heart hammering a frantic rhythm against his chest. *Just ten more minutes,* he told himself, his breath coming in shallow, ragged gasps. *Just get to Elm Street, get the money, and go home.*

He didn't look in the mirror again. He kept his eyes locked onto the white lines painted on the asphalt, using them as a guide through the blinding fog. The silence in the car grew heavier, pressing down on his eardrums like the pressure of deep water. The scent of river mud and wet earth became overpowering, filling his lungs until he felt like he was suffocating.

Then, the phone on the dashboard flashed brightly, breaking his concentration.

The screen changed from the navigation map to a stark, white notification window. The app emitted a sharp, discordant three-tone chime—the specific sound reserved for systemic alerts.

Leo glanced down, his eyes scanning the text on the screen.

*Notification: Your rider has canceled the trip. Please pull over safely at your earliest convenience to dismiss the passenger. Your cancellation fee has been processed.*

Leo stared at the screen, a wave of confusion washing over him, followed immediately by a spike of adrenaline. Canceled? They were in the middle of a bridge, miles from the destination, and she had canceled the ride while sitting right behind him?

"Uh, ma'am?" Leo said, his voice trembling uncontrollably now. He couldn't help it. He looked up, his eyes darting to the rearview mirror. "The app says you just canceled the—"

The words died in his throat.

The backseat was empty.

Leo’s eyes widened in sheer terror. He blinked hard, shaking his head, looking back at the mirror, then twisting his entire body around to look directly over his right shoulder, nearly losing control of the steering wheel. The car swerved slightly, the tires shrieking against the wet pavement before he jerked the wheel back into alignment.

The seat was entirely devoid of life. There was no young woman. There was no dark trench coat. The heavy, dark mass of hair was gone.

But she hadn't opened a door. The safety locks were engaged, and no warning light had dinged to indicate a door had been opened while the vehicle was in motion. It was physically impossible for anyone to have exited the moving vehicle.

Leo’s gaze drifted downward, tracing the empty black fabric of the upholstery.

There, cutting diagonally across the center seat, was the black nylon strap of the seatbelt. It was pulled taut, stretched tightly across the empty space as if wrapping around an invisible torso. And at the bottom, locked firmly into the receptor, the metal tongue was still clicked in.

The seatbelt was buckled tightly around nothing at all.

The sedan fishtailed violently across the rain-slicked metal grading of the bridge, the tires letting out a high-pitched, metallic shriek that echoed off the concrete barriers. Leo’s hands slammed against the steering wheel, his knuckles white, his forearms straining as he desperately tried to counter-steer. For a terrifying two seconds, the car drifted sideways, the headlights sweeping across the black, yawning void of the river below. The world narrowed to the scream of rubber, the blinding flash of the bridge’s support beams, and the suffocating panic rising in his chest.

With a brutal jerk of his arms, he corrected the skid. The car straightened out, its suspension groaning as the tires grabbed the asphalt on the far side of the river span.

Leo slammed his foot onto the brake, bringing the vehicle to a staggering, shuddering halt on the dark, narrow shoulder just past the bridge's exit. He threw the transmission into park, his breath coming in ragged, desperate gasps that sounded incredibly loud in the sudden confinement of the cabin. His chest heaved, his heart hammering against his ribs like a trapped animal trying to break free.

He sat there for a long moment, paralyzed, staring straight ahead through the windshield. The wipers continued their monotonous, rhythmic sweep—*thwack, thwack, thwack*—clearing the heavy mist only for it to instantly reform.

"I’m exhausted," he whispered aloud, his voice cracking, a pathetic attempt to anchor himself to reality. "I’m hallucinating. I fell asleep. I had a micro-nap. That’s all this is. I fell asleep at the wheel."

Slowly, deliberately, he reached up and turned on the overhead dome light. The interior of the car was instantly flooded with a harsh, yellowish glare, exposing every detail of the cabin. The vinyl dashboard, the dangling air freshener, the clutter of old receipts in the center console—everything looked completely normal. Everyday. Mundane.

Then, he forced himself to look into the rearview mirror.

The backseat was empty. But the seatbelt remained pulled tight, cutting a rigid, diagonal line across the empty fabric of the center seat, locked securely into the buckle.

Leo’s breath hitched. He unbuckled his own seatbelt, turned his entire upper body around, and gripped the headrest of the passenger seat, staring directly into the back. The air in the rear of the car was still freezing, so cold that the breath exiting his mouth formed thick, roiling clouds of white vapor. The smell was still there, too—not fading, but intensifying. It was the heavy, suffocating scent of stagnant river water, decayed autumn leaves, and cold, wet clay.

He reached out a trembling hand toward the buckled seatbelt. His fingers hovered just inches away from the black nylon strap. He could see that the fabric wasn't hanging loose; it was under immense tension, stretched taut as if keeping a heavy, solid mass pinned back against the seat.

With a sudden burst of panicked courage, Leo reached out and slammed his thumb down on the red release button of the buckle.

*Snap.*

The metal tongue ejected from the housing with a sharp click. The moment the mechanism released, the seatbelt didn't just snap back into its retractor coil. Instead, it was violently yanked backward, flying through the air and slamming against the plastic side panel of the car with a deafening *thwack* before slowly winding itself back up into the pillar. It was as if whatever had been holding it had suddenly vanished, or shifted forward.

Leo scrambled backward, his spine slamming against the driver's side door, his hands thrown up in a defensive posture. He stared at the empty seat, expecting a figure to materialize, expecting hands to reach out from the shadows. But there was nothing. Only the lingering cold and the smell of the earth.

Suddenly, his phone buzzed violently in its dashboard mount, breaking the silence with the loud, upbeat ringtone of a new incoming ride request. The sound was so jarringly normal, so aggressively mundane, that Leo let out a short, hysterical laugh.

He lunged forward and grabbed the phone. The screen was flickering wildly, the digital map spinning in tight, chaotic circles as if the internal GPS couldn't determine where on earth the vehicle was located. The pickup request window kept flashing on and off, the text corrupting into strange, unreadable characters, a jumble of letters and numbers that looked like an entirely different language.

Beneath the glitches, a single line of text remained perfectly legible at the top of the screen:

*Rider 'M' has updated the destination.*

Leo’s thumb hovered over the screen, trembling. He didn't want to accept. He wanted to throw the phone out the window, drive straight to a crowded, well-lit diner, and sit under the fluorescent lights until the sun came up. But before his fingers could even touch the glass to reject the notification, the app forced an automatic acceptance. The screen turned solid black, then bled back into the navigation map.

The route had changed. It was no longer directing him toward 442 Elm Street.

The new destination was a dead-end road that terminated directly at the edge of the river, deep within the industrial valley—a place known locally as Blackwood Turnout. It was an area completely abandoned by the city, a graveyard for rusted shipping containers and forgotten machinery.

Leo gripped the steering wheel, his mind racing. He could just ignore the app. He could turn the car around, drive back across the bridge, and go home. He put his foot on the brake and reached for the gear shift, ready to slide it into reverse.

But as he looked down at the center console, he froze.

The digital odometer on his dashboard, which usually displayed his total mileage, began to rapidly count backward. The numbers spun like a slot machine—hundreds of miles disappearing in seconds, clicking down, down, down. At the same time, the car's radio, which he had turned down to a low murmur, suddenly flared to life at maximum volume.

It wasn't playing jazz anymore. It was white noise—a loud, rushing static that sounded like a heavy downpour of rain hitting a metal roof. Beneath the static, a faint, rhythmic sound began to emerge.

*Thump... thump... thump...*

It was the sound of something heavy, wet, and solid, dragging itself against the underside of the car.

Leo screamed, a raw, ragged sound of pure terror. He slammed the car into drive and floored the accelerator. The tires spun on the wet asphalt, smoking as they struggled for traction, before the sedan launched forward into the thick, blinding fog. He didn't know where he was going; he just knew he had to keep moving. The boundaries of the city, the safety of the streetlights, everything felt infinitely far away. He was trapped on a road that felt like it was lengthening beneath him, a corridor of mist and dark trees that had no beginning and no end.

In the rearview mirror, the rear window of the car began to frost over from the inside, thick, crystalline patterns of ice spreading across the glass, mapping out the shape of two small, pale hands pressed firmly against the window pane.

The engine roared, a high-pitched, straining whine that vibrated through the floorboards and rattled his teeth. The high stone wall of the municipal cemetery, which he should have left miles behind by now, still hovered to his right, a continuous, unbroken barrier of moss-covered granite that felt less like a landmark and more like a prison wall.

On the dashboard, the phone was a chaotic beacon. The screen kept flashing, shifting between blinding white and deep, bruised purple. The navigation line—usually a comforting, bright blue path—had turned a deep, visceral red. It didn't show turns or street names anymore. Suddenly, the static blasting from the car speakers cut out completely. The abrupt silence was deafening, a physical pressure that made Leo’s ears pop.

Then, the car’s climate control system gave a violent shudder. The vents, which had been dormant since he unbuckled the rear seatbelt, began to hiss. A blast of air hit Leo’s face. It wasn't just cold; it was the freezing, dead air of a subterranean vault. Along with the temperature drop came a sound that made his stomach violently churn.

*Drip.*

*Drip.*

*Drip.*

It was the slow, rhythmic sound of water hitting the fabric of the rear seats.

Leo’s eyes flew to the rearview mirror. The back of the car was shrouded in a darkness that the dim dashboard light couldn't penetrate, but he could see the water. The fabric of the ceiling liner directly above the center seat was darkening, saturating with a heavy, black fluid that was beginning to pool and drip down onto the headrest. The scent of the river returned with a vengeance, no longer just a faint smell, but a suffocating wave of rot, decaying fish, and wet, ancient silt. It filled his mouth, coating his tongue with a metallic, muddy taste.

"Get out," Leo choked out, his voice a frantic, desperate whisper. "Get out of my car!"

He slammed his hand against the door panel, hitting the master unlock button. The heavy plastic switches clicked, but the locks didn't pop up. Up ahead, the endless stone wall abruptly vanished, replaced by a rusted, leaning signpost that read: *Blackwood Turnout. No Trespassing. City Property.*

Before Leo could even attempt to brake, the steering wheel violently wrenched itself out of his grip. It didn't just slip; it felt as though two invisible, immensely strong hands had grabbed the wheel and jerked it hard to the right.

The sedan veered off the paved road, crashing through a barrier of rotting wooden posts and plunging into the gravel turnout. The undercarriage scraped horribly against large rocks, the sound of tearing metal echoing through the night as the car barreled down a steep, unlit slope toward the edge of the river.

Leo slammed his foot onto the brake pedal with all his might. The pedal went completely soft, sinking all the way to the floor without resistance. The brakes had failed.

"No, no, no! Please!" he screamed, bracing his arms against the dashboard as the car hurtled toward the black, rushing waters of the river below.

The headlights illuminated the end of the line: a crumbling concrete loading dock that jutted out over the swirling, white-capped currents of the swollen river. The water was angry, churning violently from the earlier storm, throwing up sprays of dark foam. The car was on a direct trajectory to launch off the edge of the dock and into the deep, freezing abyss.

The sedan went into a wild, screeching sideways slide, the tires throwing up a massive cloud of gravel, dirt, and sparks. *Click.*

The seatbelt was buckled again.

"Leo," she whispered.

The sound didn't come from her mouth. It didn't travel through the air of the car. It echoed directly inside the caverns of his own skull, vibrating behind his eyes like a low, resonant frequency. A memory, violent and unbidden, slammed into Leo’s mind with the force of a physical blow. It wasn't a thought; it was a sensory explosion. He had swerved. He had hit something—or someone. There had been a horrific thud, a flash of a dark coat in his headlights, and then the sound of a heavy splash in the black river below the bridge.

So, he had walked back to his car, wiped the mud from his bumper, and driven away into the fog. He had spent two years burying that night beneath a mountain of long shifts, sleepless nights, and cheap distractions, forcing himself to believe it had just been a stray deer, a shadow, a trick of the light.

The screen, which had been completely dead, suddenly flared to life one final time. It didn't show the map. It didn't show the distorted characters. It showed a single, clean user interface with a bright green button at the bottom.

"End the ride, Leo," the voice commanded, a dark, heavy tide pulling at his consciousness. "Or I will."

The shattered screen of the phone held its blinding green light, pulsing like a dying star in the cramped, freezing cabin. *Complete Trip.* The text seemed to burn straight through Leo’s retinas, embedding itself into his frantic, looping thoughts.

Beside his ear, the wet, raspy sound of her breathing—or rather, the sound of the river moving through her collapsed throat—grew louder. Leo could feel the physical weight of her presence pressing against the back of his seat, a cold so absolute it began to numb his spine. The black water dripping from her chin had pooled in the center console, drowning the gear shift in a shallow, murky puddle of silt and tiny, dead river weeds.

"Complete it," the chorus of voices echoed inside his skull, a command that felt like a physical hand pressing down on the crown of his head. "The balance must be settled."

Leo’s hand shook so violently he could barely raise his arm. His fingers felt heavy, like lead, his joints stiffening as the unnatural frost creeping across the dashboard began to glaze over his skin. He looked at the shattered phone, then down at the ruined front end of his car pinned against the rusted shipping container. He looked at the driver’s side window, where the thick frost had formed the distinct, perfect outline of two hands pressing inward from the outside, as if a crowd of the drowned were waiting just beyond the glass.

There was no escape. There was no waking up from this. The universe had simply opened a ledger that had been left unbalanced for two years, and it had sent a collector to his door.

"If I do," Leo whispered, his voice nothing more than a dry click in his throat, "does it stop? Do you... do you rest?"

The entity behind him didn't answer with words. Instead, the blinding white of her pupilless eyes seemed to soften slightly, shifting from a gaze of pure, violent vengeance to one of profound, eternal weariness. It was the look of someone who had been drifting in the dark for far too long, dragging a heavy chain that anchored her to the mud of Route 9. She didn't want to haunt him; she just wanted the ride to be over.

Leo closed his eyes. A strange, sudden wave of calm washed over him, overriding the primal terror that had consumed him for the last hour. The frantic hammering in his chest began to slow, matching the deep, rhythmic, heavy thumping of the river currents outside the door. He thought of his two years of running, the constant paranoia, the sleepless nights, the phantom smell of river water that had haunted his dreams long before tonight. He had never truly left that bridge. He had just been idling on the shoulder, waiting for the bill to arrive.

"Okay," Leo murmured. "Okay."

He reached out his trembling, frostbitten index finger. The air between his hand and the dashboard felt thick, like pushing through deep water. He hovered over the cracked glass, looking at the glowing green button one last time.

He lowered his finger.

The moment his skin touched the shattered glass, the green light flared into an absolute, blinding flash of white that consumed the entire interior of the car. The deafening roar of the river, the hum of the broken radio, the whispers in his mind—all of it vanished in an instant, replaced by a profound, echoing silence.

*Snap.*

The morning sun broke through the heavy river fog at 6:45 AM, casting a bright, golden warmth over the industrial valley of Blackwood Turnout. A passing municipal utility truck slowed down, its driver blinking in confusion at the sight of a sedan parked neatly at the very edge of the crumbling concrete loading dock.

Within an hour, two police cruisers sat on the gravel turnout, their red and blue lights spinning lazily under the clear morning sky. A pair of officers stood by the driver's side of the sedan, looking through the open window.

The car was entirely undamaged. There was no warped metal, no shattered headlights, and no sign of an impact against the nearby shipping containers. The engine was off, the keys sitting quietly in the ignition.

"What do you think?" the younger officer asked, leaning over to peer into the cabin. "A runner? Left the car here overnight?"

The older detective shook his head, looking at the interior. The car was completely dry, smelling faintly of vanilla air freshener and old coffee. There was no frost on the windows, no mud on the floorboards.

"I don't think so," the detective said quietly, pointing a gloved finger inside. "Look at the dashboard."

Mounted on the plastic cradle was a cell phone. The screen was perfectly intact, displaying the standard home screen of a rideshare application. The interface showed a completed trip summary, indicating a flat-rate fare that had been successfully processed hours ago.

But it was the driver's seat that held the officers' attention.

Leo was gone. There was no sign of a struggle, no blood, no keys or wallet left behind. The driver's side door was unlocked, swinging open easily with a gentle push of the wind.

However, in the back of the car, cutting a clean, rigid, diagonal line across the center seat, the seatbelt was pulled taut. It was stretched tightly across the empty space, locked firmly into the receptor, the metal tongue clicked into place around nothing at all.

And on the pristine black fabric of the center headrest, completely dry but perfectly distinct, was the faint, dark stain of a single, pale handprint.
