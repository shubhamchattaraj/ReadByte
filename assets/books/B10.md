The sky above Olaria did not belong to the birds; it belonged to the heavy-hearted. 

From the cobblestone plazas of the Lower Ward, looking up was an exercise in civic melancholy. A massive, tangled web of rusted iron cables, hemp ropes, and oxidized copper mesh stretched between the spires of the city’s highest cathedrals and smoke-belching factories. This was the Grid—a great, groaning canopy suspended three hundred feet in the air, designed to catch the citizens who lost their anchor. 

Silas spent his days in the rigging of the Grid, his boots hooked into the thick hemp weaves, a heavy spool of lead-weighted twine slung across his chest. He was a net-mender, a man paid by the municipal council to patch the holes left by desperate people who hit the mesh with too much velocity. 

"Got a heavy one coming up from the Seventh District!" shouted Enoch, Silas’s senior partner, from a nearby watch-platform. Enoch was a thick-necked man whose skin had been tanned to leather by the high-altitude wind. He was pointing down into the sulfurous smog that perpetually blanketed the city floor. 

Silas wiped grease from his forehead and looked over the edge. Far below, amidst the gray slate roofs, a figure had just broken free from the cobblestones. To a stranger, it might have looked like flight, but to anyone in Olaria, the mechanics of Inverted Density were unmistakably violent. The man didn't soar; he fell upward. His coat tails whipped over his head, his boots kicked frantically at the empty air, and his arms flailed toward the earth he was losing. 

The cause was always the same: a sudden, catastrophic shift in the blood’s specific gravity, triggered by the weight of the mind. A bankrupt merchant whose ledger had finally collapsed; a lover whose betrayal had just been discovered; a thief whose conscience had caught up with him in a dark alley. In Olaria, guilt and grief were not abstract emotional burdens—they were literal, physical mass that altered a man’s relationship with the earth. But by some cruel joke of physics, when the internal weight became too great to bear, the world rejected you. You sank into the clouds. 

## _Thump._ 

The floater hit the underbelly of the Grid fifty yards from Silas’s position. The hemp net groaned, sagging upward under the reverse-pressure of the man’s inverted weight. 

Silas unhooked his safety carabiner and scrambled across the swaying ropes with the practiced agility of a spider. As he drew closer, he could hear the man sobbing. He was a middle-aged 

clerk, his pockets bursting with stolen municipal bonds that were now spilling out and fluttering down into the streets like dead leaves. 

"Don't struggle," Silas said, his voice flat, devoid of the pity that green recruits usually wasted on floaters. "The more you panic, the harder the sky pulls. You know the rules." 

"I didn't mean to take it," the clerk wheezed, his face pressed hard against the rough twine of the net, his body straining upward toward the gray expanse of the stratosphere. "My daughter... she was sick. The medicine cost three seasons' wages. Please, you have to let me go! If I go higher, maybe it stops!" 

"Nothing stops up there," Silas said mechanically. He reached into his leather pouch and pulled out a pair of iron-weighted ankle cuffs—standard issue for the Department of Buoyancy Control. "Above the Grid, the atmosphere thins out, but the lift doesn't. You just keep falling until your lungs pop and you freeze. Now, give me your legs." 

With practiced efficiency, Silas wrestled the clerk’s thrashing limbs into the heavy iron cuffs. The moment the lead-weighted metal clicked shut around the man's ankles, his upward momentum slowed. The net stopped straining. Silas attached a retrieval line to the cuffs and cranked the manual winch on his belt, dragging the clerk down toward the nearest stone pillar where the ground-ward stairs were carved. 

"You're saving my life," the clerk wept, his fingers clawing at Silas’s leather vest as they reached the platform. 

"I’m doing my job," Silas replied, untying the man and handing him over to the waiting graycoated city guards. "And the city will bill your family for the lead." 

Silas walked back to the edge of the platform and looked out over the sprawling, smoky expanse of Olaria. He was twenty-eight years old, but his shoulders were permanently hunched from the weight of his own harness. He wore a heavy leather vest lined with lead plates, and his boots were double-soled with solid iron. He was one of the "Heavy Men"—those whose cynicism, lack of ambition, and stubborn emotional numbness kept them firmly, unyieldingly rooted to the stone. Silas didn't love anyone; he didn't hate anyone; he didn't waves of regret disturb his day because he had designed his existence to be entirely frictionless. He was perfectly balanced, perfectly dense, and utterly hollow. 

That was why he was the best net-mender in the Upper District. The sky had no hold on him. 

He was preparing to pack his tools for the evening shift change when he saw her. 

She wasn't falling fast, which was the first thing that caught his attention. Most floaters shot into the sky like stones dropped into water, driven by a sudden, sharp spike of terror or remorse. But this woman was drifting upward with a slow, agonizing grace, like a dandelion seed caught in a gentle draft. 

She had broken away from the balcony of a dilapidated tenement house three blocks away. She wore a faded blue dress that billowed around her like an inverted parachute, and her dark hair fell upward, framing her face in a halo of silk. She wasn't screaming. She wasn't kicking. Her hands were folded loosely across her chest, and her eyes were closed, her face turned toward the sky as if she were simply going home. 

Silas blinked. He felt a strange, unfamiliar hitch in his chest—a momentary crack in his carefully cultivated density. 

"Enoch!" Silas called out, his voice sharp. "We’ve got a stray drift in Sector Four. I’m going out." 

"Let the patrol get it," Enoch grunted, wiping down a winch. "Shift's over in ten minutes, Silas. The tavern’s calling." 

"She’s missing the main net," Silas said, already clipping his line to the high-tensile guide wire that spanned the gap between the foundry chimney and the cathedral spire. "She’s heading for the fringe. If she misses the wire, she's gone." 

Without waiting for a reply, Silas threw his weight forward and slid down the guide wire. The wind roared in his ears, carrying the scent of coal smoke, boiled cabbage, and damp stone. He watched the woman rise. She was drifting directly toward a section of the Grid that had been damaged by a collapsed chimney the week before—a jagged, twenty-foot tear that hadn't been patched yet. 

He unhooked his secondary tether, swinging himself out into the open air beneath the wire. His heavy boots swung like pendulums. He timed the drift perfectly, dropping from the guide wire onto the swaying fringe of the damaged mesh just as the woman reached its level. 

He reached out through the tear in the net and caught her by the wrist. 

The moment his gloved hand clamped around her skin, he felt the pull. It wasn't the violent, jerky tug of a criminal or a madman. It was a steady, rhythmic, almost magnetic lift, like a warm tide trying to pull a boat out to sea. Her wrist was warm, her pulse steady and slow. 

She opened her eyes. They were a startling, luminous amber, completely clear of the bloodshot terror Silas was used to seeing in floaters. 

"You shouldn't have caught me," she said softly. Her voice was remarkably calm, carrying over the whistling wind. 

"It’s a city ordinance," Silas grunted, straining against his harness to pull her down through the gap in the mesh. "Unlicensed ascension is a public hazard. You could have hit a steam line." 

He dragged her body over the lip of the net. Because they were on the upper side of the mesh, her weight pushed her up into the rope, while Silas’s lead-weighted boots pressed down into it. 

They were trapped in a bizarre sandwich of opposing gravities, separated only by two inches of knotted hemp. 

Silas reached for his ankle cuffs, but as he pulled them from his belt, she placed a gentle, floating hand over his fingers. 

"Please," she whispered. "Don't put the iron on me. It hurts." 

"If I don't put the iron on you, you'll go up into the black," Silas said, though he found his fingers freezing over the brass latches of the cuffs. "What did you do? Who did you rob? Who did you hurt?" 

"I didn't hurt anyone," she said, a faint, melancholic smile touching her lips. "My name is Maeve. I haven't committed a crime, Inspector." 

"Then why are you turning?" Silas demanded. "People don't just float for nothing. The density law is absolute. Your mind is heavy, so your body is light." 

"My mind isn't heavy with guilt," Maeve said, her amber eyes locking onto his with an intensity that made Silas feel suddenly, terrifyingly exposed. "It’s heavy with beauty. And there is no room left for it down there." 

Silas stared at her. In seven years on the Grid, he had heard every excuse, every confession, every mad rambling of the inverted. But he had never heard anyone claim that beauty had stolen their gravity. 

He looked down at her hands. Her fingers were stained with ink and oil, and around her neck hung a small, glass vial filled with a strange, silvery liquid that seemed to defy gravity even within its container, clinging to the top of the glass rather than the bottom. 

"I can't leave you here without ballast," Silas said, his voice dropping to a rough whisper. For the first time in his career, he didn't click the iron cuffs. Instead, he took his own heavy leather utility belt—laden with tools and small lead weights—and wrapped it tightly around her waist. 

The extra weight dragged her down just enough to keep her pinned firmly against the upper side of the net. She didn't sink to the earth, but she stopped pulling toward the stars. 

"Thank you," she murmured, her face inches from his through the diamond-shaped gaps in the rope. "What is your name?" 

"Silas," he said, his heart hammering against his ribs like a trapped bird. He didn't understand what was happening to him. His density—his beautiful, protective, cynical density—was shifting. And for a net-mender in Olaria, that was the most dangerous thing in the world. 

Within three weeks, Silas had become a liar. 

In Olaria, lying was an expensive vice; it created a sudden, sharp internal friction that usually resulted in an immediate loss of density. But Silas wasn't lying to the magistrates or the graycoated guards of the Buoyancy Control Department—he was lying to himself, and somehow, his profound, lifelong cynicism acted as a layer of insulation, allowing him to carry the deceit without losing his footing on the cobblestones. 

He had hidden Maeve. Instead of turning her over to the nearest Buoyancy Sanatorium—where floaters were chained to stone benches and subjected to rigorous, mind-numbing labor until their spirits became "suitably heavy" again—he had smuggled her into an abandoned watch-station nested deep within the structural labyrinth of the Grid. 

It was a small, iron-walled cubicle built into the side of a massive stone buttress that supported the city's grand, dripping aqueduct. No one came here anymore. The old brass gears of the station's monitoring dials had long since rusted solid, and the air was permanently thick with the scent of wet moss, cold iron, and old soot. To keep Maeve from drifting up to the iron ceiling plates, Silas had bolted a heavy, high-backed oak chair to the center of the floorboards and modified it with thick leather harness straps, reinforcing its base with stacks of discarded iron slag from the nearby foundries. 

"You brought bread," Maeve said, her voice a soft, musical contrast to the industrial hum of the city filtering through the vent. She smiled as Silas slid through the narrow hatchway, his coat dripping with oil-slicked rain. 

She was sitting in the chair, her feet floating lightly a few inches above the floor, held down only by the wide leather straps pinned across her lap. Her dark hair was drawn upward toward the ceiling by the invisible tide of the sky, waving gently like seaweed in a calm current. 

"It’s rye," Silas said, setting the paper-wrapped loaf on a bolted iron table. "The baker was in a foul mood. His eldest son has started showing signs of light-headedness. The family is panicked. They’re burning all his books and poetry tonight, hoping it’ll settle his stomach back into the stone." 

Maeve sighed, her amber eyes turning toward the small, grated window that looked out over the endless sea of gray slate roofs. "They shouldn't burn the books. The light-headedness isn't a disease, Silas. It’s just the world’s way of saying there’s nothing left for them down in the soot. The earth here is so incredibly tired. The coal smoke, the greed, the endless, grinding misery of the machines... it crushes people until they break. And when they break, they finally realize they don't have to stay." 

"If everyone leaves, the city dies," Silas said pragmatically. He sat down on a low iron stool, the heavy soles of his boots hitting the floor with a comforting, solid thud. He didn't wear his full lead-lined vest inside the room—it made his ribs ache—but he kept enough ballast in his pockets to ensure he didn't twitch toward the ceiling. 

"Maybe some cities should die," Maeve replied softly. She extended her hand across the space between them. It drifted upward naturally, like a helium balloon, until Silas reached out and caught her fingers, pulling her arm down so he could hold her hand against his knee. 

Over the past weeks, Silas had learned the shape of her life. Maeve wasn't a criminal, nor was she a grieving widow or a bankrupt merchant. She was an archivist, a collector of forgotten things. She had spent five years in the deepest subterranean vaults beneath the Grand Library, translating ancient, crumbling texts from a time before the Inverted Density had ever cursed Olaria. 

"The old texts don't call it a curse," she told him, her voice barely a whisper as her fingers traced the calluses on his hand. "They call it the Great Rebalancing. Long ago, people kept everything inside. They hoarded their grief, their guilt, their love, and their secrets until the earth itself grew sick and heavy under the weight of their footsteps. So, the sky opened up a pressure valve. Now, the moment your heart becomes too full to coexist with the dirt, the universe releases you. You become buoyant." 

"Your heart is full of what, Maeve?" Silas asked, his thumb moving over her pulse point. It was steady, warm, and entirely lacking the frantic rhythm of the terrified floaters he caught daily. 

"Wonder," she said simply. "I found old maps of the world beyond the clouds, Silas. There are mountains up there that don't breathe sulfur. There are vast oceans of sweet water that aren't black with factory oil, and a sun that doesn't have to hide behind a veil of perpetual smog. I spent so many years visualizing it, wanting it, loving a world I’ve never even seen... that one morning, I woke up and my slippers were touching the ceiling of my bedroom. I hadn't lost my mind. I had just lost my anchor." 

Silas looked away, staring at the rusted rivet heads on the wall. He felt a cold, hard knot of fear twisting deep in his stomach. He was a creature of the mud, the iron, and the damp ropes. He understood the mechanical logic of winches, cables, and the cold efficiency of municipal law. He didn't understand wonder. And more than that, he realized with a terrifying, paralyzing clarity that he was falling in love with her. 

It was a catastrophic development. 

In Olaria, love was a highly volatile emotional state. For some, the sudden terror of vulnerability made them incredibly heavy, pinning their heels to the cobblestones like lead statues. For others, the sudden, overwhelming joy of connection stripped away their density instantly, sending them skyrocketing into the clouds during their wedding night. But Silas’s love was a toxic compound; it was mixed with a deep, agonizing despair. He loved a woman who belonged to the open sky, while he was chained to the earth by his own stubborn cynicism. 

"You're getting lighter, Silas," Maeve whispered, her free hand reaching out to touch the edge of his jaw. 

Silas stiffened, pulling back slightly. "No, I'm not. I’m a Heavy Man. I’ve always been heavy. Ask anyone on the Grid." 

"Your touch is different," she said, her amber eyes searching his face with an intensity that made him feel completely naked. "When you first caught me through the net, you felt like a mountain. Solid. Unyielding. Miserable, but grounded. But now... when you hold my hand, I can feel a vibration in your skin. You're starting to fight the floorboards." 

"I am _not_ floating," Silas said sharply, standing up to prove his point. His iron-soled boots hit the floorboards with a loud, aggressive _clack_ . But inside his chest, his heart felt like it was spinning, trying to find a north that no longer existed on the ground. 

The next day on the Grid, Silas found his work becoming treacherous. While patching a hightension line over the Industrial District, he misjudged a routine step on a swaying guide wire. Normally, his body’s natural density allowed him to drop predictably, his mass working with the earth's gravity to keep his movements sharp and centered. But this time, when his boot slipped on the grease-smeared cable, he didn't drop. 

He hung in the air for a fraction of a second, suspended like a pendulum, his body refusing to fall until the heavy lead plates in his vest finally dragged him down to a lower platform. He landed poorly, scraping his palms raw against the iron mesh. 

"You're off today," Enoch observed, squinting at Silas from across the watch-platform as he reeled in a stray cargo line. "Your balance is soft, Silas. You ain't getting light-headed on me, are you? You know what happens to net-menders who lose their gravity. They end up on the wrong side of the mesh, and the department doesn't pay pensions to floaters." 

"I’m fine," Silas muttered, wiping the blood from his palms onto his trousers and tightening the straps of his lead vest until it bruised his ribs. "Just a bad night’s sleep. The damp is getting into my joints." 

"Well, sharpen up," Enoch said, spitting a dark glob of tobacco over the edge into the smog. "The city’s changing the deployment schedule tomorrow morning. The Council is bringing in the highaltitude harvesters from the capital. They’re clearing out all the fringe sectors. Too many floaters have been escaping through the unpatched gaps in the outer nets. Any 'un-weighted anomalies' found hiding in the rigging or the old structures are going straight to the fuel furnaces or the Sanatorium." 

Silas felt the blood drain from his face. "The fringe sectors? Even the old structural buttresses and the aqueduct watch-stations?" 

"Every square inch," Enoch said, tapping his iron-tipped staff against the stone pillar. "They’re purging the roosts, Silas. If you’ve got any spare tools or scrap iron stashed away in the old lockers up there, you better move 'em today. The harvesters start their sweep at dawn." 

Silas didn't finish his shift. He claimed a sudden bout of lead-sickness—a common ailment among the net-menders caused by the constant, heavy weight of their gear—and hurried back through the labyrinth of swaying catwalks and dripping iron stairs to the aqueduct station. 

When he burst through the narrow hatchway, his breath catching in his throat, he found Maeve floating at the absolute limit of her restraints. 

The heavy oak chair was groaning, its thick wood creaking violently as her body strained upward toward the ceiling with a new, terrifying urgency. The small glass vial around her neck was vibrating against her collarbone, the silver liquid inside swirling like a miniature, frantic storm. 

"It’s pulling so much harder today," Maeve said, her voice strained with physical effort, though her face remained remarkably serene. "The sky... it knows they’re coming to close the gates, Silas. I can hear it through the iron walls. It sounds like a massive brass bell ringing just above the cloud line." 

"We have to leave right now," Silas said, his hands shaking as he dropped to his knees and began unbuckling the heavy leather straps holding her thighs to the chair. "I’ll take you down to the Lower Ward. We can hide you in the old coal cellars beneath the foundries. The gravity is thicker down there, deep in the earth. The weight of the dirt will hold you." 

"No!" Maeve cried out as the final leg strap snapped free. 

Without the weight of the oak chair, she shot upward instantly, her shoulders striking the iron ceiling plates with a dull, heavy thud. She pinned against the metal overhead, her arms and legs splayed out against the rust as if she were standing on a solid floor, looking down at Silas with tears gathering in the corners of her eyes. 

"I can't go down into the dirt, Silas!" she wept, her voice echoing in the small room. "The coal cellars will kill me. The density down there is toxic to anyone who has seen the light. It would crush my spirit until I become exactly like the rest of them—hollow, gray, and dead while still drawing breath. I would rather fall into the empty sky until I freeze." 

Silas stood on the wooden floorboards, looking up at her. The distance between them was barely eight feet, but it felt like a canyon that spanned different dimensions. He reached his right hand upward, his fingers straining toward her descending hand, but the heavy iron soles of his boots held him firmly, mercilessly to the floor. 

"I can't lose you," he whispered. The raw, unadulterated truth finally broke through his protective armor, and the confession felt like a physical wound opening in his chest. "If you go into the sky, you die. If you stay here, they take you. What am I supposed to do?" 

"Come with me," Maeve said, extending her arm further down from the ceiling, her fingertips hovering just out of his reach. "Lose your weight, Silas. Let go of the city. Let go of the iron." 

"I don't know how!" Silas cried out, a single tear finally escaping his eye. 

To his horror and amazement, the droplet didn't fall to the floor. It drifted upward through the air, rising slowly like a tiny glass bead, until it landed with a soft _ping_ against the iron ceiling plate right next to Maeve’s hand. 

They both stared at the rogue droplet of moisture. 

Silas looked down at his boots. The iron soles were still flat on the wood, but inside his chest, the final remnants of his protective cynicism had completely dissolved, replaced by a terrifying, beautiful, and lighter-than-air despair. He was no longer a Heavy Man. He was something far more dangerous: a man caught between the earth and the stars, tearing himself apart from the inside out. 

The dawn over Olaria broke in jagged shards of bruised purple and industrial orange. 

From the central platforms of the Grid, the sound of the high-altitude harvesters could already be heard—a rhythmic, mechanical clanking of iron gears and steam winches as the city guards began their systematic sweep of the upper structures. They carried long, weighted poles tipped with iron hooks, designed to snare floaters like stray kites. 

Silas stood on the edge of the outer catwalk, far past the safety perimeter of the Lower Ward. Maeve was tied to his back, secured by a thick web of hemp ropes. To keep her from pulling them both into the sky prematurely, Silas was wearing his maximum ballast: his lead-lined vest, his double-iron boots, and a heavy coil of anchor chain wrapped around his waist. Altogether, he carried nearly two hundred pounds of dead weight. 

And yet, his toes were lifting inside his boots. 

"There they are!" a voice bellowed from the fog behind them. 

Silas turned his head slightly. Enoch was standing fifty yards away on a connecting span, surrounded by four city guards in gray coats. Enoch’s face was a mask of confusion and betrayal. 

"Silas!" the older man shouted, his voice cracking against the morning wind. "What the hell are you doing? You’re a net-mender! You’re one of us! Step away from the edge and hand over the floater. The Council will give you a pension if you turn her in now." 

"I don't want a pension, Enoch," Silas called back, his voice surprisingly calm. He felt a strange lightness in his skull, an clarity he hadn't experienced since he was a child. "I’ve spent seven years patching holes in a world that’s trying to keep people from breathing. I’m done fixing your cages." 

"You can't fly, you idiot!" Enoch yelled, taking a heavy, iron-shod step forward. "You've got two hundred pounds of lead on you. If you jump with her, you won't float—you’ll just rip her in half, or she’ll pull you into a spin and you’ll both smash into the foundry spires!" 

"He’s right, Silas," Maeve whispered against his ear, her arms wrapped tightly around his neck. Her body was straining upward against the ropes like a hot-air balloon trapped in a cage. "The iron is too heavy. You can't carry the weight of the city into the sky. You have to let it go." 

"I can't let it go yet," Silas murmured, looking down into the abyss beneath his feet. "If I drop the iron now, before my mind is ready, the sky will reject me. I’ll just freeze. I have to find the balance." 

"He’s resisting! Get the hooks!" the guard captain ordered. 

The guards advanced, their long poles reaching out across the narrow catwalk. An iron hook caught the shoulder strap of Silas’s lead vest, tearing the leather with a sharp screech. 

Silas didn't look back. He looked at Maeve's reflection in the glass vial around her neck. The silver liquid was no longer swirling; it had grown perfectly still, pointing like a compass needle toward a small, brilliant break in the clouds far above them—a single shaft of pure, unpolluted sunlight cutting through the sulfur. 

"Hold on," Silas said. 

He stepped off the edge. 

For the first three seconds, it was a disaster of conflicting physics. Silas’s iron boots and lead ballast dragged them down toward the cobblestones with terrifying speed. They dropped past the middle tiers of the Grid, past the smoke stacks of the bakeries, directly toward the jagged iron spikes of the lower district fence. 

"Silas!" Maeve screamed, the wind ripping the sound from her lips. 

But Silas wasn't panicking. He closed his eyes. He didn't think about the spikes below, or the guards above, or the laws of Olaria. He thought about his life—the years spent cold, alone, and numb in the grey rigging. He thought about the absolute futility of trying to remain heavy in a world that was meant to be light. He felt the profound sorrow of every floater he had ever caught, every desperate soul he had chained back to the dirt. He accepted the grief of his city, the beautiful tragedy of its existence, and finally, he let himself want something more. 

He let himself love her completely, without fear of the fall. 

The internal shift was instantaneous, like a glass bottle breaking beneath the sea. The specific density of his blood inverted. The guilt didn't make him heavy anymore; it became fuel, a massive explosion of upward kinetic energy that collided violently with the two hundred pounds of metal strapped to his body. 

The downward plummet stopped with a violent jerk, twenty feet above the iron spikes. 

For a terrifying moment, they hung suspended in mid-air, perfectly balanced between the earth and the stars. Silas’s boots were pulling down, while his upper torso and Maeve’s entire body 

were pulling up. The strain on his spine was immense. He was a human tug-of-war between two opposing universes. 

"The buckles!" Silas choked out, his vision blurring from the pressure. "Maeve... cut the ballast!" 

Maeve’s fingers, ink-stained and nimble, reached around his chest. She didn't have a knife, but she didn't need one. She found the main release latch of his utility harness—the single brass pin that held the lead plates and the anchor chain in place. 

With a sharp pull, she yanked the pin. 

The heavy leather vest split open. The iron-lined boots, the lead plates, and the heavy iron chains slipped from Silas’s body, falling down into the darkness like iron rain. They struck the cobblestones below with a massive, distant clatter, shattering the stone slabs. 

Without the iron, Silas didn't just float; he soared. 

The sky didn't feel like empty air anymore; it felt like a rushing river, catching them in its current and pulling them upward with breathtaking speed. They shot past the lower district, past the middle tiers, and burst through the grand canopy of the Grid like a bullet through paper. 

Silas saw Enoch’s face flash past—his mouth wide open in astonishment—before the netmender’s platform vanished beneath a sea of gray fog. 

They rose higher than anyone in Olaria had ever gone. The air grew cold, crisp, and remarkably clean. The smell of sulfur and coal smoke faded, replaced by the scent of ozone and ice. Silas gasped, his lungs filling with pure, unpolluted air for the first time in his life. It burned, but it was a beautiful burn. 

Below them, the great city of Olaria looked like nothing more than a rusted gear forgotten in the mud, its smoky breath unable to reach the altitude they had achieved. 

"Look," Maeve whispered, pointing upward. 

Silas looked. 

They had broken through the eternal smog layer. Above them, the sky wasn't a terrifying black void; it was a deep, brilliant azure, filled with endless clouds that looked like floating islands of white wool. And far in the distance, rising from the cloud-sea like silent giants, were the peaks of mountains that didn't smoke—green, lush, and bathed in a golden sunlight that had never touched the cobblestones of the Lower Ward. 

The Inverted Density hadn't ceased. Silas could still feel the sky pulling at his bones, but it was no longer a violent force. It was simply a gentle drift, guiding them toward the distant green peaks. 

Silas reached out, his bare feet dangling in the open air, and wrapped his arms securely around Maeve. They were no longer falling, and they were no longer sinking. They were simply moving sideways, traveling along the ceiling of a world they had finally outgrown.
