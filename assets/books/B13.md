"I got it..." Hux wheezed, coughing up a dark slurry of blood and cybernetic fluid. "The extraction... it finished a split-second before the flash-bang. The entire Level 5 file is on this drive. The ledger, the kill-switches, the authorization codes from the Commissioner... it's all in here." 

"Hux, hold on, I can get you out," Oliver said, his voice flat, steady, and entirely devoid of the panic that had consumed him just hours ago. The civilian named Oliver was gone; only Detective Vance remained. 

"Don't be an idiot," Hux gasped, shoving the heavy data slate into Oliver’s jacket pocket. "My shop is dead. I’m dead. They didn't come to arrest you, Vance. Look at their gear. This is a blackfile scrub team. If you don't get that data to a public broadcast array, you’re just another ghost in the system. Go out the back... the ventilation shaft behind the primary server stack. It leads to the sewer main." 

A heavy thud echoed from the other side of the counter. The third operative was reloading, his boots crunching methodically over the broken glass. 

Oliver looked at Hux for a fraction of a second, nodding once in grim acknowledgment. He grabbed the operative's dropped submachine gun from the floor, rolled out from behind the counter, and fired a blind, three-round burst to suppress the advancing shooter. Without waiting to see if the shots hit home, he spun around, tore away the loose grate of the ventilation shaft behind the smoking server racks, and lunged headfirst into the darkness. 

The shaft was tight, smelling of rust and ancient stagnant water. Behind him, the sound of automatic gunfire echoed through the metal tube, bullets ripping through the aluminum casing near his boots. Oliver scrambled forward with feral desperation, using his elbows and knees to propel himself through the pitch black until the shaft suddenly ended, dropping him ten feet down into a roaring torrent of underground wastewater. 

He hit the icy, chemical-laden current hard, swallowed a mouthful of bitter water, and fought his way back to the surface. 

He dragged himself onto a narrow concrete walkway flanking the sewer main, coughing violently, his body shivering as the adrenaline began to recede. He reached into his jacket pocket with a trembling hand. The obsidian data slate was still there, its reinforced waterproof casing glowing with a faint, steady amber light. 

He turned the slate over. A simple, text-based interface lit up his face in the darkness of the sewer tunnel. 

**Mene-Tech Level 5 Encrypted File: 'Project Clean Slate'** _Status: Extracted & Unverified. Warning: File contains high-frequency carrier waves capable of broadcasting across all standard consumer neural networks._ 

Oliver leaned his back against the slimy brick wall, his breath rattling in his chest. He tapped the screen, bypass protocols flickering past as his law-enforcement decryption training effortlessly came back to him. He didn't just find a ledger of corruption; he found the true purpose of the Remi-Shield infrastructure. 

Mene-Tech hadn't built a tool for psychological healing. They had built a city-wide obedience network. The "Premium Tier" wasn't for the elite; it was a honeypot designed to identify individuals with high-value trauma, political secrets, or dangerous knowledge. Once a target signed the contract, Mene-Tech didn't just mute the memories; they replaced them with a digital back-door. At any moment, the Department of Public Safety could activate a localized broadcast, turning every Remi-Shield subscriber within a five-mile radius into a suggestible, compliant drone, or worse, triggering an artificial stroke to clean up a political mess without a trace. 

And his own partner, Detective Miller, had been the one managing the payouts. 

Oliver looked up at the ceiling of the sewer tunnel. Above him, through a rusted iron manhole cover, he could see the neon-blurred sky of District 9. The amber countdown banner in his visual field was flashing erratically now, its code glitching due to the physical trauma of the extraction. 

**Mene-Tech Remi-Shield Services: Error 404** _Time Remaining until Permanent Cognitive Restoration: 0 Hours, 12 Minutes. Synaptic lock imminent. System override detected._ 

He had twelve minutes before his brain completely locked into its current state. If he didn't broadcast the decryption keys contained on the slate within those twelve minutes, the data would remain trapped on a physical drive that the Department of Public Safety would inevitably track down and destroy. He needed a high-output broadcast array, and there was only one within walking distance that could punch through the corporate firewalls of Neo-Chicago: the District 9 Public Telecom Relay Station, located directly above the central plaza. 

Oliver stood up, his muscles aching, his jaw set. He checked the magazine of the captured submachine gun. Ten rounds left. 

"Enough," he whispered to the dark. 

He climbed the rusted iron rungs of the ladder beneath the manhole cover, pushing the heavy iron disc aside, and stepped out into the pouring rain of the central plaza. 

The plaza was crowded even at midnight. Dozens of citizens walked briskly beneath umbrellas, their eyes vacant, their faces illuminated by the massive holographic advertisements floating between the skyscrapers. None of them noticed the bleeding, drenched man stepping out of the sewer until he sprinted toward the secure glass doors of the Telecom Relay Station. 

"Hey! Clear the area!" a voice shouted from the plaza. 

Oliver turned his head. Emerging from a sleek, unmarked black hover-cruiser at the edge of the square were three more tactical operatives, led by a man whose face was not obscured by a helmet. It was Detective Miller—his old partner. The severe facial scar across Miller’s cheek twitched in the ambient blue light of a neon billboard. 

"Vance!" Miller yelled over the roar of the rain, drawing his heavy service weapon. "Stop running! You're an anomaly, Oliver! The system already replaced you five years ago! You don't have a life to go back to!" 

"I have my name, Miller!" Oliver shouted back, his voice cutting through the storm. 

He turned and fired his remaining ten rounds directly into the reinforced glass doors of the Telecom Relay Station. The safety glass spiderwebbed but didn't shatter. Using his shoulder as a battering ram, Oliver threw his entire weight against the fractured barrier. The glass gave way, showering the sterile lobby inside with thousands of crystalline shards. 

An alarm began to wail, a high-pitched crimson siren bathing the lobby in a strobe effect. 

Oliver scrambled over the security desk, his eyes locking onto the primary fiber-optic trunk line rising through the center of the room—a massive pillar of pulsing blue light that fed data to every billboard, terminal, and neural implant in District 9. 

Behind him, Miller stepped through the shattered doorway, his boots grinding heavy against the glass. He raised his weapon, his cold cybernetic eyes tracking Oliver’s movements with lethal precision. 

"It’s over, partner," Miller said, his voice flat. "Even if you plug that slate into the trunk, the corporate firewalls will kill the upload before it hits ten percent. You can't fight the network." 

Oliver didn't look back. His fingers found the auxiliary data port at the base of the fiber-optic trunk. He slammed Hux’s black data slate into the slot. 

"You're right, Miller," Oliver said, a grim, bloody smile spreading across his lips as he looked at the flashing countdown banner in his eyes. 

_0 Hours, 0 Minutes. Synaptic Lock Engaged._ 

"The corporate firewalls will stop an external upload," Oliver whispered, his fingers flying across the slate’s terminal interface. "But they won't stop a diagnostic hard-reset originating from a licensed user's neural implant. I’m not uploading the file to the network, Miller. I'm routing the network _through my own brain_ ." 

"Vance, stop!" Miller screamed, his composure shattering as he realized what Oliver was about to do. 

Oliver hit the final sequence key. 

The world didn't end with an explosion. It ended with an absolute, terrifying silence. 

The massive fiber-optic pillar flared from a calm sapphire blue to a blinding, incandescent white. A violent jolt of pure, unadulterated data erupted from the trunk line, traveling through the slate and shooting straight into Oliver’s neural implant. 

Every single blocked memory, every line of corrupted corporate code, every transaction ledger, and every dirty secret of the Department of Public Safety tore through Oliver’s consciousness like a solar flare. His eyes glowed with a faint, electric blue luminescence as his brain acted as a living bridge, broadcasting the decrypted Level 5 files directly into the public telecom array. 

Across Neo-Chicago, millions of people stopped in their tracks. 

In the central plaza, the citizens under their umbrellas dropped their belongings. The vacant, manufactured peace in their eyes vanished, replaced by an instantaneous, overwhelming flood of their true history. Women fell to their knees as the memories of lost children returned; men gripped their heads as old traumas, corporate crimes, and stolen lives came rushing back through the shattered dams of their minds. 

The artificial bridges had collapsed city-wide. The fine print of forgetfulness had been torn to shreds. 

Miller dropped his weapon, stumbling backward as his own cybernetic eyes glitched wildly, overloaded by the sudden, massive data-dump flooding the department's encrypted tactical network. The faces of the people he had helped erase flashed before his eyes in an endless, damning carousel. 

Oliver fell back against the base of the fiber-optic pillar, his body completely spent, his breath slowing to a faint, erratic rhythm. The glowing digital banners in his visual field flickered one last time, then dissolved into a field of clean, empty blackness. 

The heat in his head was gone. The throbbing had stopped. 

He looked down at his hands. They were covered in grease, blood, and rain, but they were his hands. He remembered the accident. He remembered Clara. He remembered the badge he used to wear and the crimes he had fought to expose. The guilt was heavy, an immense, crushing weight that sat squarely on his chest, but for the first time in five years, it was _his_ weight to carry. 

He was no longer a ghost paid for by a monthly subscription. He was Oliver Vance. And he was finally awake.
