The classroom clock ticked to 11:36 AM.

Aanya Rao stood at the chalkboard, her fingers stained with white dust, a geometry problem half-sketched behind her. The late morning sun filtered through the high windows, and outside, birds warbled through a gentle breeze that swayed pine branches. The hills of Solen, her home for ten years, lay quiet and cradled in fog.

“Now, if angle A is forty degrees—what’s angle C?” she asked.

Hands shot up. The murmurs of children filled the air like birdsong.

And then it happened.

At exactly 11:37:00 AM, time stopped.

Not like a slow freeze. Not like sleep. It was instant.

Every student froze. Not in fear—but like a paused video. One girl had her hand in mid-air, half-raised. A boy yawned, mouth wide but unmoving. Chalk dust hung suspended in sunlight.

Aanya blinked.

She blinked again.

The second hand on the classroom clock trembled, caught mid-tick.

At first, she thought it was a prank. An earthquake? A gas leak? She stepped back, slowly, cautiously, heart hammering in her chest. The stillness was… absolute.

She rushed to the nearest student—Mehul, a quiet boy who always sat in the front row. She touched his shoulder. Rigid. Not stiff like death—just… frozen.

"A prank," she whispered aloud. "Right?"

She opened the window. The birds outside hung mid-flight.

Her breath caught in her throat. Her heart pounded so hard it hurt.

She burst into the hallway. Silence. The corridor echoed with nothing. She ran toward the staffroom—Mrs. Kaushik frozen mid-sip of tea, Mr. Biswas reaching for the switchboard. Time had curled up like a cat and fallen asleep.

The town beyond the school was no better. She ran into the open streets of Solen, calling out.

No one moved.

A child stood with a balloon. The wind had stopped. The balloon floated, but did not drift. The leaves hung perfectly still.

She stumbled back, panic seizing her. Her breathing turned shallow, rapid.

Was she dead? Dreaming? Had she lost her mind?

Then she heard it.

A faint hum.

Not from the sky. Not from the earth.

From inside her.

Like a vibration in her chest. A pulse. A thread pulling her toward something.

She turned slowly toward the hill’s slope, where the old part of town lay nestled in the shadow of Solen’s bell tower. There, half-hidden beneath tangled ivy, stood **Dinanath Watch Repairs**, a place that had been closed for nearly a decade. Or so she thought.

But smoke curled from its chimney.

And the door stood open.

The hands on the signboard’s giant clock still turned.

Aanya took a step toward it.

Then another.

And though the world remained frozen, somewhere deep in the bones of Solen, something ancient had awakened.

Time was watching.

Aanya stood at the foot of the old shop, its chipped wooden sign swaying lightly—*Dinanath Watch Repairs*. Time had stopped, and yet… this place moved. The faint smell of burning wood leaked from a chimney above, curling around her senses like déjà vu.

She stepped inside, half-expecting the stillness to shatter like glass underfoot.

But instead, she was met with… ticking.

Dozens—no, hundreds—of clocks.

Cuckoo clocks, pendulum clocks, grandfather clocks. Pocket watches hung from a wire strung across the ceiling. All ticking. All alive.

The sound was overwhelming. Not chaotic, not noise. A rhythm. The rhythm. The heartbeat of something hidden.

A figure stirred in the back corner of the shop, bent over a desk cluttered with brass gears and glass domes.

“Come in, Aanya,” the man said without looking up.

She froze. “How do you know my name?”

He looked up then. Thin, wiry, with a long face and a snowy beard that reached his collar. His eyes were strangely clear—like they belonged to a younger man—and when he smiled, it was like time itself bowed to his memory.

“Because you’re not frozen like the others,” he said simply. “And because I’ve been expecting you.”

“Expecting me? What’s going on? Why is everyone… why is everything…”

He raised a finger and motioned toward a chair.

“You may want to sit down.”

She did, hesitantly. The clocks ticked around them like a thousand whispers.

“I’m Dinanath. You may have heard of me.”

She nodded slowly. “My students said your shop closed years ago.”

“Oh, it did. In this *reality,* anyway.”

She stared at him, unsure if she had heard that correctly.

“You’re saying this… isn’t real?”

“Oh, it’s very real,” he said, carefully winding a tiny silver watch. “Just not bound to the flow you know as time.”

She exhaled sharply. “What does that even mean?”

Dinanath leaned forward. “Time isn’t a line, Aanya. It’s a lattice. A fabric woven across perception. Most of the time”—he chuckled at his own pun—“it holds. But sometimes, it tears. What you’re inside now is a fracture.”

“A… fracture?”

“Some call it a *stillpoint*. A moment that breaks away from the river of time and floats on its own. Rare. Dangerous. But necessary.”

“Necessary?”

“For healing,” he said.

Her eyes narrowed. “Healing what?”

He met her gaze. “You.”

Aanya stood abruptly. “What are you talking about? I didn’t cause this.”

“Not consciously. But you’re the anchor. The reason why *you* can move while others can’t. Your soul is in conflict with time.”

She backed away. “That’s insane.”

“And yet,” he said gently, “you’re here. Awake. While the rest of the world sleeps in a perfect 11:37 AM.”

Outside the windows, nothing moved. Even the birds in the trees were sculptures of stillness.

“How long has it been like this?” she asked.

“For the world? A second. Maybe less. For you and I?” He glanced at the ticking clocks. “As long as we need.”

Her mind reeled. “What happens when it ends?”

“Depends.”

“On what?”

“On what you choose to remember.”

His words struck her like a bell.

“Why can’t I remember anything from this morning?” she asked, suddenly aware that her memories seemed to stop a few hours ago.

He nodded. “That’s where it starts. The fracture doesn’t form around a moment. It forms around what the soul refuses to let pass through it. Memory is both the lock and the key.”

“I don’t understand.”

He stood and walked toward a tall case. Inside was a single ornate clock with black wood and a single glowing pendulum. “Come. Watch this.”

As she stepped closer, the pendulum began to shimmer. The ticking slowed. The hands twisted in reverse. And then she saw it—

A flash of something buried in her mind.

A red dress on a clothesline.

Rain pouring onto a narrow alley.

A child's laughter—suddenly silenced.

Then darkness.

She gasped and stumbled back.

“What… what was that?”

“A memory,” Dinanath said softly. “One you’ve buried so deep, even time itself bends around it.”

“I don’t have a child,” she said instinctively. “That… wasn’t real.”

He didn’t correct her. He didn’t have to.

Her hands trembled.

“Why is this happening to me?” she whispered.

“Because sometimes,” he said, “our pain becomes so large, the world must stop to let it pass.”

Aanya sat back down, her knees weakening.

“I don’t know what I’m supposed to do.”

“You don’t need to do anything,” Dinanath said. “You need to remember. And choose.”

“Choose what?”

“To stay in this fracture… or to wake the world again.”

She looked out the window.

The town was still frozen. But in the far distance, she could now see something else. A ripple, almost imperceptible, moving through the sky like a fold in glass.

Time… was watching.

And waiting.

The silence had begun to change.

It was subtle at first—the kind of shift that only happens when you stop resisting and start listening. Aanya stepped out of Dinanath’s shop with her breath still unsteady, but her senses sharpened. The frozen world felt… brittle. The light no longer sat softly; it bounced unnaturally off glass and skin. Shadows lingered a second too long. And some of the birds overhead weren’t *quite* where she remembered them being.

As if the illusion was fraying.

Time wasn’t just paused.

It was… cracking.

Dinanath had let her go, saying only, “Memory makes the map. You’ll find the places you need.”

So she walked. Past the school. Past the empty bus stop. Down roads that usually buzzed with life but now sat still, soundless. She could hear her own footsteps echo.

In the window of the old sari store, she caught her reflection.

Except it wasn’t hers.

Not exactly.

Aanya leaned closer. Her face stared back—same eyes, same tired lines—but there was something off. The reflection blinked too late. The eyes seemed sadder. Older. And then—

A child appeared behind her.

In the reflection only.

A girl, maybe six, with short curls and wide eyes. Wearing a red dress. The same red from the flicker of memory Dinanath had shown her.

Aanya spun around.

The street was empty.

When she turned back, the window was a mirror again. Her reflection, just hers.

Her knees wobbled. She leaned on the glass to steady herself.

The name came suddenly. Like a whisper caught in wind.

“Meera…”

The name burned on her tongue. Bitter and sweet.

Meera.

She had said that name before.

She had *screamed* that name once.

Another ripple tore through her memory.

The red dress.

A toy horse rolling down concrete steps.

Rain.

Thunder.

And then silence.

She stumbled away from the shop and ran—not knowing where her feet were taking her, only that they were being pulled. Past the clock tower. Past the bakery. Through a narrow alley that led to a gated house at the edge of the woods.

Her old house.

The one she had left years ago.

She didn’t remember walking here. Didn’t even remember putting the key in her pocket. But when she stood before the rusted gate, it was already unlocked.

She stepped through.

The garden had overgrown. Tulsi plants reached over cracked tiles. The windchime still hung by the porch—still, of course—but gently rattling in the echo of memory.

Inside, everything was coated in dust.

Except one thing.

A child’s drawing pinned to the wall by a fridge magnet.

A house. A hill. A stick figure woman and child.

“Ma,” the drawing read, in childish script.

Aanya reached for it, fingers trembling. Her throat tightened as the memory crashed into her like a tidal wave.

It was raining. That afternoon. Hard.

She had let Meera play on the verandah. Just for a minute.

One minute.

She had taken a call. Work. Something petty. Something forgettable.

And when she returned—

The toy horse had fallen.

And so had Meera.

Down the steps.

Onto the stones.

She had slipped. Screamed. Reached for her mother who was not there.

And in the seconds that followed, time had not stopped.

Time had *taken*.

She dropped to her knees. The sob came from somewhere ancient. Somewhere she’d locked away for years. It tore out of her like a curse, like a release. Her hands gripped the floor as if she could anchor herself there, in that house, in that grief.

But grief is a wave. It cannot be stopped. Only faced.

Somewhere behind her, a faint tick echoed.

She turned.

The hallway clock. Long dead for years.

Now ticking.

Only once every few seconds.

But ticking.

The fracture… was responding.

She understood now.

This stillness wasn’t a punishment. It wasn’t madness.

It was mercy.

Time had given her one impossible gift: a chance to remember, to hold what was too painful to face.

And maybe, just maybe… to let go.

That evening, she walked to the ridge above Solen. The sun still hovered in the same place. But now, the clouds around it were moving—just a little. Like a breath had returned.

At the edge of the cliff, there was a girl sitting.

She didn’t question it.

Meera looked at her, not with blame, but with eyes full of something far more difficult—understanding.

“Why did you forget me, Ma?” she asked.

Aanya knelt beside her.

“I didn’t,” she whispered. “I just couldn’t bear to carry you.”

Meera took her hand.

“You don’t need to carry me,” she said. “Just remember.”

A breeze passed. A real one. The first she’d felt in days.

A leaf fluttered to the ground.

The fracture was breaking.

Time was beginning to move again.

And soon, she would have to choose.

The sun hung stubbornly above the hills, locked in the amber of 11:37 AM, casting long shadows that did not move. But the town of Solen was no longer a frozen photograph. It was shifting—minutely, imperceptibly, like a dream stirring before waking.

The air carried warmth again. A leaf stirred on a windowsill. Far off, a pigeon blinked.

Time, like a tide, had begun to rise.

Aanya descended the ridge where she had seen Meera. Her knees were weak, her breath uneven, but something had eased inside her. Not gone. But softened. She felt the difference in how her feet met the earth. No longer running from memory, she was walking within it.

As she passed the town square, she noticed a woman standing near the frozen statue of Gandhi—a woman she hadn’t seen before. Mid-thirties, sunburned face, carrying a rucksack that had clearly lived through storms.

But the woman blinked.

Moved.

Turned toward Aanya.

“You’re awake too,” the woman said, her voice rough from disuse.

Aanya stared at her. “You… you can move?”

The woman nodded. “Since the fracture began.”

“Who are you?”

“Rekha,” she said. “Journalist. Or… was. I don’t know what I am in here.”

Aanya’s breath caught. “There are others?”

Rekha gave a sad smile. “Yes. Scattered. Some woke up days ago. Others… just today.”

“This has lasted days?”

“Inside, yes. Outside?” She shrugged. “It’s still 11:37.”

Aanya felt her pulse quicken. “Why are we here?”

Rekha turned her gaze to the mountains. “You already know. Don’t you?”

Aanya nodded. “Grief. Memory. Pain too loud for time to hold.”

Rekha gestured toward the lane behind her. “There’s someone who wants to meet you.”

Together they walked through the still alleys, past the vendors paused mid-bargain and the beggar with a frozen coin in his palm. The town had become a canvas of stillness, interrupted only by those few the fracture had awakened.

They reached a house that once belonged to Solen’s only painter, long abandoned and now claimed by ivy and silence.

Inside sat three people—none of whom Aanya recognized.

There was a man in his sixties with spectacles slipping down his nose and eyes that never seemed to blink. A girl, maybe twenty, dressed in a school uniform too short for her frame. And a woman in a red shawl who stared at the wall as if waiting for something to emerge from it.

They all turned when she entered.

“Aanya,” said the man. “We were hoping you’d find your way here.”

She hesitated. “You know me?”

He nodded. “We’ve all seen the same clock.”

She looked at them one by one. “You’re all… fractured?”

“No,” the girl corrected. “We’re all *healing*.”

The woman in the red shawl finally spoke, her voice a whisper. “The fracture chose us. Or maybe… we chose it. A place between memory and motion. A hallway between sorrow and peace.”

Aanya sat down slowly.

Rekha leaned against the doorframe. “We’ve all seen different memories. Different losses. But the same time.”

“11:37,” Aanya said.

The man nodded. “The moment of fracture. When our lives turned and couldn’t turn back.”

Aanya spoke without thinking. “I lost my daughter.”

There was no gasp. No pity. Only silence. A silence that understood.

“I looked away for a second,” she continued. “A single second. And she fell.”

The girl across from her gently reached forward and placed a paper crane into Aanya’s hand. “You were holding your own storm. You can put it down now.”

Tears welled in Aanya’s eyes. “I don’t know if I can forgive myself.”

“Then forgive the moment,” the woman in the red shawl said. “It’s not always our fault. Sometimes time simply *happens*.”

They sat together for hours, not speaking much, just existing inside that sacred hush.

When night should have fallen, the sky flickered.

The first true signal.

Time was breaking back in.

The fracture was losing hold.

“I need to return to Dinanath,” Aanya said suddenly.

Rekha nodded. “He’ll know when the moment comes.”

As she walked back through Solen, she noticed other subtle changes.

A radio crackled in a shop window.

Smoke curled again from chimneys.

And now, the sound of ticking came not just from the watch shop—but from the entire town. The clocks were waking.

She reached Dinanath’s shop just as he was winding a golden pocket watch, the final one on the wall.

“You’re back,” he said without turning.

“There are others,” she said. “Like me.”

“I know.”

“Why did we all wake?”

“Because pain echoes,” he said. “And when enough echoes fill a place, time cannot ignore it.”

Aanya stepped forward. “How do I fix it?”

“You don’t.” He looked up at her. “You accept it. You choose to carry it forward… or stay.”

“Stay?”

He nodded. “You can remain here. In the fracture. Forever. No pain. No time. Just stillness. But only for those who refuse to let go.”

Aanya closed her eyes.

She saw Meera again.

Smiling.

Laughing.

Falling.

And then standing again.

She saw herself not as a broken woman—but as a mother who had loved, lost, and still remained.

“I want to go back,” she whispered.

“Then there’s one last thing.”

He held out the pocket watch.

It ticked, softly.

“This will start the world again. But once you open it… the fracture ends. No more do-overs. No more limbo. Just time. Moving. And pain, and joy, and all the noise that comes with it.”

Aanya held the watch.

It was warm.

Alive.

“I’m ready,” she said.

“Then speak the name you’ve held back.”

Her throat trembled.

She said it.

“Meera.”

The watch clicked.

Outside, the church bells of Solen began to toll.

11:38.

The sound rang through Solen like thunder behind lace.

**Dong.**\
**Dong.**

The bells of St. Agnes Church hadn’t rung in years—not since the old priest passed. And yet, they now pealed through the crisp morning air with the solemn clarity of resurrection.

**11:38.**

Time had moved.

Aanya stood in front of Dinanath’s shop, holding the open pocket watch. The gears ticked with ancient memory, glowing faintly against her palm like a living heart. Around her, the town stirred, stretching from its long silence.

The clouds began to shift.

A breeze moved through the trees with purpose.

And then, one by one, people resumed motion—*not* like unpaused video, but like they had been gently nudged awake from a long, healing sleep.

Back at the school, students finished half-sentences mid-word. A girl blinked and realized her hand had been raised too long. Birds completed their wingbeats and took off toward the hills. A water droplet finally fell from a leaf.

The world breathed again.

But something was different.

Not broken.

Not perfect.

Just… deeper.

Aanya walked slowly through the school grounds, passing children who hadn’t noticed a thing. Laughter echoed across the courtyard, seamless, ignorant of the eternity that had passed between seconds. She passed the staff room—Mr. Biswas sipping tea, Mrs. Kaushik still scrolling through her phone.

Normal.

But inside her, nothing was the same.

She returned to her classroom. The chalk still rested in its tray. The geometry problem still half-drawn on the board. She picked up the chalk and completed the final angle. Triangle closed.

Whole.

She turned to her students and smiled. “Alright. Where were we?”

They groaned in unison. “Math class, Miss!”

She grinned. “Good. Let’s carry on.”

That afternoon, Aanya didn’t take her usual route home. Instead, she walked toward the western edge of Solen, where the ridge dropped into a rolling sea of pine and mist. The bench near the drop was damp from morning fog, but she sat anyway.

The air was brighter. Time was flowing again, but slower now—no longer rushing past her ears like wind, but humming beneath her skin like a soft melody.

She opened her notebook and wrote:

*Today, I returned to time.\
Not as a prisoner,\
But as someone who learned\
That memory is not a chain.\
It is a thread.\
And Meera…\
You are stitched into every breath I take now.\
Not as loss.\
But as love.*

Behind her, a voice spoke. “That’s beautiful.”

She turned to see Rekha, the journalist, smiling.

“You remember?”

Rekha nodded. “Most of us do. Not all. Some woke up with only the peace. Others with names they hadn’t said in years. But we remember enough.”

“Do you think we’ll ever understand what that was?”

Rekha sat beside her. “Maybe not. But I think we were meant to feel it, not dissect it.”

They watched the sky go from bright to golden.

Then Rekha stood. “I’m going to write again. Not about war or politics. I think… I’ll write stories now. Like this one.”

“Start with yours,” Aanya said. “It’s worth telling.”

As the town of Solen glowed under the descending dusk, Aanya felt a soft flutter against her wrist. A butterfly. Yellow. It landed, waited, and took off.

That night, she dreamt of Meera again.

But this time, they were not standing on that rainy stairwell.

They were flying kites.

On a rooftop.

In sunlight.

Laughing.

And when she woke, she did not cry.

A week passed.

The town returned to its rhythm.

Except now, more people lingered in conversations. They watched the sunrise. They made time to listen.

The watch shop stood closed again, silent and sealed like a story complete. But every now and then, Aanya thought she heard ticking from behind its walls.

On her desk sat a letter.

It was unsigned.

Written in neat, angular strokes.

*To the one who remembered—\
You chose well.\
The fracture is gone.\
But the echo will remain.\
Use it.\
Teach them how to hold silence,\
And how to walk through time without fear.*

There was no address. No sender.

Just a final sentence scratched across the bottom:

**"Time is never still. Only we are."**
