The neon sign outside _The Entangled Bean_ didn't so much glow as it sort of vibrated between wavelengths, occasionally phasing into a shade of ultraviolet that made your teeth itch. It was tucked down a damp alleyway between a dry cleaner and a shop that sold antique clocks that all told different, highly speculative times. 

Arthur Pendelton adjusted his tweed jacket, shook the drizzling rain from his umbrella, and pushed open the door. Inside, the cafe smelled of freshly ground espresso, old paperbacks, and a distinct, sharp tang of ozone that tasted faintly of copper and bad decisions. 

"Afternoon, Arthur," said Brenda from behind the counter. Brenda was a woman of indeterminate age who wore safety goggles pushed up onto a nest of graying hair and a heavy-duty apron stained with what Arthur deeply hoped was raspberry syrup, though it occasionally shimmered with a faint bioluminescence. 

"The usual, Brenda," Arthur said, sliding onto his favorite vinyl stool at the counter. "And please, tell me the subatomic destabilizer isn't acting up today. My left knee has been anticipating the weather in a parallel dimension all morning." 

"Oh, she’s purring like a radioactive kitten," Brenda cackled, patting a massive, brass contraption that looked like a Victorian espresso machine crossed with a particle accelerator. Pipes twisted frantically around glowing glass vials, and a small digital readout on the side currently read: _Probability Matrix: 94.2% Caffeine / 5.8% Existential Dread._ 

Arthur smiled safely. He liked the routine. Every Tuesday at 4:00 PM, he came in, paid seven dollars and fifty cents, and ordered the _Rockstar Riff Macchiato_ . 

The rules of _The Entangled Bean_ were simple, etched onto a brass plaque by the sugar shaker: 

1. **One Choice Per Brew:** Your drink connects you strictly to the timeline of a single, unmade decision. 

2. **Look, Don't Touch:** You are a passive observer. The alternate reality cannot see, hear, or interact with you. 

3. **The Cold Limit:** When the steam dies and the cup goes cold, the quantum entanglement snaps. You return to your boring, linear reality. 

For Arthur, a man who spent his days data-entering insurance claims into a spreadsheet that felt actively hostile, the _Rockstar Riff_ was his escape. For fifteen glorious minutes, while his macchiato steamed, he got to watch _Alternative Arthur_ —the version of him who hadn’t panicked at his Royal Academy audition twenty years ago. That Arthur wore leather pants, played sold-out stadiums in Berlin, and had a jawline that didn't look like it was giving up on life. 

Brenda set a ceramic mug down in front of him. "Here you go, love. Don't let it sit too long. The Higgs-Boson foam is extra frothy today." 

Arthur sighed happily, inhaling the scent of vanilla and stadium pyrotechnics. He didn't notice that Brenda had been multitasking, simultaneously brewing a _Corporate Blend_ for a stressed accountant and a highly unstable, off-menu concoction for a twitchy man in a trench coat who had just bolted for the restroom. 

Arthur reached out, cupped the mug in both hands, and took a deep, greedy gulp. 

The world didn't just tilt; it inverted. 

The walls of the cafe dissolved into a swirling vortex of neon violet and static hiss. The smell of coffee vanished, replaced instantly by the suffocating stench of wet asphalt, cheap cigars, and a sudden, violent downpour. 

Arthur blinked. He wasn't sitting on a vinyl stool anymore. He was hovering, phantom-like, about three feet off the ground in a dark, garbage-strewn alleyway. The rain passed right through his ethereal body. 

"Ah," Arthur thought, expecting to see his rockstar self stepping out of a limousine. "Here comes the Berlin after-party." 

But there was no limousine. Instead, a heavy metal door burst open at the end of the alley. A man stumbled out, tripping over a stack of plastic crates. He was wearing a torn, mud-splattered leather jacket, his face was bruised, and he was clutching a glowing, metallic briefcase to his chest like a newborn child. 

Arthur leaned closer, his spectral eyes widening. The man had his nose. He had his receding hairline. But this version of him had a desperate, feral look in his eyes, and a jagged scar running across his left cheekbone. 

"What choice is this?" Arthur muttered aloud, panicking. He had never auditioned for a gang. He had never stolen a briefcase. He had never even stolen a pen from the office supply closet without feeling guilty for a fiscal quarter. This was completely wrong. This wasn't the _Rockstar Riff_ . 

Sirens wailed in the distance—strange, synthesized sirens that sounded like dying synths. Red and blue searchlights cut through the rain from the sky above. _Hover-cars?_ Arthur thought, bewildered. _What timeline is this?!_ 

The alternate Arthur scrambled to his feet, wiping blood from his lip. He backed up against the brick wall, trapped in the dead-end alley. He looked up at the sky, cursing under his breath. 

Then, the alternate Arthur froze. 

He didn't look past Arthur. He didn't look through him. 

His bloodshot eyes locked directly onto Arthur’s spectral, hovering face. 

Arthur gasped, instinctively trying to back away, but as a ghost in this reality, he had no traction. "He can't see me. Rule number two. You can't interact. You never interact." 

The alternate Arthur dropped the briefcase. He lunged forward, reaching right out toward the empty air where Arthur’s floating consciousness hung. His hands didn't pass through. Instead, Arthur felt a terrifying, freezing grip tighten around his actual, physical collar back in the cafe. 

The desperate man in the rain bared his teeth, a wild, manic grin breaking through the dirt on his face. He leaned in so close Arthur could smell the copper on his breath. 

"Perfect timing," the alternate Arthur hissed, his voice a gravelly mirror of Arthur's own. "Swap with me." 

Before Arthur could even open his mouth to scream, the desperate man yanked him violently forward, pulling him straight into the mud. 

The physical sensation of being pulled through a trans-dimensional tear felt remarkably like being dragged face-first through a frozen hedge backwards, whilst simultaneously being recalculated by a very old, very tired computer. 

Arthur’s lungs violently expanded, inhaling not the comforting, artificial vanilla syrup of _The Entangled Bean_ , but a lungful of sulfur, ozone, and freezing, gritty rain. He hit the ground hard. His knees—specifically his bad left one—smacked into the cracked asphalt of the alleyway, sending a sickening jolt of pain up his spine. 

"Ouch!" Arthur shrieked, his voice cracking. He scrambled backward on his hands and knees, splashing into a puddle of oily, iridescent water. "Brenda! Brenda, the matrix has malfunctioned! I’m experiencing tactile feedback!" 

But Brenda wasn’t there. There was no brass espresso machine. There was no safety-gogglewearing barista. 

Arthur looked down at his hands. They were covered in grease and dark, drying blood. He wasn’t wearing his sensible, beige corduroy trousers anymore; he was wearing heavy, combat-style cargo pants tucked into combat boots that felt three sizes too big. He reached up to clutch his chest and felt the stiff, cracked leather of a jacket that smelled strongly of gunpowder and cheap tobacco. 

He looked across the puddle. 

Sitting on the vinyl stool back at _The Entangled Bean_ was... him. Or rather, the other him. The rugged, scarred version of Arthur was currently staring down at his own hands, flexing his fingers with a look of profound, ecstatic relief. Through the shimmering, translucent portal of the 

coffee steam—which was rapidly thinning—Arthur could see the warm, safe interior of the cafe. He could see a half-eaten blueberry scone on a saucer just inches away. 

"Hey!" Arthur yelled, lunging toward the fading window of steam. His hands smacked into thin air. "Hey! You can't be in there! That's my life! My spreadsheets! My subscription to _Cat Fancy_ !" 

The alternate Arthur in the cafe looked up, caught Arthur's eye through the thinning vapor, and gave a cheerful, mocking little salute with a ceramic mug. "Thanks for the coffee, mate," the alternate Arthur’s voice echoed faintly, sounding like it was being played through a broken walkie-talkie. "Good luck with the Syndicate. If they ask about the hard drive, tell 'em it's already encrypted in New Kyoto." 

"What? What is New Kyoto?!" Arthur cried, banging his fists against the empty air. "Swap back! The rules say you can't interact! Rule number two! _Rule number two!_ " 

"Rule number two doesn't apply when someone drinks a _Multiverse Mutiny Macchiato_ by mistake, sweetheart," the alternate Arthur smiled, taking a slow, appreciative sip of the warm brew. "The steam is dying, Artie. Better run." 

With a final, pathetic _pop_ that sounded like a bubble bursting, the vision of the cozy cafe vanished completely. The air grew instantly colder. Arthur was left utterly alone in the dark, pouring rain of a city he didn't recognize. 

Arthur sat in the mud for a solid thirty seconds, hyperventilating. He was a man who planned his route to the grocery store using three different traffic apps to minimize the risk of unexpected roundabouts. Now, he was stranded in a reality where the sky was a deep, unnatural shade of bruised purple, and giant, neon billboards floated lazily between towering, crumbling skyscrapers. One billboard featured an enormous, hyper-realistic hologram of a cybernetic cat advertising something called _Cyber-Kibble: Now with 40% More Real Meat-Adjacent Matter!_ 

"Okay, okay, think," Arthur muttered to himself, rocking back and forth. "You are having a vivid, caffeine-induced hallucination. Brenda accidentally used the dark roast. It’s fine. I’ll just wait for the coffee to go cold. It always goes cold. How long does a macchiato take to cool down? Ten minutes? Fifteen if she used the insulated mugs?" 

A sudden, deafening _whoosh_ shattered his rationalization. 

A sleek, matte-black vehicle with no wheels dropped out of the sky, hovering barely six feet above the entrance of the alley. It looked like a cross between a Lamborghini and a predatory beetle. Spotlights mounted to its underbelly snapped to life, casting blinding, blue-white beams down into the darkness. 

The light washed over Arthur. 

A mechanized megaphone boomed from the belly of the craft, shaking the loose bricks in the walls. **"CITIZEN PENDELTON. YOU ARE IN POSSESSION OF CLASSIFIED** 

## **QUANTUM DATA PROPERTY OF THE CHRONOS CORPORATION. STAND DOWN AND SUBMIT TO IMMEDIATE NEURAL DEGAUSSING."** 

"Neural degaussing?" Arthur whispered, his stomach dropping. "That sounds like it involves magnets and a complete loss of personality." 

The heavy metal door at the end of the alley burst open again. Two men in armored tactical gear, sporting helmets with glowing red visors, stepped out into the rain. They weren't holding coffee mugs. They were holding large, metallic rifles that hummed with a terrifying, high-pitched electrical current. 

"There he is!" one of them shouted, pointing a gloved finger at Arthur. "He’s still got the case! Take him alive, but feel free to disintegrate the lower extremities!" 

"The lower extremities?!" Arthur shrieked. "Those are my walking legs!" 

Instinct—pure, unadulterated, evolutionary panic—took over. Arthur didn't think. He didn't weigh his options. He scrambled to his feet, grabbed the heavy metallic briefcase his alternate self had dropped, and bolted in the opposite direction. 

As he ran, a bolt of blue electricity shot past his left ear, striking a stack of plastic crates behind him. The crates didn't just break; they instantly dissolved into a puddle of smoking, purple goo. 

"Oh dear God!" Arthur screamed, tucking the briefcase under his arm like a football and sprinting toward the mouth of the alleyway. 

His boots splashed through the puddles. The briefcase was surprisingly heavy, clunking awkwardly against his hip. He burst out of the alley and onto a crowded, chaotic sidewalk. 

If Arthur thought the alley was bad, the main street was a technological nightmare. People shuffled past in a daze, their faces illuminated by glowing data-streams projected directly onto their retinas. Some had chrome limbs; others wore breathing masks that hissed in rhythm with their steps. Nobody looked up when Arthur burst out of the darkness, screaming and covered in mud. 

"Excuse me! Sorry! Out of the way, please!" Arthur yelled, pushing past a man who appeared to have a digital billboard embedded in his trench coat displaying stock prices. 

Behind him, the two armored guards emerged from the alley, shoving civilians aside with brutal efficiency. "Clear the sector! Priority target!" 

Arthur ran blindly down the neon-lit avenue. Up ahead, he saw a glowing, subterranean entrance with a sign that read: _Sub-Transit Sector 4_ . It looked like a subway. Arthur loved subways. Subways meant public transit, and public transit meant rules, schedules, and hopefully, transit police who didn't want to disintegrate his lower extremities. 

He leaped down the concrete stairs three at a time, his bad knee screaming in protest. He reached a row of sleek, biometric turnstiles. A digital screen flashed: _INSERT CHIP TO PROCEED._ 

"Chip? What chip? I don't have a chip! I have a Visa debit card with twelve dollars on it!" Arthur panicked, frantically patting down the pockets of the stolen leather jacket. 

His fingers hit something hard in the interior pocket. He pulled out a small, glowing glass vial filled with a swirling, iridescent blue liquid. It looked suspiciously like the espresso concentrates Brenda used at the cafe. Taped to the vial was a messy, handwritten note from his alternate self: 

_Artie, if you're reading this, you're alive. Congrats. If you get cornered, drink this. It’s an unrefined espresso shot brewed from a dying universe. It’ll buy you ten seconds. Don't blink._ 

The guards appeared at the top of the subway stairs, their red visors locking onto him. 

"End of the line, Pendelton!" one of them yelled, raising his rifle. 

Arthur looked at the vial. He looked at the guards. He looked at the turnstile. 

"I am an insurance claims adjuster," Arthur said to himself, tears mingling with the rain on his face. "I have a hybrid sedan. I don't do this." 

With a desperate cry, Arthur twisted the cap off the vial and slammed the glowing blue liquid down his throat. 

It tasted like lightning, sour patch kids, and extreme existential dread. 

The unrefined espresso shot hit Arthur’s stomach like a tiny, caffeinated supernova. 

For a fraction of a second, his heart didn’t beat; it _vibrated_ . The taste of lightning and sour candy exploded behind his teeth, followed by an overwhelming wave of vertigo that made him feel as though his consciousness was being stretched through a very long, very narrow straw. 

Then, everything stopped. 

Literally. 

The rain froze in mid-air, thousands of perfect, needle-thin water droplets suspended around him like a glass bead curtain. The two armored guards from the Chronos Corporation were frozen at the top of the concrete stairs, their bodies leaned forward in mid-stride. A bright, crackling arc of blue plasma was halfway out of the barrel of the lead guard’s rifle, frozen in space like a jagged neon sculpture. A few feet away, a commuter who had been mid-sneeze was locked in an expression of horrific, eternal agony. 

Arthur blinked. The handwritten note had said the dying-universe espresso would buy him ten seconds, but in this eerie, silent stasis, ten seconds felt like an eternity. 

"Right," Arthur panted, his breath not even forming mist in the frozen air. "Ten seconds. Don't blink. Don't waste it." 

He didn't have a transit chip, but with the entire universe paused, the biometric turnstile’s scanning laser was just a static red line painted across the floor. Arthur scrambled over the barrier, kicking his heavy combat boots over the metal casing, and hauled the bulky metallic briefcase with him. He sprinted down the silent concrete corridor of Sub-Transit Sector 4. 

He passed frozen commuters, static digital advertisements, and a cleaning drone suspended two inches off the ground in mid-hover. His lungs burned. The sheer kinetic energy of running through a frozen timeline felt like pushing through thick, invisible gelatin. 

_Eight... nine... ten._ 

The world snapped back like a rubber band. 

The deafening roar of the city, the screech of the subway trains, and the thunderous _CRACK_ of the plasma rifle all returned at once. Behind him, the guard's plasma bolt blasted into the empty space where Arthur had been standing a microsecond before, obliterating the biometric turnstiles in a spectacular shower of sparks and molten plastic. 

"Where did he go?!" a guard screamed, his voice echoing down the tunnel. "He blinked! He's using unauthorized temporal displacement!" 

Arthur didn't look back. He burst onto the train platform just as a massive, levitating bullet train shrieked to a halt. Its sleek, pneumatic doors hissed open. Without thinking, Arthur dove through the opening, crashing onto the metal floor inside just as the doors slammed shut behind him. The train instantly accelerated, throwing Arthur against the far wall with enough G-force to make his teeth rattle. 

He lay on the floor of the train, clutching the briefcase to his chest, gasping for air. The other passengers—a mix of tired-looking people with cybernetic eyes and corporate workers in sharp, metallic suits—glanced at him with mild annoyance before returning to their personal datastreams. In this world, a bruised man covered in mud holding a stolen briefcase was apparently just a standard Tuesday commute. 

Arthur pulled himself into a seat and stared at the briefcase. It was sleek, made of a brushed titanium alloy, with a digital keypad that glowed with a faint crimson light. 

_What did Alternative Arthur say? 'If they ask about the hard drive, tell 'em it's already encrypted in New Kyoto.'_ 

"I don't care about New Kyoto," Arthur whispered, his voice trembling as he wiped a smear of grime from his forehead. "I just want my boring life back. I want to argue with my landlord about the water pressure. I want to organize my sock drawer by thread count." 

He looked down at his wrist, hoping by some miracle his sensible, waterproof digital watch was there. It wasn't. Instead, he wore a strange, biometric band that monitored his adrenaline levels. But then he noticed something else. 

The leather jacket. The cuffs were stiff. And as he stared at his hands, he realized the sensory details of this world weren't fading. Usually, when a coffee at _The Entangled Bean_ went cold, the alternate timeline began to pixelate at the edges. The sounds would muffle, the smells would vanish, and you would gently slide back into your own skin. 

But the mud on his face felt real. The ache in his bad knee was sharp and persistent. 

"The coffee isn't going cold," Arthur realized with a spike of pure terror. "Because I'm not _watching_ this timeline anymore. I'm _in_ it. He stole my seat. He’s drinking my macchiato. If he finishes that cup before I find a way out, the entanglement snaps with me on the wrong side of the fence." 

Panic, cold and sharp, threatened to paralyze him. But Arthur Pendelton, despite his beige lifestyle, was a man who understood bureaucracy, systems, and logic. If a quantum portal could be opened by an espresso machine, then a quantum portal was ultimately just a matter of formula, ingredients, and delivery. 

He looked at the briefcase. He looked at the red glowing keypad. 

"Think, Arthur," he muttered. "What would a rogue, high-stakes, dimension-hopping version of you use as a password?" 

He tried his birthday. _04-12-88._ The keypad flashed red: **ACCESS DENIED.** He tried his mother’s maiden name using the alphanumeric layout. **ACCESS DENIED.** He tried the registration number of his hybrid sedan back home. **ACCESS DENIED.** 

"Come on!" Arthur hissed, banging his fist against the titanium box. "If you're me, what is the one thing we never forget? The one thing that haunts us?" 

He froze. His mind flashed back to the _Rockstar Riff Macchiato_ . He remembered why he drank it every week. He drank it to escape the memory of his failed Royal Academy audition twenty years ago. The audition where he had panicked, forgotten his sheet music, and walked off the stage in silent humiliation. 

What was the number of the audition seat? The one etched into his brain forever? 

_Seat 42C._ 

Arthur hurriedly punched in the corresponding numbers: **0-4-2-3.** (Using 3 for 'C'). 

The keypad whirred. A soft, hydraulic hiss echoed through the train carriage as the latches clicked upward. The lid popped open. 

Arthur held his breath and looked inside. There was no bomb. There was no alien technology. Instead, nestled in a velvet-lined compartment, was a portable, highly compact version of Brenda’s brass espresso machine, powered by a glowing, pulsing blue core that looked like a captured fragment of a shooting star. Next to it sat a single, pristine ceramic mug and a pressurized canister labeled: _CONCENTRATED QUANTUM GROUNDS: DO NOT EXPOSE TO OPEN AIR UNLESS PREPARED FOR INSTANT REALITY COLLAPSE._ 

There was also a small digital sticky note attached to the lid. It lit up, displaying a message in his own messy handwriting: 

_Hey, Artie. If you figured out the code, you're smarter than you look. This is the Prototype. It’s what Chronos wants. It’s a portable transit engine. It brews a way out. If you want to go home, you need to brew a 'Return to Origin' espresso. Just add water, press the red button, and drink. But warning: it tastes like absolute garbage._ 

"I love garbage," Arthur sobbed with relief. 

The train screeched to a halt at Sector 9: The Industrial Spire. The doors slid open, and Arthur immediately bounced out of his seat. He needed water. He needed hot water. 

He bolted out of the station and into a bleak, concrete plaza surrounded by massive, venting factories. The rain was still pouring, but it wasn't hot. He needed a heat source. 

Suddenly, a heavy, metallic thud echoed from above. Arthur looked up. Three Chronos Corporation hover-crafts were descending from the purple sky, forming a triangle directly over the plaza. Spotlights pinned him to the ground. 

From the shadows of the plaza, a dozen armored guards emerged, their red visors glowing menacingly through the downpour. They raised their weapons in perfect unison. 

"Nowhere left to run, Pendelton," the lead guard barked through his megaphone. "Drop the briefcase and put your hands on your head." 

Arthur looked at the guards. He looked at the briefcase. He was trapped in the middle of an open plaza, surrounded by heavily armed soldiers from a dystopian future, with absolutely no combat training whatsoever. 

But he did have a portable espresso machine. 

Arthur slowly knelt down in the pouring rain, placing the open briefcase on the wet concrete. 

"That's right. Submitting to authority. Very sensible," the guard said, signaling his men to advance. 

"I am not submitting," Arthur shouted back, his voice surprisingly steady. "I am preparing a hot beverage." 

With trembling fingers, Arthur caught the rainwater pouring from the sky, channeling it into the small reservoir of the portable machine. He snapped the canister of concentrated quantum grounds into the intake valve. The machine groaned, its miniature brass pipes glowing a violent, blinding blue as the star-core activated. 

"He's priming a device! Fire! Fire!" the guard screamed. 

A volley of blue plasma bolts erupted from the rifles, tearing through the air toward Arthur. 

Arthur slammed his thumb down on the machine's red button. 

A hyper-pressurized stream of pitch-black liquid shot out of the nozzle, filling the small ceramic mug in exactly 0.5 seconds. The steam that exploded from the cup didn't just rise; it expanded outward like a shockwave, creating a massive, swirling dome of shimmering white mist that deflected the incoming plasma bolts, sending them ricocheting harmlessly into the sky. 

Inside the dome of steam, time began to fray. The concrete beneath Arthur’s knees dissolved into a floor of old paperbacks and vinyl tiles. He could hear the faint, muffled sound of a jazz radio station playing in the distance. 

He picked up the scalding hot ceramic mug. The coffee inside was black, oily, and literally bubbling with anti-matter. Through the swirling vapor of the cup, he could see _The Entangled Bean_ . He could see his alternate self, sitting on his vinyl stool, reaching out to grab a blueberry scone. 

"My scone," Arthur whispered fiercely. 

He didn't wait for it to cool. He raised the mug to his lips and took a massive, agonizingly hot gulp of the Return to Origin brew. 

It tasted like burnt hair, old pennies, and extreme, unmitigated paperwork. It was, without a doubt, the worst thing he had ever tasted. 

The world exploded in a flash of pure white light. 

Arthur opened his eyes. 

The air was warm. The smell of sulfur and rain was gone, replaced by the rich, comforting aroma of freshly ground dark roast and vanilla syrup. He was sitting on a soft vinyl stool. 

He looked down. He was wearing his beige corduroy trousers. His tweed jacket was perfectly dry. His hands were clean, free of blood and grease, though his tongue felt quite badly scaled. 

Directly across the counter, Brenda was staring at him, her safety goggles pushed up, an eyebrow raised. 

"Well?" Brenda asked, leaning on her elbows. "How was the _Rockstar Riff_ today, Arthur? You look like you've seen a ghost. Or a copyright lawyer." 

Arthur blinked, looking around frantically. Sitting on the stool next to him was a stressed accountant, quietly weeping into a _Corporate Blend_ . The door to the restroom clicked open, and the twitchy man in the trench coat walked out, looking completely ordinary. 

Arthur looked down at his saucer. His blueberry scone was untouched. His ceramic mug was still steaming gently, the Higgs-Boson foam forming a perfect little whirlpool in the center. 

He looked around the cafe for the scarred, leather-jacketed version of himself, but the shop was quiet. The alternate Arthur was gone, sent back to his own chaotic, neon-drenched timeline to face the Chronos Corporation with nothing but a half-empty cup of regular macchiato. 

Arthur let out a long, shaky breath that slowly turned into a laugh. He reached out, picked up his spoon, and gently stirred his coffee, watching the foam swirl. 

"You know, Brenda," Arthur smiled, his heart finally returning to its normal, boring, 70-beatsper-minute pace. "I think I'm done with the exotic blends. From now on, just give me a regular house drip. Black. No sugar." 

Brenda cackled, turning back to her massive, rumbling machine. "Suit yourself, love. But you're missing out. Next week we're trialing a roast that lets you see what happens if you'd invested in Bitcoin in 2010." 

Arthur shuddered, taking a cautious sip of his perfectly normal, beautifully mundane coffee. "No thank you, Brenda. I think I've had quite enough regret for one lifetime."
