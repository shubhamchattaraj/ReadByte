The city rose from the horizon like a memory returning after centuries of silence.

At first, Elias mistook it for another trick of the Ocean. A pale line shimmered where the sky met the sea, growing brighter with every passing moment. Then towers emerged—slender spires of crystal that caught the sunlight and scattered it into countless colors across the water. Bridges arched gracefully between them, suspended without chains or pillars. Rivers flowed through the city, not downward, but upward into the clouds before falling again as silver rain.

No one aboard the Starling spoke.

The Keeper stood at the bow, his weathered hands resting on his staff, his eyes fixed on the distant skyline. Beside him, the Tide King remained unusually still. The arrogance that had defined every word he had spoken since Elias met him had disappeared, replaced by something closer to reverence.

Even the sea seemed different.

The endless gray waters of the Last Ocean slowly transformed into a brilliant sapphire blue. Schools of fish surfaced alongside the ship, creatures unlike anything Elias had ever seen. Their scales reflected the stars, even beneath the afternoon sun. Giant whales breached peacefully in the distance, their songs echoing across the water with a harmony so pure that it made the silence between them feel sacred.

It was as though the Ocean itself had awakened from a long and troubled sleep.

By sunset, the Starling entered the city's harbor.

There were no docks crowded with merchants.

No fishermen repairing nets.

No guards waiting to inspect travelers.

The harbor was completely empty.

Yet it did not feel abandoned.

It felt... preserved.

As though the city had simply paused and was patiently waiting for someone to return.

The ship drifted forward on its own before stopping beside a staircase carved from white marble that disappeared beneath the water.

When Elias stepped onto the stone, he expected to hear the familiar splash of wet boots.

Instead, the marble remained perfectly dry.

Not a single drop clung to his feet.

He turned back toward the sea.

The Starling was already drifting away from the harbor.

"Wait!"

The ship stopped only long enough for the Shipkeeper to offer him a gentle smile.

"It will return when your journey is complete."

"And if I never return?"

She looked toward the city.

"Then it will wait."

The vessel slowly sailed back into the mist until only the faint shimmer of its silver threads remained visible.

The streets of Asterion stretched endlessly before him.

Every building had been carved from the same luminous white stone, smooth as polished glass yet warm beneath his fingertips. Trees lined the avenues, their branches heavy with glowing blue fruit that illuminated the streets far better than any lantern.

There was no dust.

No weeds.

No signs of decay.

The city looked as though its inhabitants had left only moments ago.

A loaf of bread still rested on a windowsill.

A child's wooden toy floated lazily in the fountain of a public square.

Fresh flowers bloomed inside homes whose doors stood wide open.

Yet there was not a single person anywhere.

The silence wasn't unsettling.

It was respectful.

Like entering a cathedral after the congregation had departed.

As Elias wandered deeper into the city, he began noticing statues.

Thousands of them.

Some stood proudly in marketplaces.

Others overlooked bridges or lined the gardens surrounding elegant courtyards.

None depicted kings or warriors.

Instead, each statue portrayed an ordinary person.

A baker kneading dough.

A mother reading to her child.

A sailor repairing a torn sail.

A musician holding an unfamiliar instrument.

Each face carried remarkable detail.

Each expression seemed alive.

Beneath every statue, the same inscription had been carved into the stone.

"Remembered."

The city eventually led him to a vast circular plaza.

At its center stood an enormous tree unlike anything he had ever imagined.

Its trunk was wider than the lighthouse in his village.

Its branches reached so high they vanished into the clouds.

Instead of leaves, thousands upon thousands of tiny drops of water hung from every branch without ever falling.

Inside each droplet, faint images moved.

A child laughing.

A storm crossing the sea.

A wedding.

A farewell.

A sunrise.

A final embrace.

Memories.

Entire lives preserved within living water.

Elias stepped closer, unable to look away.

One droplet drifted gently from the tree.

It floated toward him before settling into the palm of his hand.

The image inside became clear.

A young boy sat upon the shoulders of his father, laughing as they watched the sunset from the old lighthouse.

Elias recognized the memory instantly.

It was his own.

A memory he had long believed forgotten.

The droplet slowly dissolved into his skin.

The warmth of that evening returned so vividly that, for a brief moment, he could almost hear his father's laughter beside him.

A voice broke the silence.

"You've found the Memory Tree."

Elias turned.

An elderly woman stood at the edge of the plaza.

She wore simple white robes embroidered with silver waves. Her silver hair flowed freely over her shoulders, and her eyes reflected the same impossible blue as the deepest part of the Ocean.

Unlike everyone else he had encountered in Asterion...

She was unmistakably alive.

"I was beginning to wonder," she said with a faint smile, "how long it would take the Twenty-Seventh Captain to arrive."

She walked toward the great tree with slow, deliberate steps.

"My name is Lyra."

She gently rested her hand against the ancient trunk.

"I have been waiting for you..."

"...for one thousand years."

The wind stirred for the first time since Elias had entered the city.

High above them, one of the countless memory droplets suddenly turned black.

Then another.

And another.

Lyra looked up.

The smile vanished from her face.

"They've found Asterion."

Far beyond the city's shining walls, the sea began to darken.

Something vast was moving beneath the water.

Not toward the city.

Around it.

As if preparing to lay siege to the last place the Ocean had ever called home.

The bells of Asterion rang before the first shadow reached the city's walls.

There were no bell towers.

No ropes.

No watchmen announcing danger.

The sound simply rose from the stone itself, flowing through every street and every empty home. It was soft at first, almost comforting, but with each passing moment it deepened until the entire city seemed to breathe with it.

Elias looked toward the sea.

The water beyond the harbor had turned black.

Not with mud or storm clouds.

With absence.

It reflected neither the sky nor the sun.

It was as though light itself refused to touch it.

"What is that?" he asked.

Lyra remained silent for a long moment.

Then she answered.

"It is not the Ocean."

"It is what remains when the Ocean begins to forget."

She turned away from the harbor and motioned for Elias to follow.

"If Asterion has been found," she said, "there is very little time."

They crossed the city in silence.

As they walked, Elias noticed something strange.

The statues he had seen earlier were changing.

The baker's smile had faded.

The musician's hands were beginning to blur.

Even the inscriptions beneath them seemed less distinct.

He stopped beside one statue.

The words Remembered had become difficult to read, as though centuries of wind had worn them away in the space of a few minutes.

"They're disappearing."

Lyra nodded.

"So are the memories they hold."

"The black sea doesn't destroy cities," she explained as they continued walking.

"It destroys remembrance."

"It begins with names."

"Then faces."

"Then voices."

"Eventually..."

"...it reaches history itself."

Elias frowned.

"And when history is gone?"

She looked at him.

"How do you mourn someone you no longer remember existed?"

The question lingered between them.

For the first time since beginning his journey, Elias felt a fear greater than death.

To die was one thing.

To be forgotten completely...

...was another.

At the far end of the city stood a circular temple unlike the others.

It had no doors.

Only a wide pool of perfectly still water occupying its center.

The surface reflected not the ceiling above but an endless night sky filled with unfamiliar constellations.

Lyra stepped to the edge of the pool.

"This is the entrance."

Elias looked around.

"I don't see one."

"You won't."

She removed a small silver pendant from around her neck and let it fall into the water.

Instead of sinking, it remained floating upon the surface.

Gentle ripples spread outward.

The stars reflected in the pool rearranged themselves into the shape of a key.

Then the water slowly divided down the middle.

A staircase emerged beneath it.

Stone steps descended into darkness while walls of suspended water stood on either side, held back by a force Elias couldn't comprehend.

He looked upward.

The water should have collapsed.

It didn't.

"The Library Beneath the Tide," Lyra said softly.

"It is older than Asterion."

They descended together.

The deeper they walked, the quieter the world became.

Eventually even their footsteps made no sound.

The staircase opened into an immense chamber.

Rows upon rows of shelves stretched farther than Elias could see.

Books.

Scrolls.

Stone tablets.

Glass cylinders filled with shimmering water.

Crystal spheres.

Objects from every civilization imaginable rested carefully preserved within the library.

A man dressed in robes was writing with a feather pen at one of the tables.

He never looked up.

Nearby, a young girl carefully copied symbols into a leather journal.

She too remained focused on her work.

Dozens of people filled the library.

Some appeared ancient.

Others no older than Elias himself.

None spoke.

Only the quiet scratching of pens echoed through the chamber.

"They're alive..."

Elias whispered.

"Some of them."

Lyra smiled sadly.

"Others have been dead for centuries."

Elias stared at her.

She simply continued walking.

An old man approached them.

His beard reached almost to the floor, and tiny shells were woven into his silver hair.

He bowed politely.

"You've brought him."

"I have."

"The Captain?"

"The Twenty-Seventh."

The old man's tired eyes settled upon Elias.

"I hoped you would arrive sooner."

"I'm sorry?"

He shook his head.

"Not your fault."

"We've merely run out of centuries."

Without another word, the old librarian led them through endless corridors until they reached a single pedestal.

Unlike every other shelf in the library, this one held only one object.

A thin blue book.

Its cover bore no title.

No author.

Only a familiar symbol.

The same symbol engraved upon the Drowned Compass.

Elias instinctively reached toward it.

The moment his fingers touched the cover...

The entire library trembled.

Every candle extinguished.

Every page in every book turned simultaneously.

A powerful wind rushed through the chamber despite the absence of doors or windows.

The old librarian closed his eyes.

"So..."

"It's finally chosen."

The book opened by itself.

Its pages were blank.

Then words slowly appeared.

Not ink.

Light.

The first sentence read:

If you are reading this, then the Ocean has already failed.

Elias's heartbeat quickened.

The next line appeared.

My name is Caelum.

Another.

I built the Three Thrones.

The room fell completely silent.

Even Lyra seemed unable to breathe.

The final line emerged slowly across the page.

Everything you have been told is only half of the truth.

Before Elias could read another word, the library shook violently.

A deafening crack echoed overhead.

One of the walls of suspended water burst inward.

Not with water.

With darkness.

It poured into the library like liquid night, swallowing shelves, books, and memories in absolute silence.

The old librarian whispered a prayer.

Lyra stepped protectively in front of Elias.

But her eyes were fixed on the spreading darkness.

"It came faster than I feared."

The darkness stopped only a few feet away.

Its surface rippled.

A human shape slowly emerged from within it.

Not the Tide King.

Not the Keeper.

Not the Silent Current.

Someone else.

A young woman dressed in black robes, carrying a lantern whose flame burned without light.

She looked directly at Elias.

Then smiled.

"You've been reading lies, Captain."

She gently closed the blue book with one hand.

"My father..."

She glanced at the name Caelum on its cover.

"...was never trying to save the world."

The library fell into stunned silence.

"He was trying to escape it."

No one moved.

The woman stood quietly at the edge of the spreading darkness, her black robes untouched by it, as though the shadows recognized her as one of their own. The lantern in her hand gave off no light, yet the darkness around her seemed to bend away from its invisible flame.

Elias looked from the woman to the blue book lying on the pedestal.

"You... know Caelum?"

A faint smile touched her lips.

"I should."

"He was my father."

The old librarian staggered backward.

Lyra caught his arm before he fell.

"No," the old man whispered. "That's impossible."

"It has been over a thousand years."

The woman nodded.

"It has."

"And yet..."

She looked down at her own hands.

"...here I am."

Elias stepped forward cautiously.

"My father told me Caelum built the Three Thrones."

"He did."

"The Keeper said they imprisoned the Ocean."

"They did."

"The Tide King said the Thrones were prisons."

"They were."

Elias frowned.

"Then all of them were telling the truth."

"They were."

"But not the whole truth."

The woman walked slowly toward the pedestal.

With every step she took, the darkness behind her remained perfectly still.

It did not consume the library.

It waited.

Almost obediently.

"My name is Neris."

She rested her fingertips on the cover of the blue book.

"When history becomes too complicated..."

"...people shorten it."

"They remove the uncomfortable parts."

"They turn choices into legends."

"And eventually..."

"...they mistake legends for truth."

She opened the book.

The pages filled with moving images instead of words.

Elias watched as an endless blue ocean stretched across the page.

There was no land.

No mountains.

Only water beneath an endless sky.

"The world before humanity," Neris said.

A single figure appeared on the page.

Caelum.

Young.

Standing upon the sea as though it were solid ground.

"He wasn't a king."

"He wasn't a god."

"He was an explorer."

The image changed.

Caelum sailed farther than anyone before him.

He crossed storms.

He crossed frozen seas.

He crossed waters where even the stars looked unfamiliar.

Finally...

He reached something impossible.

The Ocean ended.

Not at a shore.

At a wall.

An endless curtain of shimmering water rising into the sky itself.

Elias stared.

"The Ocean..."

"...had an edge?"

Neris nodded.

"Everything does."

The old librarian spoke for the first time.

"We searched for that place."

"We never found it."

"You weren't meant to."

Neris gently turned another page.

The image continued.

Caelum stepped through the wall of water.

Beyond it...

...was darkness.

Not empty darkness.

Living darkness.

Countless stars drifted through it like glowing islands.

The Ocean had not ended.

It had flowed into something infinitely larger.

A cosmic sea.

Where worlds floated like ships.

Elias felt his breath catch.

"The map..."

"The Compass showed stars."

"Not oceans."

Neris smiled.

"Because the Compass never belonged to this world."

She closed the book.

"It belonged to the Navigators."

"Who were they?"

"The first people to sail between worlds."

Silence filled the library.

Even Lyra seemed unable to process what she had heard.

"The Last Ocean wasn't the last ocean."

Neris continued.

"It was the first harbor."

"A gateway."

"A place where worlds met."

Elias's thoughts raced.

"Then why seal it?"

"Why build the Thrones?"

Neris's expression grew distant.

"Because Caelum made a mistake."

"The greatest explorer in history discovered that the sea connected countless worlds."

She paused.

"He also discovered..."

"...that something else used those same waters."

The darkness behind her rippled.

For the first time, Elias noticed shapes moving within it.

Not monsters.

Silhouettes.

Entire cities drifting beneath black waves.

Ships larger than mountains.

Creatures with wings made of starlight.

All barely visible before disappearing again.

Neris looked back at the darkness.

"They followed him home."

Lyra whispered,

"So the Ocean wasn't imprisoned..."

Neris nodded.

"It was hidden."

"The Thrones didn't chain the Ocean."

"They closed the harbor."

"They locked the door."

The realization struck Elias all at once.

"The Ocean is dying because..."

"...the door has remained closed for too long."

"Yes."

"And if we open it..."

"The Ocean will live again."

"But..."

Neris looked directly into his eyes.

"So will everything waiting beyond it."

Before anyone could speak again, the invisible flame inside Neris's lantern flickered violently.

Her calm expression disappeared.

"They're here."

The darkness throughout the library suddenly retreated, flowing back toward the broken wall as though frightened.

A deep horn echoed from somewhere above Asterion.

Not once.

Three times.

The old librarian's face drained of color.

"That's impossible."

Lyra looked toward the ceiling.

"The city shields..."

"They've fallen."

Outside, distant screams echoed through Asterion for the first time in a thousand years.

Neris closed the blue book and placed it into Elias's hands.

"Whatever happens..."

"...don't let them take this."

"Who?"

She looked upward.

Not with fear.

With recognition.

Then she whispered a single name.

"The First Navigator has returned."

At that exact moment, every memory droplet hanging from the great tree above the city shattered.

And for the first time in history...

Someone knocked on the gates of Asterion from the other side of the world.

The knock echoed through Asterion like the heartbeat of a giant.

Once.

Then again.

The sound was neither loud nor violent, yet every stone in the city seemed to recognize it.

The library fell silent.

The scribes stopped writing.

The old librarian slowly closed the book he had been holding.

Even the suspended walls of water trembled.

Elias looked toward Lyra.

"Who's at the gate?"

She didn't answer.

Her eyes had already filled with a fear he had never seen before.

They hurried back through the flooded corridors.

The great staircase rose before them, and as they climbed, the city's bells continued their slow, mournful rhythm.

By the time they reached the streets, Asterion had changed.

The warm blue glow that had bathed the city since their arrival was fading.

One by one, the crystal lamps lining the roads dimmed.

The fruit hanging from the silver trees lost their light.

The fountains no longer flowed upward.

It was as though the city itself had grown tired.

People had appeared.

Not many.

Perhaps a few hundred.

Men and women dressed in white robes emerged from homes Elias had believed abandoned. Some carried children. Others held ancient scrolls or musical instruments as though they had been interrupted in the middle of ordinary lives.

None of them were running.

None of them were shouting.

They simply walked toward the western wall in solemn silence.

An old woman gently closed the door to her house before joining the others.

A young boy placed a carved wooden bird upon his windowsill and smiled at it one last time.

A baker wiped flour from his hands before locking his shop.

Elias frowned.

"They knew this day would come."

Lyra nodded.

"They've been preparing for it for a thousand years."

The western gate stood at the edge of the city.

It wasn't built of iron or stone.

It was formed from two immense arches of crystal water that flowed upward into the sky before meeting overhead.

Beyond the gate stretched only endless sea.

No ships.

No islands.

Nothing.

Yet every citizen of Asterion had gathered there.

Waiting.

The Keeper stood among them.

So did the Tide King.

Neither looked surprised to see Elias.

The old enemies stood side by side without speaking.

For the first time since meeting them, they seemed united by something greater than their disagreement.

Another knock.

This time, cracks of silver light spread across the watery arch.

The citizens lowered their heads.

Someone began quietly singing.

Within moments, hundreds of voices joined together.

The melody was unlike anything Elias had ever heard.

It sounded ancient.

Older than language.

The Ocean answered.

Gentle waves rolled toward the shore, matching the rhythm of the song.

Elias turned to Arlen.

"Who are they singing for?"

His father watched the gate without blinking.

"Not for."

He swallowed.

"Against."

The watery gate slowly became transparent.

A figure appeared beyond it.

Not standing on the sea.

Standing in darkness.

An endless black ocean stretched behind him, filled with distant stars.

He wore a simple traveler's cloak, faded by impossible journeys.

No armor.

No crown.

A leather satchel hung over one shoulder.

His boots were covered in silver dust instead of sand.

At his belt rested an old compass.

Its design was simpler than Elias's.

Older.

The man looked utterly ordinary.

Until he smiled.

It was the smile of someone who had seen worlds rise and fall and still found wonder in a sunrise.

He raised one hand in greeting.

"Good evening."

His voice passed effortlessly through the sealed gate.

"I apologize for arriving unannounced."

No one answered.

The man sighed.

"I suspected you still held a grudge."

He looked across the gathered crowd until his eyes settled on Elias.

For a long moment, neither of them spoke.

Then the traveler smiled again.

"So."

"You finally became Captain."

Elias felt a chill run through him.

"You know me?"

"I knew your grandfather."

The answer made no sense.

"My grandfather died before I was born."

The traveler nodded.

"He did."

"He was very stubborn."

Neris stepped forward.

Her expression had hardened.

"You shouldn't have come back."

The traveler looked at her with unmistakable affection.

"My daughter."

"It has been far too long."

Neris didn't move.

"You lost the right to call me that."

A flicker of sadness crossed the man's face.

"Perhaps."

Elias stared.

His mind struggled to keep up.

"This is..."

Neris answered quietly.

"Caelum."

The name spread through the crowd like a wave.

Some citizens wept.

Others bowed.

The Keeper closed his eyes.

The Tide King tightened his grip on his trident.

The man beyond the gate simply stood there, looking almost embarrassed by the reaction.

Caelum placed his hand gently against the barrier of living water.

"I never wanted to leave you."

"No?" Neris replied.

"You abandoned an entire world."

"I tried to save it."

"You tried to save yourself."

The words hung in the air.

Caelum lowered his gaze.

For the first time, Elias saw regret in the old explorer's face.

Real regret.

Not the polished sorrow of legends.

The quiet regret of a man who had lived with one decision for longer than anyone should.

"I made a choice," Caelum said softly.

"I believed that if I sealed the harbor..."

"...the darkness would never find this world."

He looked toward Elias.

"I was wrong."

"The darkness didn't stop searching."

"It simply had more time."

The watery gate shuddered violently.

A new crack spread across its surface.

This one did not glow silver.

It bled black.

Behind Caelum, shapes moved beneath the cosmic sea.

Massive.

Countless.

Patient.

Their outlines were impossible to comprehend.

Some resembled ships.

Others resembled living storms.

One unfolded wings large enough to eclipse entire constellations.

They had been waiting.

Not for centuries.

For millennia.

Caelum turned slowly toward the darkness behind him.

His calm smile disappeared.

When he looked back at Elias, his voice was almost a whisper.

"They've crossed the threshold."

The gate screamed.

A sound like breaking glass echoed across Asterion.

The first black hand reached through the crack.

It wasn't made of flesh.

It was made of flowing night.

Its fingers touched the waters of the Last Ocean...

And everywhere they met...

The sea forgot how to shine.The black hand rested upon the surface of the Ocean.

There was no explosion.

No towering wave.

No flash of light.

Instead, the water beneath its fingers slowly lost its color.

The brilliant blue faded into dull gray.

Fish floating beneath the surface simply... disappeared. Not dead. Not consumed. They vanished as though they had never existed.

A sailor standing on the city wall frowned.

"There were fish there."

His wife looked at the empty water.

"What fish?"

The sailor hesitated.

For the first time in his life, he could no longer remember.

Elias felt the blue book grow warm beneath his arm.

The Drowned Compass, hanging around his neck, began spinning wildly.

Its needle pointed neither toward the city nor the darkness.

It pointed upward.

To the sky.

He looked up.

The stars had changed.

Constellations he had known since childhood were gone.

New ones appeared in their place.

It was as if the heavens themselves were rewriting their map.

Caelum noticed.

"It has begun."

"What has?"

"The crossing."

The black hand slowly withdrew.

It had not attacked.

It had only touched the world.

Yet that single touch had changed something fundamental.

The Memory Tree in the center of Asterion stood silent.

Every droplet hanging from its branches had turned perfectly clear.

No memories remained inside them.

Children looked at their parents with uncertainty.

Old friends searched one another's faces, trying to recall names that had been familiar only moments before.

The city was beginning to forget itself.

Caelum closed his eyes.

"I caused this."

"No."

The voice came from behind Elias.

It was Lyra.

She stepped beside the old Navigator.

"You delayed it."

"For a thousand years."

Caelum smiled sadly.

"I always appreciated your kindness."

"It isn't kindness."

"It's the truth."

She looked toward the black sea.

"But delay isn't victory."

The Keeper approached Elias.

His robes were torn, and dried blood stained one sleeve.

For the first time, he no longer looked like a guardian.

He looked like an old man who had carried an impossible burden for far too long.

"The time for choosing has ended."

Elias looked at him.

"What do I do?"

The Keeper didn't answer.

Instead, he gently placed his hand over the Drowned Compass.

"It was never meant to guide you."

"It was meant..."

"...to prepare you."

The Tide King walked forward next.

He removed his black coral helmet.

No one had ever seen his face.

Elias stared.

The man beneath the armor looked ordinary.

Weathered skin.

Gray eyes.

A tired smile.

He wasn't a monster.

He was simply another captain who had survived too long.

"I spent centuries believing I had to destroy the Thrones."

He looked toward the sea.

"I was wrong."

He then looked toward the Keeper.

"You spent centuries believing you had to restore them."

The Keeper nodded.

"I was wrong too."

Both men laughed softly.

Not because anything was funny.

Because after centuries of conflict, they finally understood they had been asking the wrong question.

Elias frowned.

"If not restore them..."

"...and not destroy them..."

"Then what?"

Caelum answered.

"Finish them."

Everyone turned.

The old explorer stepped through the fractured gate.

For the first time in a thousand years, his boots touched the world he had left behind.

The city bells rang once.

Not in warning.

In welcome.

Caelum walked to the center of the gathering.

"There were never supposed to be Three Thrones."

"There were supposed to be Four."

Silence.

Neris slowly lowered her lantern.

"The Fourth..."

She whispered.

"You hid it."

Caelum nodded.

"I hid it because it required something no one would willingly give."

He looked directly at Elias.

"The First Throne remembers."

"The Second judges."

"The Third opens the Harbor."

He paused.

"The Fourth..."

"...chooses."

"The Fourth Throne has no king."

"It has no guardian."

"It has no crown."

"It only asks one question."

Elias already knew.

He could feel it.

Caelum stepped closer.

"The Ocean doesn't need someone to rule it."

"It needs someone willing to become part of it."

The realization struck Elias with terrifying clarity.

"You mean..."

"If I choose the Fourth Throne..."

"I won't come back."

Caelum didn't speak.

He simply nodded.

Arlen stepped forward immediately.

"No."

His voice broke.

"There has to be another way."

"There isn't."

"I'm his father."

"I won't let him—"

Caelum gently interrupted.

"I know."

"I once said the same words."

Arlen fell silent.

Elias looked around him.

The Keeper.

The Tide King.

Lyra.

Neris.

His father.

The people of Asterion.

Every one of them was waiting.

Not because they expected him to save them.

Because only he could answer the final question.

He closed his eyes.

He remembered the lighthouse.

His mother's laughter.

The first time he held the Compass.

The Starling waiting beneath the cliffs.

Every step had led here.

Not to find the Ocean's last secret.

But to become it.

Far out at sea, beneath the darkening horizon, the Fourth Throne slowly rose.

It wasn't carved from coral.

Or ice.

Or stone.

It was made entirely of living water.

Transparent.

Beautiful.

Waiting.

The Ocean itself seemed to bow before it.

Elias took one step toward the shore.

Then another.

Behind him, no one tried to stop him.

Some journeys could not be shared.

As he reached the water's edge, the sea parted into a shimmering path leading directly to the waiting throne.

The Drowned Compass floated gently from his neck.

It opened like a flower.

Inside, where a needle had once rested, there was only a single line engraved in silver.

"Every Ocean is remembered by the one who chooses to carry it."

Elias smiled.

Not because he wasn't afraid.

But because, for the first time since his journey began...

He understood.

And he walked toward the Fourth Throne as dawn broke over both the Last Ocean...

...and the first morning of a new age.
