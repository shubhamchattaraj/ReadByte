The old lighthouse had not shone for nearly sixty years.

Still, every evening, as the sky melted into crimson and the sea swallowed the horizon, Elias climbed its broken spiral staircase with a brass lantern in his hand.

Not because anyone expected ships.

Because his grandfather had always said,

"The day no one lights the tower is the day the Ocean remembers us."

Nobody in the fishing village believed those words anymore.

The Last Ocean was dying.

Its waters were strangely calm.

Too calm.

No storms.

No whales.

No dolphins.

Not even gulls flew above it anymore.

It was as though the sea itself had forgotten how to breathe.

The fishermen returned each morning with empty nets, soaked not in saltwater but in a thin layer of black sand that clung to everything it touched.

Children stopped learning to swim.

Boats rotted at their docks.

The tide no longer followed the moon.

It arrived whenever it wished.

Sometimes twice in a day.

Sometimes not for weeks.

No one questioned it.

People simply learned not to ask questions the Ocean refused to answer.

Elias was twenty-three.

His father had disappeared twelve years ago while searching for something everyone called a myth.

The Drowned Compass.

According to village legends, it wasn't a compass that pointed north.

It pointed toward what the Ocean wanted someone to find.

His father never returned.

Only his boat drifted home.

Perfectly dry.

Covered in coral that belonged to waters deeper than any map claimed existed.

Since then, Elias had inherited the lighthouse.

And every sunset, he lit it.

Even if no ships came.

That evening felt different.

The wind vanished.

Not slowed.

Vanished.

The waves froze in place as though someone had painted them.

The sea became glass.

Then...

A bell rang beneath the water.

Not above.

Below.

One.

Two.

Three.

The sound echoed through the cliffs.

Every villager closed their doors.

Candles were extinguished.

Windows locked.

No one looked outside.

Everyone knew the rule.

Never answer bells from the sea.

Only Elias remained on the cliff.

Another bell.

Closer.

The lantern flickered.

Then the Ocean moved.

Not with waves.

With footsteps.

Someone was walking beneath the surface.

A silhouette crossed the water.

A human figure.

Impossible.

Each step produced circles of pale blue light.

The figure stopped exactly below the lighthouse.

It looked up.

Elias couldn't see a face.

Only darkness beneath a hood woven from seaweed.

Then...

The figure slowly raised one hand.

Holding something.

Metal.

Round.

Covered in shells.

A compass.

Before Elias could react, the sea exploded upward.

Water climbed like giant fingers toward the sky before crashing back into silence.

The figure was gone.

But something floated against the rocks.

He climbed down the cliff before sunrise.

The object lay tangled in kelp.

Heavy.

Cold.

Older than memory itself.

A bronze compass no larger than his palm.

Its glass wasn't cracked despite centuries beneath the sea.

Its needle spun wildly.

Not north.

Not south.

It pointed directly toward Elias.

He frowned.

Impossible.

He turned around.

The needle turned with him.

Always pointing at his chest.

Then words appeared beneath the glass.

Letters forming one by one as droplets rolled across its surface.

"The Ocean has chosen its final keeper."

The needle stopped.

For the first time in centuries.

Far beyond the horizon...

Something enormous awakened beneath the water.

Not seen.

Only felt.

The sea rose by a single inch.

Every lighthouse along the coast—abandoned for generations—lit themselves at the exact same moment.

And deep beneath the Last Ocean...

Something ancient opened its eyes.

By sunrise, the entire village knew.

Not because Elias had spoken.

Because every lighthouse on the coast still burned.

For generations they had stood empty—crumbling towers claimed by vines and salt—but now, each beacon shone with the same pale blue flame.

No one dared approach them.

The elders gathered in the village square, whispering prayers that had not been spoken in decades.

Children were ordered indoors.

Boats were dragged farther onto the shore.

Even the oldest fishermen refused to look at the sea.

"The Ocean has begun counting again," one old woman murmured.

No one asked what she meant.

Elias wrapped the strange compass in a piece of cloth and hid it beneath his coat.

It felt warm now.

Almost alive.

Every few minutes it pulsed once, like a heartbeat.

His first instinct was to throw it back into the sea.

His second was to discover why his father had died searching for it.

The second instinct won.

There was only one person who might know anything.

The Cartographer.

North of the village, hidden among cliffs where the fog never lifted, stood a lonely stone house.

Its owner had not sailed in forty years.

His name was Corvin Ash.

Children called him "the man who drew places that didn't exist."

Once he had been the Royal Cartographer.

The greatest mapmaker alive.

Then, during an expedition into the Last Ocean, he returned alone.

His maps were confiscated.

His crew vanished from every official record.

He never spoke of the voyage again.

Now he lived surrounded by thousands of maps.

None of them matched the world.

Elias found the old man bent over a wooden table, drawing coastlines that curved like spirals.

Without looking up, Corvin said,

"You shouldn't have brought it here."

Elias froze.

"I haven't shown you anything."

"You didn't need to."

The old man's charcoal stopped moving.

"The compass announces itself."

Silence filled the room.

Finally, Elias placed the bronze compass on the table.

Corvin stared at it.

His weathered face lost all color.

"It returned..."

He whispered the words as if speaking to a ghost.

"You know what this is?"

Corvin nodded slowly.

"No."

Elias frowned.

"You just said—"

"I know what people call it."

He carefully reached toward the compass.

The moment his fingers touched the bronze, the needle began spinning violently.

The room darkened.

Every map hanging on the walls fluttered without wind.

Then...

Ink began moving.

Rivers shifted.

Mountains walked across parchment.

Entire continents slid into new positions.

Elias stepped backward.

"What is happening?"

Corvin didn't answer.

His eyes were fixed on a single map.

One that had been completely blank for years.

Blue lines slowly spread across it.

An ocean appeared.

Then islands.

Then a city beneath the sea.

At the center of the map, three words wrote themselves.

THE FIRST THRONE

The old man snatched his hand away.

Everything stopped.

The maps became ordinary once more.

Corvin locked every door.

Then every window.

Then he lit seven candles in a circle around the table.

Only then did he speak.

"You've been lied to your entire life."

Elias folded his arms.

"By who?"

"Everyone."

"The Ocean wasn't always called the Last Ocean."

"What was it called?"

Corvin looked toward the sea beyond his window.

"No one remembers."

"Not because the name was forgotten."

"Because the Ocean erased it."

He opened an old chest.

Inside lay dozens of journals.

Salt-stained.

Half-rotted.

Each belonged to a different expedition.

Different captains.

Different centuries.

Yet every final page contained the exact same sentence.

"Do not let it find the Compass."

No signatures.

No explanations.

Just those six words.

Corvin handed Elias one final journal.

Unlike the others, its leather cover was familiar.

His heart nearly stopped.

Embossed on the front were the initials:

A. M.

Arlen Marris.

His father.

Hands trembling, Elias opened it.

Every page had been torn out.

Except the last.

There, in his father's unmistakable handwriting, were only two lines:

If you're reading this, I failed.

Never trust the Keeper of the Tide.

Before Elias could ask who that was, a deafening crack echoed outside.

The house shook violently.

Books crashed from their shelves.

The candles extinguished all at once.

Corvin rushed to the window.

His face turned pale.

"They found you."

"Who?"

The old man pointed toward the shoreline.

Hundreds of figures stood motionless in the shallow water.

Men.

Women.

Children.

Their clothes were centuries out of date.

Their skin glistened with seawater.

Their eyes reflected no light.

They did not blink.

They did not breathe.

And every one of them was staring directly at Elias.

Then, in perfect unison...

They took one step onto dry land.

The first foot touched the shore.

The village bell rang.

Not because anyone pulled the rope.

Because the tower shook on its own.

Once.

Twice.

Then silence.

The figures did not rush forward.

They walked.

Slowly.

As though they had all the time in the world.

Each left wet footprints that shimmered silver before fading into the earth.

Corvin pulled Elias away from the window.

"We have to leave."

"Who are they?"

The old cartographer hesitated.

"They were sailors."

"Were?"

"They drowned."

Elias stared.

"They don't look dead."

"They aren't."

The answer chilled him more than if Corvin had said they were ghosts.

The back door led to a narrow path climbing the cliffs.

As they fled, the old man grabbed a weathered satchel.

Inside were only three things:

A folded map with no markings.
A brass key shaped like a wave.
A telescope whose glass was filled with swirling seawater instead of lenses.

"You packed those first?" Elias asked.

"They're the only things worth saving."

Behind them, the front door creaked open.

No one had knocked.

No one needed to.

They climbed until they reached an abandoned watchtower overlooking the bay.

From there, the village appeared strangely peaceful.

Smoke still rose from chimneys.

Fishing nets fluttered in the breeze.

Yet not a single person stood outside.

The tidebound walked silently through the empty streets.

None entered the houses.

Instead, each stopped before a different door.

Waiting.

As if expecting someone to answer.

One child among them looked no older than eight.

She held a wooden toy boat against her chest.

Her clothes belonged to another century.

She stared at one cottage and whispered something.

The wind carried only a single word.

"Mother."

Corvin lowered the telescope.

"They're searching."

"For what?"

"For home."

"I don't understand."

"They remember where they belonged."

"They don't remember why they drowned."

Elias looked again.

"They're harmless?"

"For now."

The old man didn't sound convinced.

That night they sheltered inside the ruined tower.

Elias finally opened his father's journal again.

Only two lines remained.

He turned the pages once more.

Blank.

Until a drop of seawater from his sleeve landed on the paper.

Ink bloomed across the empty page like dark flowers.

More words appeared.

Not written.

Revealed.

The Compass does not point to places.

It points to promises.

Another drop fell.

A sketch emerged beneath the words.

Three crowns.

Connected by waves.

Below them were names.

Throne of the Tides

Crown of Storms

The Last Secret

Corvin's breathing became shallow.

"The Three Thrones..."

"You know them?"

"They're older than kingdoms."

"They ruled the Ocean before humans learned to sail."

Elias frowned.

"You're speaking as if the Ocean was alive."

Corvin looked at him with tired eyes.

"It is."

Near midnight, the compass began to hum.

Its needle spun wildly.

Then settled.

Not toward the sea.

Not toward the cliffs.

Toward the tower floor.

Directly beneath their feet.

Elias knelt and brushed away centuries of dust.

Stone.

Cracked stone.

In its center was a symbol identical to the one engraved on the compass.

He pushed.

Nothing happened.

Corvin inserted the wave-shaped brass key into a tiny hole hidden in the carving.

A deep click echoed underground.

The floor split apart.

Cold air rushed upward carrying the scent of salt, ancient wood, and something else...

Fresh rain.

Impossible.

It hadn't rained here in years.

A spiral staircase descended into darkness.

Torch brackets along the walls ignited by themselves with pale blue fire.

Corvin whispered, almost reverently,

"I prayed this place had been destroyed."

"What is it?"

"The First Waystation."

"The beginning of every voyage."

They descended.

The staircase ended in a vast underground chamber carved directly into the bedrock.

The ceiling disappeared into darkness.

Rows upon rows of ancient ships rested in perfectly still water beneath the earth.

Not wrecks.

Ships.

Hundreds of them.

Their sails untouched by time.

Their hulls free of rot.

Some were made of oak.

Others of black stone.

One appeared to be carved entirely from white bone.

None were tied to docks.

Yet none drifted.

They waited.

As though expecting captains who had merely stepped away.

At the center of the cavern floated a single vessel unlike the others.

Smaller.

Elegant.

Its mast bore no sail.

Instead, countless strands of silver thread stretched upward into the darkness like the strings of a celestial instrument.

Across its bow, letters slowly emerged in glowing blue:

THE STARLING

The compass in Elias's hand pulsed once.

Then twice.

The ship answered.

Its bell rang.

From somewhere deep within the cavern, another bell answered.

Then another.

And another.

One by one, every ancient ship in the hidden harbor began to ring, until the chamber echoed with a chorus that had not been heard for a thousand years.

Far above them, out in the Last Ocean, the water began to rise—not in waves, but as if the sea itself had stood up to listen.

The bells ceased all at once.

The silence that followed felt heavier than the stone above them.

Elias stood frozen at the edge of the underground harbor.

Hundreds of ships floated without a ripple, their reflections perfectly still, as if the water beneath them was polished glass instead of an underground sea.

Only one vessel moved.

The Starling.

Without wind or oars, it drifted toward the dock where Elias stood.

Its silver threads shimmered softly, humming with a melody too ancient to remember and too beautiful to forget.

It stopped exactly an arm's length away.

A narrow gangplank unfolded by itself.

Corvin didn't move.

"I cannot board."

"Why not?"

"The ship won't allow it."

"How do you know?"

The old man slowly rolled up his sleeve.

Across his forearm was a faded blue mark.

A circle crossed by three waves.

"The Starling chooses only one captain."

Elias stepped onto the gangplank.

The moment his foot touched the deck, every lantern aboard ignited.

Warm golden light spread across the ship.

The polished wood looked freshly crafted despite its impossible age.

There were no cobwebs.

No dust.

No decay.

Someone—or something—had been caring for the vessel.

The deck was strangely empty.

No ropes.

No sails.

No anchor.

Instead, dozens of tiny silver stars were embedded into the planks, forming constellations beneath his feet.

As he walked, they shifted.

Not randomly.

They rearranged themselves into a path leading toward the captain's cabin.

Inside the cabin, nothing resembled an ordinary ship.

There was no wheel.

No compass stand.

No maps.

The room was circular, lined with shelves filled with glass bottles.

Each contained seawater from a different shade of blue.

Some glowed green.

Others shimmered black.

One bottle held water as clear as crystal, yet tiny stars floated inside it.

At the center stood a pedestal.

Upon it rested a leather-bound logbook.

Its pages were blank.

When Elias opened it, words slowly appeared.

Captain Accepted.

Below that, another sentence wrote itself.

State your name.

He hesitated.

Then spoke aloud.

"Elias Marris."

Ink flowed across the page.

Captain Elias Marris.

Twenty-Seventh Keeper of the Starling.

His heartbeat quickened.

Twenty-seven?

"So there were twenty-six before me..."

The pages turned on their own.

One name after another appeared.

Some lasted decades.

Some only days.

Many entries ended with a single word:

Lost.

A few ended with:

Returned.

Only one name was followed by a different fate.

Captain Arlen Marris.

His father.

Instead of "Lost," his page read:

Awaiting Judgment.

Elias's hands trembled.

"He's alive..."

A soft voice answered behind him.

"Perhaps."

He spun around.

A woman stood near the doorway.

She wore a coat woven from feathers that shimmered like fish scales.

Her hair was silver, though her face looked no older than thirty.

Most unsettling were her eyes.

One reflected the sunrise.

The other reflected the night sky.

She smiled gently.

"You've taken your father's ship."

Elias instinctively reached for a nearby harpoon.

She laughed.

"It won't harm me."

"Who are you?"

"I have been many things."

She bowed slightly.

"Most recently..."

"...the Shipkeeper."

"You live here?"

"I wait here."

"For me?"

"For every captain."

Corvin entered cautiously, remaining near the door.

His expression hardened.

"I thought you were dead."

"So did I," she replied.

"Then who are you really?"

She looked at Elias instead.

"My name no longer matters."

"Names are anchors."

"I cut mine loose long ago."

She led them below deck.

Instead of cargo holds, the lower levels contained impossible things.

A room where rain fell upward into the ceiling.

A library whose books whispered to each other.

An orchard growing beneath the sea, its trees bearing glowing blue fruit.

A hallway whose windows looked not into the cavern outside—but into distant oceans under unfamiliar constellations.

Elias stopped before one window.

He saw enormous cities beneath the waves.

People walked through streets of coral inside shimmering bubbles.

Children chased schools of glowing fish.

Above them floated three colossal thrones, carved from living stone and covered in flowing water that never spilled.

One throne was empty.

One was cracked.

The third was occupied.

A figure cloaked in darkness slowly lifted its head.

Though separated by impossible distance, it turned directly toward Elias.

Its eyes opened.

The window shattered.

Water burst into the hallway—but instead of flooding the ship, it hung suspended in the air like countless floating mirrors.

Within every droplet, Elias saw the same face.

His own.

Older.

Wounded.

Wearing a crown made of coral and black pearls.

The Shipkeeper whispered,

"The Ocean has shown you one possible ending."

"Is it my future?"

"No."

"It is the future the Ocean prefers."

Before Elias could ask another question, the Starling lurched violently.

A deep horn echoed through the underground harbor.

Not from the ship.

From somewhere beyond the cavern.

Corvin rushed to the deck.

"The entrance..."

His voice faltered.

"The sea has found us."

The cavern wall began to crack.

Not from pressure—

From something pushing its way in.

Through the widening fissure emerged an enormous eye, larger than the Starling itself.

Its iris glowed the same pale blue as the compass.

It blinked once.

Then a voice, deep enough to shake the stone foundations of the harbor, spoke a single sentence:

"Return what was stolen... or the Last Ocean will drown the world one memory at a time."

The eye did not blink again.

It simply watched.

Ancient.

Patient.

As though centuries were no more than moments to it.

The cavern trembled with each slow breath the unseen creature took beyond the stone.

The Starling rocked gently, though the underground water remained perfectly still.

No one spoke.

The voice had not echoed through the air.

It had echoed through memory itself.

Elias suddenly remembered his mother singing a lullaby he hadn't heard since childhood.

Corvin remembered the face of his wife, long forgotten.

The Shipkeeper closed her eyes, tears silently rolling down her cheeks.

The creature wasn't merely speaking.

It was awakening memories.

"Who are you?" Elias called toward the enormous eye.

For several long seconds, nothing happened.

Then the voice returned.

"I..."

"...am what remains."

The words carried the weight of continents.

"I was called many names."

"The First Sea."

"The Endless Tide."

"The Mother Current."

"The Last Ocean is only the wound left behind."

The eye slowly closed.

"When the Three Thrones were broken..."

"...my heart was divided."

The cavern darkened.

"Find them."

"Before the one who sits upon the Broken Throne finds you."

The eye withdrew into the darkness.

The fissure in the cavern wall slowly sealed itself, leaving only silence behind.

No one moved for a long while.

Finally, Corvin whispered,

"The legends were wrong."

"They always are," the Shipkeeper replied.

"Legends remember victories."

"They forget the price."

Back aboard the Starling, the captain's log opened on its own.

Fresh ink flowed across the page.

Voyage One Initiated.

Destination Revealed.

A map slowly appeared where blank parchment had been only moments before.

At first, it showed the familiar coastline.

Then the sea unfolded.

Hidden islands emerged.

Ancient currents twisted across the page like veins.

Far to the east, a single island appeared where no island had existed before.

Above it were written three words.

The Isle of Echoes.

Beneath the name, another line formed.

The First Throne waits.

Corvin frowned.

"I've sailed these waters my entire life."

"That island doesn't exist."

"It does now," said the Shipkeeper.

"The Compass never reveals a destination before its captain is ready."

She placed a weathered wooden chest on the captain's table.

Inside lay three objects.

A sword whose blade looked like frozen seawater.

A black spyglass wrapped in silver wire.

And a small leather pouch.

Elias opened the pouch.

Inside were six strange coins.

Each was made from a different metal.

Gold.

Silver.

Copper.

Obsidian.

Pearl.

And one that seemed to be made from solid moonlight.

"They're not money," the Shipkeeper explained.

"They're promises."

"You'll understand when it's time to spend them."

As sunset approached, the hidden harbor began to change.

The ancient ships that had rested there for centuries slowly drifted backward into the darkness, disappearing one by one into tunnels beneath the water.

Only the Starling remained.

Waiting.

Corvin stood at the dock.

"I can't go with you."

"I know."

"My place is here."

He handed Elias the map he had carried all these years.

It was still blank.

"Maps don't only record places," he said.

"They record the people who survive them."

"When you return..."

"...this map will tell your story."

The Shipkeeper untied the Starling's final rope.

No wind filled the empty mast.

Instead, the silver threads above the ship stretched toward the cavern ceiling like strands reaching for the stars.

The underground sea began to glow.

The hidden harbor opened.

Stone walls slid apart, revealing a narrow passage leading directly into the open ocean.

The Starling sailed forward without sails, without oars, guided only by the Compass.

As the ship emerged beneath the moonlight, Elias looked back.

The entrance to the harbor vanished.

The cliffs became ordinary stone once more.

No trace of the secret harbor remained.

Hours later, the coastline had disappeared behind them.

The Last Ocean was endless in every direction.

The sea was unnaturally calm.

Then the Compass spun.

Once.

Twice.

It stopped.

Straight ahead.

The water rippled.

A bell rang beneath the waves.

Another answered.

Then dozens more.

The ocean surface bulged upward.

Slowly.

Silently.

An entire island rose from beneath the sea, shedding waterfalls from cliffs that had not seen sunlight in a thousand years.

At its summit stood a colossal throne carved from white coral.

It was empty.

But someone had been there only moments before.

Fresh footprints crossed the stone steps.

They led into a forest of towering blue trees.

And waiting at the edge of that forest stood a lone figure in a cloak of dark kelp, holding another ancient compass.

The stranger smiled.

Then said the one sentence Elias had hoped never to hear:

"I've been waiting for the son of Arlen Marris."
