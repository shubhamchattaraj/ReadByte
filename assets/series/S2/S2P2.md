The Starling glided toward the newly risen island without a single sail unfurling. The sea around it remained unnaturally smooth, as though the water itself feared disturbing the silence. Behind them, the mainland had already disappeared beneath a curtain of silver mist.

The stranger waited at the shoreline.

He neither waved nor moved.

His dark kelp cloak fluttered despite the complete absence of wind. Around his neck hung a compass nearly identical to Elias's, except its bronze had turned almost black with age.

As the Starling reached the shore, the man smiled.

"So," he said softly, "you really do have his eyes."

Elias stepped off the ship, one hand instinctively resting on the hilt of the strange sword the Shipkeeper had given him.

"You knew my father."

"I did."

"Where is he?"

The man's smile faded.

"I was hoping you could answer that."

For several moments, neither of them spoke.

The waves gently touched the sand before retreating, leaving no footprints behind.

Finally Elias asked, "Who are you?"

The stranger removed his hood.

His hair was streaked with white, though his face suggested a man no older than forty. A long scar crossed one eyebrow, disappearing into his beard.

"My name is Rowan Vale."

He extended a hand.

"I was First Navigator aboard your father's expedition."

Elias didn't take it.

"My father never mentioned you."

"He couldn't."

"Why not?"

"Because history decided I drowned."

The Shipkeeper remained on the deck, watching silently.

She whispered to herself,

"He's alive after all..."

Rowan looked toward her and laughed.

"You don't look surprised."

"I've stopped being surprised by impossible things."

"Wise."

Elias crossed his arms.

"If you sailed with my father, tell me what happened."

Rowan looked toward the forest behind him.

"I will."

"But not here."

"The island listens."

Elias frowned.

"The island... listens?"

Rowan simply nodded.

"Everything on this island has ears."

They began walking.

The forest was unlike anything Elias had imagined.

The trees were translucent blue, their trunks filled with flowing water instead of sap. Schools of tiny glowing fish swam inside the branches as though wood and water obeyed different rules here.

Leaves occasionally detached from the canopy.

Instead of falling, they floated upward into the sky.

Elias caught one.

It dissolved into a droplet that whispered a sentence before disappearing.

"Don't trust the first answer."

He looked around.

"Did you hear that?"

Rowan didn't even turn.

"The trees remember conversations."

"They repeat them."

"Sometimes centuries later."

After nearly an hour, they reached a clearing.

At its center stood a stone table covered with moss.

Three cups already rested upon it.

Steam rose from each.

Elias glanced around.

"Who's here?"

"No one."

"Then who made the tea?"

"The island."

"...You're joking."

"I wish I were."

They sat.

Elias cautiously lifted the cup.

The tea smelled strangely familiar.

"Cinnamon..."

"My mother used to make this."

Rowan nodded.

"The island serves memories."

"Drink."

Against his better judgment, Elias took a sip.

Instantly, he was seven years old again.

He saw himself sitting beside a fireplace while his father unfolded a faded map.

Young Elias had asked,

"What's beyond the Last Ocean?"

His father smiled without looking up.

"The wrong question isn't 'what.' It's 'who.'"

The memory faded.

The tea was gone.

So was the cup.

Elias stared at the empty table.

"What... was that?"

"The island lets you revisit truths you've forgotten."

Rowan leaned back.

"You asked what happened to your father."

"Yes."

"He found the First Throne."

Elias's heartbeat quickened.

"And?"

"He refused to sit on it."

"...Why?"

"Because the throne asked for something he couldn't give."

"What did it ask?"

Rowan looked away.

"I don't know."

"He never told me."

"He simply stood."

"Then he handed me my compass..."

Rowan lifted the blackened compass around his neck.

"...and said, 'If I don't come back, make sure my son never follows me.'"

Silence settled between them.

Elias let out a bitter laugh.

"Well..."

"That didn't work."

"No."

"It didn't."

A deep rumble echoed through the clearing.

The ground vibrated beneath their feet.

The blue trees bent toward the mountain at the island's center.

Birds—if they could even be called birds—burst into the sky, their wings shimmering like liquid glass.

Rowan immediately stood.

"We're out of time."

"What happened?"

"The throne knows you're here."

"It has... awakened."

Elias looked toward the mountain.

For the first time, he noticed something enormous near its summit.

The coral throne wasn't empty anymore.

A figure now sat upon it.

Completely still.

Too far away to make out any features.

Yet Elias felt the stranger's gaze lock onto him.

A voice drifted across the entire island, calm and impossibly clear.

"Captain Elias Marris."

The voice knew his name.

"You have come seeking answers."

A pause.

"Instead..."

The figure slowly rose from the throne.

"...you will be asked a question."

The entire forest fell silent.

Even the sea stopped moving.

The silence was unbearable.

Even the air seemed unwilling to move.

Elias looked toward the mountain where the figure stood upon the coral throne. It was too distant to make out a face, yet he had the unsettling feeling that every detail of him—from the scar on his hand to the thoughts in his mind—was already known.

Rowan placed a hand on Elias's shoulder.

"Whatever happens," he said quietly, "answer honestly."

"You make it sound like we're walking into a trial."

"We are."

The climb was steeper than it had appeared from the shore.

Coral steps spiraled around the mountain, worn smooth by countless feet that had climbed them long before maps or kingdoms existed.

Neither Elias nor Rowan spoke.

Occasionally, they would pass stone statues carved into the cliffside.

Each depicted a different person seated upon a throne.

Kings.

Queens.

Children.

An old woman.

A sailor.

A blind man.

None wore crowns.

Beneath every statue was a single inscription.

"Chosen."

After hours of climbing, they reached a circular plateau.

The throne stood at its center.

It was immense, carved from living coral that pulsed faintly like a heartbeat. Streams of water flowed through the armrests before disappearing into the stone without spilling a drop.

The figure waiting beside it turned slowly.

He was neither old nor young.

His face seemed to change each time Elias blinked.

For one moment he resembled a fisherman.

The next, a queen.

Then an elderly monk.

Then a little girl.

Finally, his features settled into those of an ordinary man dressed in robes woven from shimmering seafoam.

"You climbed faster than your father."

His voice was calm.

Gentle.

As though greeting an old friend.

Elias stepped forward.

"Who are you?"

The man smiled.

"I've forgotten."

"...Forgotten your own name?"

"I surrendered it."

"When?"

"When I accepted the First Throne."

Rowan bowed his head.

"My Lord."

The man sighed.

"I haven't been anyone's lord for centuries."

He looked at Rowan.

"You've grown older."

"I had no choice."

"No."

"You didn't."

The stranger's attention returned to Elias.

"You seek your father."

"Yes."

"You seek the truth."

"Yes."

"You seek the Compass's purpose."

"Yes."

The man nodded.

"All reasonable desires."

He slowly descended the steps of the throne until he stood only a few feet away.

"But before answers..."

"...comes the question."

Without warning, the entire mountain disappeared.

Or perhaps Elias had disappeared from it.

One moment he stood beneath an open sky.

The next he was standing in the middle of the village where he had grown up.

Children laughed in the streets.

Fishing boats lined the harbor.

His mother stood outside their cottage, hanging clothes to dry.

His father waved from the lighthouse.

Everything was exactly as it had been twelve years ago.

"Elias?"

His mother's voice caught him completely off guard.

She smiled warmly.

"Breakfast is getting cold."

His heart pounded.

"No..."

He whispered.

"This isn't real."

She laughed.

"Still dreaming?"

She walked toward him and gently placed a hand against his cheek.

It felt real.

Warm.

Exactly as he remembered.

Tears filled his eyes.

A voice echoed through the illusion.

Not his mother's.

The Throne Keeper's.

"If you could remain here forever..."

"...would you?"

His father walked over carrying two fishing rods.

"Come on, Eli."

"The tide is perfect."

His younger self ran laughing across the beach.

The life he had lost unfolded before him.

No disappearances.

No dying ocean.

No cursed compass.

Just home.

The voice asked again.

"If returning to this life meant abandoning the real world..."

"...would you choose it?"

Elias closed his eyes.

His hands trembled.

Every part of him wanted to say yes.

He wanted one more meal with his parents.

One more sunset.

One ordinary day.

But...

He remembered the empty villages.

The drowned sailors.

The eye beneath the sea.

The Last Ocean.

Slowly, he looked at the smiling faces before him.

"I'm sorry."

His mother tilted her head.

"For what?"

He stepped backward.

"Because you're memories."

"And memories aren't meant to become prisons."

He whispered,

"I choose the real world."

The village shattered.

The sky cracked like glass.

The sea dissolved into light.

Everything vanished.

Elias found himself standing once more before the coral throne.

The Keeper smiled.

"So did your father."

Rowan looked stunned.

"He passed?"

The Keeper nodded.

"He answered correctly."

Elias frowned.

"Then why didn't he return?"

The Keeper's smile slowly disappeared.

"Because..."

"...he answered the second question differently."

"The second question?"

The Keeper turned toward the throne.

"There is always another question."

The living coral began to glow.

The water flowing through it rose into the air, twisting into the shape of a crown.

The Keeper looked directly into Elias's eyes.

"This time..."

"...the Ocean wishes to know..."

"What are you willing to lose to save a world that may never remember your name?"

Before Elias could answer, a deafening scream echoed across the island.

Not human.

Not animal.

The sky darkened in an instant.

Rowan's face turned white.

"No..."

"It found us."

Far beyond the cliffs, something colossal emerged from the sea.

Its back was lined with mountains.

Its eyes burned crimson instead of blue.

Unlike the ancient guardian beneath the harbor, this creature carried chains wrapped around its body.

And riding upon its head was a lone armored figure holding a black trident.

The rider raised the weapon toward the island.

The Keeper whispered a name that had not been spoken in a thousand years.

"The Tide King has returned."

The sea exploded.

Not with waves.

With silence.

Every crashing current, every whisper of foam against the cliffs, every cry of the strange birds circling above the island ceased in the same instant.

Only the enormous chained creature continued moving toward the shore.

Each step it took beneath the water sent ripples across the entire ocean.

Elias gripped the hilt of his sword.

"That's the Tide King?"

The Keeper shook his head.

"No."

He pointed toward the armored rider.

"That is the Tide King."

His voice grew quieter.

"The creature beneath him is only his throne."

The rider reached the beach.

He climbed down from the creature's head with impossible calm.

His armor was black coral veined with crimson light. Barnacles clung to his shoulders like medals earned in forgotten wars.

A long cloak of seaweed drifted behind him, though there was no wind.

Most unsettling was the helmet.

Its visor was smooth.

No eye slits.

No opening to breathe.

Yet Elias could feel someone watching him from within.

The Tide King climbed the coral steps without hurry.

Each step caused the living coral to darken.

Flowers withered.

The streams flowing through the mountain turned from blue to black.

The island itself seemed to recoil.

When he reached the summit, he stopped ten paces from the Keeper.

Neither spoke.

For a long moment, they simply stared at one another.

Finally, the Tide King broke the silence.

"You still guard an empty throne."

The Keeper smiled faintly.

"And you still wear stolen armor."

"You've become sentimental."

"You've become impatient."

Elias stepped forward.

"Who are you?"

The Tide King slowly turned his helmet toward him.

"So..."

"This is Arlen's son."

His voice was deep, but strangely familiar.

As though Elias had heard it once before in a dream he could no longer remember.

"You've grown."

Elias frowned.

"We've met?"

"Not yet."

The answer made no sense.

The Keeper sighed.

"You always enjoyed speaking in riddles."

"They're only riddles to those who arrive late."

The Tide King rested one hand upon the black trident.

"I've not come to fight."

"No?"

"No."

"I've come to recruit."

Elias almost laughed.

"Recruit me?"

The Tide King nodded.

"You carry the Drowned Compass."

"Yes."

"You command the Starling."

"Yes."

"You seek your father."

"...Yes."

"Then our goals are identical."

Rowan stepped between them.

"Don't listen to him."

The Tide King looked almost disappointed.

"You survived all these years, Rowan."

"I was hoping wisdom would eventually catch up with you."

"It did."

"That's why I oppose you."

The armored figure chuckled softly.

"Tell me, Elias..."

He slowly removed one glove.

His hand was covered in scars.

Across his wrist was the exact same blue mark Corvin had once shown.

A circle crossed by three waves.

"The mark of a Starling captain..."

Elias whispered.

"You..."

"You commanded the Starling?"

"I was its twentieth captain."

The Tide King looked toward the ship anchored below.

"It carried me long before it carried you."

The Keeper's expression hardened.

"Don't rewrite history."

"I don't."

"I simply remember the parts you hide."

He looked back at Elias.

"Has he told you why the Thrones were built?"

Elias remained silent.

"No."

"Of course not."

The Tide King spread his arms.

"The Thrones were never meant to rule the Ocean."

"They were prisons."

The words struck the plateau like lightning.

"The Ocean you wish to save..."

"...was once the greatest destroyer this world had ever known."

The Keeper took a step forward.

"Enough."

"Is it false?"

Silence.

"Answer him."

The Keeper closed his eyes.

"...No."

Elias stared at him.

"It's true?"

The Keeper finally nodded.

"Long before humanity existed..."

"...the Ocean awakened."

"It consumed continents."

"It erased civilizations."

"It drowned entire skies."

"So the First Navigators created the Three Thrones."

"They divided its heart."

"They imprisoned its will."

"And the world survived."

Elias's thoughts raced.

"If that's true..."

"...then why restore the Thrones?"

The Tide King answered before anyone else could.

"Because the prison is breaking."

"The heart is trying to become whole again."

"And when it does..."

"...it won't distinguish between innocent and guilty."

The Keeper spoke quietly.

"He's leaving out one detail."

The Tide King laughed.

"I wondered how long it would take."

The Keeper looked directly at Elias.

"If all three Thrones are restored..."

"...the Ocean will awaken exactly as it once was."

Elias felt his stomach tighten.

"You mean..."

"The world will flood."

"Yes."

The Keeper lowered his head.

"But if the Thrones remain broken..."

"...the Last Ocean will continue dying."

"And eventually..."

"...every river..."

"...every lake..."

"...every sea..."

"...will vanish."

There it was.

The impossible choice.

Restore the Ocean...

and risk the destruction of the world.

Or let it die...

and doom the world slowly instead.

The Tide King spread his arms.

"Now you understand."

"There are no heroes."

"Only people choosing which tragedy they can live with."

Before Elias could respond, the Drowned Compass suddenly began spinning so violently that it slipped from his hand.

It landed between the Keeper and the Tide King.

For the first time since Elias had found it...

The compass split into two equal halves.

One half floated toward the Keeper.

The other drifted toward the Tide King.

Both pieces glowed.

Then a third light erupted from within the broken compass.

A voice echoed across the mountain.

Older than the sea.

Older than time.

"Neither of you carries the whole truth."

The mountain shook.

The coral throne cracked down its center.

And from the darkness beneath it, a hidden staircase slowly rose into the light.

At its entrance was carved a single inscription:

"Only the Twenty-Seventh Captain may descend alone."

The crack in the throne widened.

Not violently.

Patiently.

Like a door that had waited centuries for the right hand to open it.

Stone shifted against stone until a narrow staircase spiraled into darkness beneath the mountain.

Cold air drifted upward.

It carried the scent of salt...

...and old parchment.

Elias looked at the Keeper.

"You knew this was here."

The Keeper didn't answer immediately.

Finally, he said, "I knew there was something beneath the throne."

"You've never entered?"

"I couldn't."

"Why?"

"Because the throne never chose me."

The Tide King rested both hands on the end of his black trident.

"It won't let either of us enter."

His voice was calm, almost amused.

"The inscription is quite clear."

He looked at Elias.

"Only the Twenty-Seventh Captain."

Rowan stepped beside Elias.

"Then I'm coming with you."

Before he could take another step, an invisible force threw him backward.

He landed hard against the coral wall.

The Keeper tried next.

The same thing happened.

Even the Tide King smiled to himself.

"It appears the old magic still remembers its rules."

Elias stood at the edge of the staircase.

"What if it's a trap?"

The Keeper gave a tired smile.

"It almost certainly is."

"Comforting."

"You asked for the truth."

"The truth rarely comforts."

Elias let out a slow breath.

Then, without another word, he began to descend.

The first hundred steps were carved from white coral.

The next hundred from black stone.

After that...

There were no steps at all.

Only darkness.

Yet somehow, each time Elias placed his foot forward, another step appeared beneath him.

The staircase was building itself as he walked.

He looked back.

The entrance had vanished.

There was only endless black behind him.

"No turning back."

He muttered to himself.

The darkness answered.

"There never was."

Elias spun around.

No one.

Only his own voice echoing back to him.

Or...

Something pretending to be it.

Eventually, the staircase ended.

Before him stood a massive circular chamber.

Unlike the grandeur of the throne above, this room was simple.

A wooden desk.

A single chair.

Shelves overflowing with journals.

Thousands of them.

Each carefully labeled.

Captain I

Captain II

Captain III

The shelves continued until—

Captain XXVI

Elias's heartbeat quickened.

There was one empty shelf.

A brass plaque beneath it read:

Captain XXVII

Waiting.

On the desk lay an old oil lamp.

The moment Elias approached, it lit itself.

Its warm light spread across the room.

He noticed something strange.

There was no dust.

Someone had been here recently.

Very recently.

A chair scraped across the stone floor behind him.

He turned.

A man sat calmly in the wooden chair.

He hadn't been there a moment ago.

He looked to be in his early fifties.

Sun-browned skin.

Grey at his temples.

A familiar scar above his left eyebrow.

His eyes...

Elias knew those eyes.

He had seen them every morning in his own reflection.

The man smiled.

"You've gotten taller."

Elias couldn't breathe.

"...Dad?"

Arlen Marris chuckled softly.

"I was wondering how long it would take you."

Elias rushed forward.

He stopped only inches away.

"You..."

His voice cracked.

"I thought you were dead."

"I know."

"Were you trapped here?"

"Yes."

"Can we leave?"

Arlen's smile faded.

"No."

Silence filled the chamber.

Twelve years of questions crowded Elias's mind, yet none of them would come out.

Finally, only one escaped.

"Why?"

Arlen stood and walked to one of the shelves.

He rested a hand on the journal marked Captain XXVI.

"Because I made the same mistake every captain before me made."

"You sat on the throne?"

"No."

"I believed the throne was the destination."

He looked directly at Elias.

"It never was."

Arlen picked up a journal from the shelf.

"Every captain comes here."

"They're shown the same choice."

"They believe they're deciding the future."

He placed the journal back.

"They're wrong."

"What do you mean?"

Arlen sighed.

"The Thrones don't test your strength."

"They test your understanding."

Elias frowned.

"Then tell me."

"Tell me everything."

"I can't."

"Why not?"

"Because if I simply give you the answers..."

"...you'll fail."

Elias slammed his fist onto the desk.

"I've spent twelve years looking for you!"

"I know."

"I crossed an impossible ocean!"

"I know."

"I deserve the truth!"

Arlen looked at him with deep sadness.

"No."

"You deserve the chance to discover it."

Frustration burned inside Elias.

"I don't need riddles!"

"I need my father!"

For the first time, Arlen looked genuinely hurt.

"I've wanted nothing more than to be your father again."

He stepped closer.

"But I stopped being only your father the day I became Captain."

He gently placed a hand on Elias's shoulder.

"And today..."

"...you stopped being only my son."

The room trembled.

A bell rang somewhere deep beneath the chamber.

Arlen closed his eyes.

"It's beginning."

"What is?"

"They've arrived."

"Who?"

Instead of answering, Arlen walked toward the far wall.

He placed his hand against solid stone.

The wall dissolved like mist.

Beyond it stretched another chamber.

Much larger.

Its ceiling vanished into darkness.

At its center floated something impossible.

Not a throne.

Not a crown.

A vast sphere of water suspended in the air, slowly rotating.

Inside the sphere, continents drifted like reflections.

Clouds formed.

Storms moved.

Ships sailed.

Cities glittered.

It was the entire world.

A living map.

Arlen spoke almost in a whisper.

"This..."

"...is the Heart Atlas."

"It doesn't show the world as it is."

"It shows the world as it could become."

Elias stepped closer, mesmerized.

Then he noticed something horrifying.

Across the oceans of the tiny world, black cracks were spreading.

The seas were shrinking.

Rivers vanished before his eyes.

Entire coastlines turned to dust.

"Is this..."

"The future?"

Arlen nodded.

"One of them."

Before Elias could ask another question, a voice echoed through the chamber.

Not the Keeper.

Not the Tide King.

Not even the ancient Ocean.

A woman's voice.

Clear.

Gentle.

Filled with authority.

"Captain Twenty-Seven."

The sphere stopped rotating.

One small island glowed with brilliant blue light.

The voice continued.

"The Second Throne has fallen."

Arlen's face drained of color.

"No..."

The chamber shook violently.

The oceans inside the Heart Atlas surged all at once.

Then, for the first time in centuries...

The world map began to bleed.

The blue light inside the Heart Atlas spread like ink through water.

Every ocean on the miniature world trembled.

Rivers twisted out of their courses.

Entire coastlines shifted.

Then a deep crack echoed through the chamber.

It did not come from the Atlas.

It came from the world outside.

Elias looked at Arlen.

"What just happened?"

His father didn't answer immediately.

Instead, he stared at the glowing island within the Atlas as if hoping the vision would change.

It didn't.

Finally, he whispered,

"Someone reached the Second Throne before us."

Elias felt his chest tighten.

"The Tide King?"

"I don't know."

"The Keeper?"

"I don't know."

"Then who?"

Arlen looked at him.

"That's what frightens me."

The chamber shook again.

Dust rained from the ceiling.

The suspended sphere rippled violently, and a new image appeared on its surface.

An island.

Not the Isle of Echoes.

A frozen land beneath a sky filled with green auroras.

At its center stood another throne.

This one carved from black ice.

Half of it had collapsed.

The water flowing through it had frozen solid.

Beside the ruined throne lay dozens of bodies.

Some wore ancient armor.

Others wore clothing from different centuries.

All had died reaching the same place.

Only one figure remained standing.

Their face was hidden beneath a white cloak.

In one hand they carried a shattered crown.

In the other...

...a compass.

Elias leaned closer.

"Can you see who it is?"

Before Arlen could answer, the image dissolved into waves.

The Atlas became still again.

"They don't want us to know."

"They?"

Arlen smiled sadly.

"You'll eventually stop asking that question."

A loud horn echoed from somewhere above.

The sound traveled through the mountain itself.

Arlen's expression changed instantly.

"The Keeper."

"He's calling us?"

"No."

"He's warning us."

"About what?"

Arlen turned toward the entrance.

"We're too late."

Father and son climbed the staircase together.

Unlike before, the path no longer felt endless.

It was as though the mountain itself wanted them to leave.

As they approached the surface, voices became clearer.

Not arguing.

Fighting.

Steel clashed against steel.

Stone shattered.

Someone screamed.

They emerged onto the plateau.

Chaos.

The coral throne had split completely in half.

The Keeper knelt near its base, blood staining his pale robes.

His staff lay broken beside him.

Across the shattered platform stood the Tide King.

His black trident was planted in the stone.

But he wasn't attacking.

He was defending.

Around him lay three cloaked figures dressed in white.

A fourth faced him.

Unlike the others, this one's mask was smooth porcelain, marked only by a single blue tear painted beneath one eye.

The masked figure held a slender spear that shimmered like glass.

Rowan was unconscious nearby.

The Shipkeeper knelt beside him, desperately trying to stop the bleeding from a wound across his shoulder.

The masked stranger noticed Elias.

"So..."

their voice echoed strangely, as though many people were speaking together.

"The final captain arrives."

The Tide King didn't look away from his opponent.

"Elias."

"Stay back."

"They aren't here for me."

"They're here for you."

The Keeper struggled to his feet.

His breathing was ragged.

"They call themselves..."

He coughed.

"...the Silent Current."

The masked figure inclined their head politely.

"A crude translation."

"But acceptable."

Elias stepped forward.

"I've never heard of you."

"No."

"You weren't supposed to."

The stranger lowered their spear.

"We existed before the First Throne."

"We existed before the First Compass."

"We existed before the Ocean was divided."

Arlen froze.

His face lost all color.

"It can't be..."

The masked figure looked toward him.

"Captain Twenty-Six."

"We wondered if you still lived."

Elias stared at his father.

"You know them?"

Arlen nodded slowly.

"I met them."

"Once."

"I thought they were a myth."

The masked figure smiled beneath the porcelain mask.

"You all keep making the same mistake."

"You believe there are only two sides."

They gestured toward the Keeper.

"He would restore the Thrones."

Then toward the Tide King.

"He would destroy them."

Finally, they looked directly at Elias.

"But neither understands the true purpose of the Compass."

The Drowned Compass suddenly flew from Elias's pocket.

It hovered in the air.

The two broken halves rotated around each other.

A third fragment, invisible until now, slowly emerged from empty space and joined them.

The Compass became whole again.

Only...

...it was no longer bronze.

It had become transparent, as though carved from water itself.

Every person on the plateau fell silent.

Even the Tide King lowered his weapon.

The masked figure whispered,

"At last."

"The Ocean has remembered."

The transparent Compass projected a map into the sky.

Not of seas.

Not of kingdoms.

Of stars.

Entire constellations stretched across the heavens, connected by glowing lines that converged on a single point beyond the known world.

The Shipkeeper gasped.

"No..."

The Keeper closed his eyes.

"So that's why..."

The Tide King actually laughed.

"I should have realized."

Elias looked from one face to another.

"What?"

"What does it mean?"

Arlen answered in a voice barely above a whisper.

"The Last Ocean..."

He looked up at the impossible map.

"...was never the end of the world."

"It was..."

"...the beginning of the journey."

Before anyone could speak again, the transparent Compass emitted a beam of blue light toward the eastern horizon.

Far beyond the sea, where no land had ever been charted, the clouds slowly parted.

Something enormous emerged.

Not an island.

Not a continent.

A city.

A city floating upon the ocean, its towers built from crystal and flowing water, illuminated by a thousand pale-blue lights.

The masked leader of the Silent Current dropped to one knee.

The Keeper bowed his head.

Even the Tide King stood in stunned silence.

Arlen looked at Elias with equal parts fear and wonder.

Then he spoke the name that had been erased from history.

"Welcome... to Asterion, the First City."
