The tin roof plays a symphony in raindrops,
a percussion built from every falling drop,
it doesn't need a conductor's careful baton,
it plays until the passing storm clouds stop.

I've fallen asleep beneath its metal drumming,
a lullaby of pattering and beat,
the tin roof symphony keeps softly coming,
each note a small, wet echo at my feet.

They say a symphony needs strings and horns,
a hall of velvet seats and formal dress,
but tin roof music, born of storms and thorns,
plays just as well in rougher, honest dress.

So let the tin roof keep its rain-soaked playing,
I'll listen from the warmth of somewhere dry,
some symphonies are only made for staying
just long enough to hum us a lullaby.
