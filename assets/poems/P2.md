The gears of dawn begin to grind,
Beneath the crust of solar light,
Where clockwork trees are silver-lined,
To catch the pulsing heart of night.
The copper apples hum a tune,
A steady tick, a rhythmic hum,
Beneath the hollow, painted moon,
Where golden gears begin to come.

The sap is oil, the bark is steel,
It shivers in the morning breeze,
A giant, rotating, brassy wheel,
Among the silent, metal trees.
The leaves are copper, thin and bright,
They clatter when the north winds blow,
Reflecting back the dying light,
In rhythmic, heavy, rhythmic flow.

No nectar drips, no pollen flies,
There is no scent of blooming spring,
Just mechanical under the skies,
Where wire birds are taught to sing.
The orchard waits for winding keys,
That turn within the iron loam,
To wake the cold and rigid trees,
And bring the ticking summer home.

But in the center, deep and dark,
A single apple dares to rust,
To lose the gleam, to lose the spark,
And crumble into orange dust.
It is the price of constant time,
The slow decay of brass and lead,
A silent end, a closing rhyme,
Where iron fruit falls to the bed.
