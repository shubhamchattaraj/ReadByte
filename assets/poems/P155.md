A requiem in copper, worn and dulling,
plays soft for wires past their usefulness,
the current stopped, the metal slowly culling
its shine for something closer to distress.

I've kept an old wire coiled beside the window,
its copper requiem hums quiet, low,
it doesn't need a current now to lend to
the story of the charge it used to know.

They say a requiem needs grand orchestras,
a hall of mourners dressed in solemn black,
but copper only needs the dust that gathers,
to sing the softest song of giving back.

So let the copper requiem keep playing,
I'll listen to the metal's fading hum,
some songs are only made for slow decaying,
of currents that remember where they're from.
