The copperline hums vespers in the evening,
a wire strung between the dusk and dark,
it carries voices, patient, never leaving,
the current of a slow, electric spark.

I've listened to its low and steady praying,
the vesper hymn of signal, hum, and heat,
the copperline don't need a choir swaying,
it sings alone, and still the song's complete.

They say a prayer needs incense, kneeling, silence,
a sacred hour marked by bell or chime,
but copperline vespers, in their quiet violence
of current, pray in their own kind of time.

So let the copper hum its evening vespers,
I'll listen as the wires start to glow,
some prayers are only sung by rust and whispers,
of power moving where it needs to go.
