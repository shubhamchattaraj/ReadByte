The rain comes down the color of old pennies,
tapping rhythms on the tin roof above,
it pools in gutters, spills through cracks and seams,
and rusts the edges of the things I love.

I stood beneath the awning watching it fall,
each drop a coin the sky no longer needs,
it wrote its story on the garden wall,
and fed the hungry mouths of thirsty weeds.

They say the rain that's copper brings you luck,
a metal blessing falling from the sky,
I held my hands out, let it fill and pluck
the tension from my shoulders passing by.

So when the copper rain begins to pour,
I won't run in, I'll stand and let it stay,
and catch each coin that lands upon the floor
of evening, spending it like borrowed day.
